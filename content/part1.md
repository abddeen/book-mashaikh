# PART ONE: THE SEEDS OF TASAWWUF — 1st–2nd Century AH (622–815 CE)

## Historical Context

The first two centuries of Islam were a period of extraordinary transformation. Within a single generation of the Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ passing in 11 AH (632 CE), the Muslim community expanded from the Arabian Peninsula to encompass a vast territory stretching from the Iberian Peninsula to the borders of China. The Rashidun Caliphate (11–40 AH / 632–661 CE) gave way to the Umayyad Dynasty (41–132 AH / 661–750 CE), which in turn was overthrown by the Abbasid revolution. This breathtaking political expansion brought immense wealth, cultural diversity, and worldly temptation into what had been an austere desert community.

The seeds of what would become tasawwuf (Islamic mysticism/spirituality) were sown in this very tension between worldly expansion and inner devotion. The tragedy of Karbala in 61 AH (680 CE), in which the Prophet's ﷺ grandson Husayn ibn Ali was martyred along with his companions, left an indelible wound on the Muslim conscience. The political fitnas (civil wars) — first between Ali and Mu'awiya, then the Umayyad suppression of dissent — disillusioned many pious Muslims who witnessed the caliphate transform from a spiritual stewardship into a worldly kingdom (mulk).

In response, a generation of devout men and women turned inward. They were not yet called "Sufis" — that term would come later — but rather zuhhad (ascetics) and 'ubbad (devout worshippers). They gathered in the mosques and homes of Basra, Kufa, and Medina, dedicating themselves to night prayers (tahajjud), fasting, weeping in remembrance of God, and renouncing the comforts that the new empire offered.

Basra, in particular, emerged as the cradle of this movement. It was a garrison city where veterans of the early conquests settled alongside scholars and merchants. The worldliness around them contrasted sharply with the simplicity they associated with the Prophet ﷺ and his Companions. Here, Hasan al-Basri would deliver sermons that moved audiences to tears, and Rabia al-Adawiyya would articulate a doctrine of divine love that transcended fear and hope.

This was not yet organized Sufism — there were no tariqas (orders), no formal silsilas (spiritual chains), no khanqahs (lodges). Rather, these were individual luminaries whose personal piety, profound insights, and spiritual practices laid the groundwork for everything that followed. Their emphasis on zuhd (renunciation), tawba (repentance), khawf (fear of God), and the purification of the nafs (ego-self) established the foundational vocabulary and concerns of the Sufi tradition.

The cities of the Muslim world — Basra, Kufa, Baghdad, Balkh, and Fustat — each produced their own spiritual heroes. The movement was organic, arising from the lived experience of Muslims grappling with the gap between the prophetic ideal and the realities of empire. It is to these early pioneers that all later Sufi chains trace their origins, and it is with them that our journey begins.

## Chapter 1: Uways al-Qarani (أويس القرني)

**Full Name:** Uways ibn Amir ibn Jaz' al-Qarani
**Born:** Yemen (exact date unknown)
**Died:** 37 AH / 657 CE, Battle of Siffin
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era (considered the spiritual ancestor of the Uwaysi tradition)
**Titles:** Khayr al-Tabi'in (The Best of the Tabi'in — according to the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ)
**Teachers:** Never met the Prophet ﷺ in person; considered spiritually connected to him
**Burial:** Disputed — attributed tombs in Syria and elsewhere

### Biography

Uways al-Qarani holds a unique and extraordinary station in Islamic spirituality. He is the only individual among the Tabi'in (the generation after the Companions) whom the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ personally praised by name without ever having met him. In an authentic hadith narrated by Muslim, the Prophet ﷺ told Umar ibn al-Khattab: *"There will come to you a man from Yemen called Uways. He has no one in Yemen except his mother. He had leprosy and prayed to Allah, and Allah cured him except for a spot the size of a dirham. If you meet him, ask him to pray for your forgiveness."*

Uways lived in Yemen caring for his elderly mother, and it was this filial duty that prevented him from traveling to Medina to meet the Prophet ﷺ during his lifetime. Yet his devotion to the Prophet ﷺ was so intense that, according to tradition, when he heard that the Prophet ﷺ had lost a tooth at the Battle of Uhud, Uways broke all of his own teeth in solidarity, not knowing which one it was.

After the Prophet's ﷺ passing, Umar ibn al-Khattab diligently sought Uways among the Yemeni delegations that came to Medina during the Hajj season. When he finally found him — a humble, unassuming man among the pilgrims — Umar asked him to pray for his forgiveness, as the Prophet ﷺ had instructed. The people around were astonished that the Commander of the Faithful would seek prayers from this unknown man.

Uways is believed to have fought and been martyred at the Battle of Siffin (37 AH) on the side of Ali ibn Abi Talib. He left behind no written works, no formal students, and no organized following. Yet his spiritual legacy is immense.

### Key Teachings

Uways represents the archetype of the hidden saint (wali) — the one whose spiritual rank is known to God and the Prophet ﷺ but concealed from the world. He embodies selfless devotion, filial piety, and the possibility of spiritual connection with a master without physical meeting.

### Famous Stories

**The Broken Teeth:** When news reached Yemen that the Prophet ﷺ had lost a tooth at Uhud, Uways was so overwhelmed with love and empathy that he broke his own teeth — first one, then, uncertain which tooth the Prophet ﷺ had lost, all of them. This story, whether literal or symbolic, became the supreme emblem of the lover's identification with the beloved in Sufi literature.

**Umar's Search:** For years, Umar ibn al-Khattab would ask each Yemeni delegation: "Is Uways al-Qarani among you?" When he finally found him — ragged, poor, and unremarkable in appearance — and asked him to make du'a (supplication), the encounter became a foundational parable: true spiritual rank has nothing to do with worldly status.

### Legacy

The "Uwaysi" tradition in Sufism — receiving spiritual guidance from a master without physical contact, sometimes from a deceased saint or even from the Prophet ﷺ himself in a vision — is named after Uways. Several later Sufis, including Bayazid Bistami and Abu al-Hasan Kharaqani, were described as having received their spiritual initiation in an "Uwaysi" manner.

## Chapter 2: Hasan al-Basri (الحسن البصري)

**Full Name:** Abu Sa'id al-Hasan ibn Abi al-Hasan Yasar al-Basri
**Born:** 21 AH / 642 CE, Medina
**Died:** 110 AH / 728 CE, Basra
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era; virtually all Sufi silsilas pass through him
**Titles:** Sayyid al-Tabi'in (Master of the Successors)
**Teachers:** Raised in the household of Umm Salama (wife of the Prophet ﷺ); learned from numerous Companions including Ali ibn Abi Talib, Anas ibn Malik, and others
**Notable Students:** Farqad al-Sabakhi, Habib al-Ajami, Abdul Wahid ibn Zayd, Malik ibn Dinar

### Biography

Hasan al-Basri is universally regarded as the fountainhead of Islamic spirituality. Born in Medina in the household of the Prophet's ﷺ wife Umm Salama — his mother Khayra was her maidservant — he grew up in the very environment that had been illuminated by prophetic presence. As a child, it is said that Umm Salama sometimes nursed him, and the Prophet's ﷺ spiritual blessing (baraka) was thus considered to have flowed into him directly.

He moved to Basra, where he became the city's most celebrated scholar, preacher, and spiritual authority. His sermons, delivered in the great mosque of Basra, were legendary for their emotional power. He would speak of death, the afterlife, and the fleeting nature of worldly life with such conviction that grown men would weep openly and faint. His gatherings drew the greatest scholars of the age.

Hasan al-Basri lived through the reigns of numerous caliphs and witnessed firsthand the transformation of the Muslim community from a pious brotherhood into a sprawling empire. He was openly critical of the Umayyad rulers, particularly al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf, the brutal governor of Iraq, yet he survived through the sheer weight of his scholarly authority and popular reverence.

### Key Teachings

Hasan al-Basri emphasized zuhd (renunciation of worldly attachment), muhasaba (self-accounting), and constant awareness of death. He taught that the world was a bridge — one should cross it but not build upon it. His teachings on sincerity (ikhlas) and the dangers of hypocrisy (riya') became foundational to later Sufi ethics.

### Famous Stories

**The Letter to Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz:** When the pious Umayyad caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz wrote to Hasan al-Basri asking for advice on governance, Hasan replied with a famous letter that became one of the earliest masterpieces of Islamic spiritual counsel: *"Beware of this world with all wariness, for it is like a snake — smooth to the touch but its venom is deadly. Turn away from whatever delights you in it, for the little you companion it... and be most wary of it when you are most pleased with it."*

**His Weeping:** It was said that Hasan al-Basri wept so frequently that his neighbors thought someone in his household had died. When asked why, he said: *"I weep because I fear that Allah will throw me on my face in the Fire."* Despite his immense knowledge and piety, his humility and fear of God were legendary.

**Al-Hajjaj's Challenge:** The tyrannical governor al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf once summoned Hasan to his court, intending to intimidate or punish him for his outspoken sermons. Hasan entered, and before al-Hajjaj could speak, he supplicated silently to God. Al-Hajjaj's demeanor completely changed — he rose from his seat, treated Hasan with utmost respect, asked for his counsel, and let him leave freely. When asked what prayer he had made, Hasan said he had asked God to be his protector.

### Major Works

While Hasan al-Basri did not leave behind formal written treatises in the manner of later scholars, his sermons, letters, and sayings were extensively recorded by his students and compiled by later scholars. His letter to Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz is among the earliest surviving texts of Islamic spiritual literature.

### Legacy

Almost every Sufi silsila (spiritual chain) passes through Hasan al-Basri, connecting later masters back through him to Ali ibn Abi Talib and the Prophet ﷺ. He is the trunk from which virtually all branches of tasawwuf grow. His emphasis on zuhd, taqwa (God-consciousness), and spiritual sincerity set the agenda for all subsequent Sufi development.

## Chapter 3: Rabia al-Adawiyya (رابعة العدوية)

**Full Name:** Rabia bint Isma'il al-Adawiyya al-Qaysiyya
**Born:** c. 95–100 AH / 713–717 CE, Basra
**Died:** c. 185 AH / 801 CE, Basra (some say Jerusalem)
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Titles:** The Mother of the Good (Umm al-Khayr), Rabia al-Basriyya
**Teachers:** Hasan al-Basri's circle (though chronological overlap is debated)
**Legacy:** The first to fully articulate the doctrine of selfless divine love (mahabbah)

### Biography

Rabia al-Adawiyya is the most celebrated female saint in Islamic history and one of the most important figures in the development of Sufi thought. Born into poverty in Basra — her name "Rabia" (the fourth) indicates she was the fourth daughter — she was orphaned young, kidnapped, and sold into slavery. According to Attar's Tadhkirat al-Awliya, her master one night witnessed her praying in the darkness, surrounded by a light that illuminated the entire house without any lamp. Realizing her spiritual rank, he freed her immediately.

After gaining her freedom, Rabia devoted herself entirely to the worship of God. She lived a life of extreme asceticism, refusing all proposals of marriage — including, tradition holds, from the great scholar Hasan al-Basri himself — declaring that she had no room in her heart for anything but God. She lived alone, possessing almost nothing, yet she became the spiritual axis around which Basra's spiritual life revolved.

Her revolutionary contribution was the introduction of selfless divine love (mahabbah) as the supreme station of the spiritual path. Before Rabia, the dominant spiritual motifs were fear (khawf) and hope (raja'). She transcended both, declaring that God should be worshipped neither out of fear of Hell nor desire for Paradise, but purely out of love for His own sake.

### Key Teachings

Rabia articulated that true worship is motivated solely by love of God, not by fear of punishment or hope for reward. She also taught the concept of intimacy with God (uns) and the need to transcend all worldly and otherworldly attachments.

### Famous Stories

**The Torch and the Bucket:** Rabia was once seen running through the streets of Basra carrying a torch in one hand and a bucket of water in the other. When asked what she was doing, she replied: *"I want to set fire to Paradise and pour water on Hell, so that these two veils disappear, and people worship God neither out of fear of Hell nor desire for Paradise, but solely for the sake of His eternal beauty."* This became perhaps the single most quoted story in all of Sufi literature.

**The Proposal of Hasan al-Basri:** When Hasan al-Basri (or, in some versions, another scholar) proposed marriage to her, Rabia replied: *"The contract of marriage is for those who have a phenomenal existence. As for me, I have ceased to exist and have passed out of self. My existence is in God and I am altogether His. The marriage contract must be asked of Him, not of me."*

**The Ka'ba Comes to Her:** It is narrated that when Rabia set out on the pilgrimage to Mecca, the Ka'ba came to meet her on the road. She said: *"What am I to do with the Ka'ba? It is the Lord of the Ka'ba that I seek. The Ka'ba cannot come to meet me — of what use is it?"*

### Legacy

Rabia's doctrine of divine love (mahabbah) became the beating heart of all subsequent Sufi thought. Without her contribution, the Sufi tradition would have remained primarily ascetic rather than ecstatic. She demonstrated that the spiritual path was not exclusive to men, and she is honored across all Sufi traditions as one of the greatest saints.

## Chapter 4: Ibrahim ibn Adham (إبراهيم بن أدهم)

**Full Name:** Abu Ishaq Ibrahim ibn Adham ibn Mansur al-Ijli al-Balkhi
**Born:** c. 100 AH / 718 CE, Balkh (present-day Afghanistan)
**Died:** c. 165 AH / 782 CE, near Byzantium (during a military expedition; buried on the Syrian coast, possibly Jableh)
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Titles:** Sultan al-Zahidin (Sultan of the Ascetics)
**Teachers:** Various scholars in Khorasan, Mecca, and Syria; also attributed with learning from Khidr
**Notable Students:** Shaqiq al-Balkhi, Hudhayfa al-Mar'ashi

### Biography

Ibrahim ibn Adham is the prince who renounced his throne for God — a story that has been compared to that of Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha), though it stands firmly within the Islamic tradition. He was born into royalty in Balkh, one of the great cities of Khorasan (in present-day northern Afghanistan), where his father was a king or wealthy nobleman.

After his awakening, Ibrahim left Balkh and wandered for years through the lands of Islam — Khorasan, Mecca, Syria — living on the labor of his own hands (he worked as a harvester and watchman), eating only what he earned through lawful toil, and dedicating his nights to worship. He became the embodiment of tawba (repentance) and zuhd (renunciation), demonstrating that the highest spiritual station required complete detachment from worldly comforts.

Ibrahim settled in Syria, where he spent his later years in worship and occasional military service on the Byzantine frontier. He was known for his insistence on halal (lawful) sustenance, often going hungry rather than eating food of doubtful origin.

### Key Teachings

Ibrahim ibn Adham taught that the path to God begins with tawba (repentance), passes through zuhd (renunciation), and culminates in complete tawakkul (reliance on God). He was especially emphatic about the importance of earning one's sustenance through lawful labor.

### Famous Stories

**The Awakening on the Throne:** As narrated by Attar, Ibrahim was once hunting on horseback when he heard a voice say: *"O Ibrahim! Is it for this that you were created? Is this what you were commanded to do?"* Shaken, he continued, and the voice came again, this time from the saddle of his horse. He dismounted, encountered a shepherd, exchanged his royal garments for the shepherd's wool cloak, and walked away from his kingdom forever. In another version, a mysterious stranger appeared in his palace at night. When Ibrahim asked what he was doing, the stranger said he was "looking for his camel." Ibrahim said, "You fool, you seek a camel in a palace?" The stranger replied, "And you seek God on a throne?"

**The Unanswered Prayers:** A man once asked Ibrahim: "I pray to God but He does not answer my prayers." Ibrahim replied: *"You know God yet you do not obey Him. You recite the Quran yet you do not act upon it. You know Satan yet you do not oppose him. You claim to love the Prophet ﷺ yet you abandon his Sunnah. You claim to love Paradise yet you do not work for it. You claim to fear the Fire yet you do not stop sinning. You say 'death is certain' yet you do not prepare for it. You busy yourself with the faults of others yet you ignore your own. You eat from God's provision yet you do not thank Him. And you bury your dead yet you take no lesson from it."*

### Legacy

Ibrahim ibn Adham became the supreme model of renunciation in Islamic literature. His story of abandoning worldly power for divine love has been retold countless times and symbolizes the Sufi conviction that the greatest kingdom is the kingdom of the heart.

## Chapter 5: Shaqiq al-Balkhi (شقيق البلخي)

**Full Name:** Abu Ali Shaqiq ibn Ibrahim al-Balkhi al-Azdi
**Born:** Balkh (present-day Afghanistan)
**Died:** 194 AH / 810 CE (martyred in battle against the Turks)
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Titles:** Pioneer of the doctrine of tawakkul (trust in God)
**Teachers:** Ibrahim ibn Adham
**Notable Students:** Hatim al-Asamm

### Biography

Shaqiq al-Balkhi was a prominent disciple of Ibrahim ibn Adham and one of the great early figures of Khorasani Sufism. Like his master, he hailed from Balkh, and he carried forward Ibrahim's emphasis on tawakkul (absolute trust in God) and zuhd (asceticism). He was a wealthy merchant before his spiritual transformation.

Shaqiq is particularly significant for his systematic articulation of tawakkul — reliance upon God — as a spiritual station. He taught that true tawakkul meant that even if the entire world were turned to gold and given to you, you should not be happier than if it were all taken away, because your trust is in God's decree, not in material possessions.

### Key Teachings

Shaqiq taught that the spiritual path progresses through four stages: zuhd (renunciation), khawf (fear), shawq (longing for God), and mahabbah (love). His systemization of spiritual states was an early step toward the later Sufi manuals.

### Famous Stories

**The Turkish Idol Worshippers:** Shaqiq once traveled to the land of the Turks and observed idol worshippers who, despite having no crops or provisions, trusted completely that their idols would provide. He was deeply moved: *"These people have such trust in idols made of stone. How much more should I, who worship the Living God, trust in Him?"* This experience cemented his doctrine of tawakkul.

**Advice to His Student:** When his student Hatim al-Asamm asked how long he had been his companion, Hatim replied, "Thirty-three years." Shaqiq asked, "What have you learned in this time?" Hatim listed eight spiritual lessons, each so profound that Shaqiq wept and said, "You have surpassed me."

### Legacy

Shaqiq al-Balkhi is remembered as a pioneer of Khorasani Sufism who bridged the generation between Ibrahim ibn Adham and later masters. His emphasis on tawakkul became central to Sufi ethics. He was martyred in battle, embodying the ideal of the warrior-saint.

## Chapter 6: Fudayl ibn Iyad (الفضيل بن عياض)

**Full Name:** Abu Ali Fudayl ibn Iyad ibn Mas'ud al-Tamimi
**Born:** c. 107 AH / 725 CE, Samarkand (or Khorasan)
**Died:** 187 AH / 803 CE, Mecca
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Titles:** The Penitent Highwayman
**Teachers:** Various scholars of hadith and fiqh; Abu Hanifa, Sufyan al-Thawri
**Notable Students:** Bishr al-Hafi, Sari al-Saqati, Ahmad ibn Hanbal (in hadith)

### Biography

Fudayl ibn Iyad is one of the most dramatic conversion stories in Islamic spiritual history — a highway robber who became one of the greatest saints and hadith scholars of his age. He operated on the road between Merv and Bavard in Khorasan, leading a band of robbers. Yet even in his days of banditry, there were signs of a latent nobility: he never robbed the destitute and always spared caravans that included women.

After his conversion, Fudayl moved to Mecca, where he spent the rest of his life in worship near the Ka'ba. He became renowned as a hadith scholar of the highest rank — Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal himself transmitted hadith from him — and as a spiritual master of uncompromising sincerity. His Meccan circle produced several of the next generation's greatest Sufis.

### Key Teachings

Fudayl emphasized ikhlas (sincerity) as the foundation of all spiritual practice. He defined sincerity in its most radical form: *"Abandoning action for the sake of people is showing off (riya'), and performing action for the sake of people is shirk. True sincerity is when God frees you from both."*

### Famous Stories

**The Conversion:** One night, Fudayl was climbing a wall to reach a woman he desired, when he heard a voice reciting the Quran: *"Has not the time come for the hearts of those who believe to be humbled by the remembrance of God?"* (57:16). He froze and said: *"Indeed, O my Lord, the time has come."* He descended, repented on the spot, and never returned to his former life.

**The Encounter with Harun al-Rashid:** The great Abbasid caliph Harun al-Rashid once visited Fudayl, seeking spiritual counsel. Fudayl reduced the caliph to tears by saying: *"O Commander of the Faithful, I fear for you. Consider — the Prophet ﷺ was responsible for a small community and it weighed heavily upon him. You are responsible for the entire umma. Who will answer for them all on the Day of Judgment?"* Harun wept until he nearly fainted, and his minister tried to intervene, but Fudayl said: *"You are the one who destroyed him — leading him into worldliness — and now you stop me from advising him?"*

### Legacy

Fudayl ibn Iyad's conversion story became one of the most beloved narratives in Islamic literature — proof that divine mercy can transform even the most hardened sinner into the most elevated saint. His influence flowed through his students into the mainstream of Baghdad Sufism.

## Chapter 7: Dawud al-Tai (داود الطائي)

**Full Name:** Abu Sulayman Dawud ibn Nusayr al-Tai al-Kufi
**Born:** Kufa
**Died:** 165 AH / 781 CE, Kufa
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Teachers:** Abu Hanifa (in fiqh), various scholars
**Notable Students:** Maruf al-Karkhi

### Biography

Dawud al-Tai was one of the most extreme ascetics of early Islam and a distinguished jurist who studied under the great Imam Abu Hanifa himself. He was considered Abu Hanifa's most brilliant student in fiqh (jurisprudence), but at a certain point he abandoned the outward pursuit of legal scholarship to devote himself entirely to worship and contemplation.

His asceticism was legendary. He lived on dry bread, often soaking it in water to save the time it took to chew, so that he could spend more time in prayer and dhikr. He sealed off the door of his house and entered through the roof. When Abu Hanifa heard of his former student's extreme renunciation, he remarked: *"Dawud has understood the purpose of knowledge."*

### Key Teachings

Dawud taught that knowledge ('ilm) is only valuable insofar as it leads to action ('amal), and action is only valuable insofar as it leads to God-consciousness (taqwa). He embodied the principle that the endpoint of scholarship is not more scholarship but spiritual transformation.

### Famous Stories

**Abu Hanifa's Tribute:** When Imam Abu Hanifa, the founder of the Hanafi school of law, was told that Dawud had abandoned legal studies for pure devotion, he said: *"He has chosen the better part."* This endorsement from the greatest jurist of the age lent enormous legitimacy to the ascetic path.

**The Sealed Door:** Dawud sealed the door of his house and would enter and exit through the roof, so that no visitor could disturb his worship. He minimized all human contact to preserve his spiritual focus.

### Legacy

Dawud al-Tai represents the transition from exoteric scholarship to esoteric practice. His teacher was the great jurist Abu Hanifa; his student was Maruf al-Karkhi, who became a central link in the chain leading to Junaid and the Baghdad school of Sufism. Through him, the legal tradition and the mystical tradition were bridged.

## Chapter 8: Sufyan al-Thawri (سفيان الثوري)

**Full Name:** Abu Abdullah Sufyan ibn Sa'id ibn Masruq al-Thawri
**Born:** 97 AH / 716 CE, Kufa
**Died:** 161 AH / 778 CE, Basra
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Titles:** Amir al-Mu'minin fi'l-Hadith (Commander of the Faithful in Hadith)
**Teachers:** Numerous hadith scholars
**Students:** Abdullah ibn al-Mubarak, Fudayl ibn Iyad, and countless hadith transmitters

### Biography

Sufyan al-Thawri was one of the greatest hadith scholars in Islamic history, honored with the title "Commander of the Faithful in Hadith" — a rank given to fewer than a handful of scholars across all centuries. Yet alongside his encyclopedic command of prophetic traditions, he was also a profound ascetic and spiritual figure who is counted among the early masters of zuhd.

He fled from the Abbasid caliphs who sought to appoint him as a judge (qadi), believing that accepting state positions would corrupt his religion. He spent years in hiding to avoid this appointment, preferring poverty and independence to worldly authority.

### Key Teachings

Sufyan taught that the greatest form of zuhd is not merely avoiding luxury but avoiding attachment to people's opinions. He said: *"Zuhd in this world is shortening your hopes. It is not eating rough food or wearing a wool cloak."*

### Famous Stories

**Fleeing from Office:** When the Abbasid caliph sought to appoint Sufyan as the chief judge, Sufyan fled and went into hiding. He preferred to live as a fugitive rather than compromise his integrity by serving political power. He is said to have remarked: *"A scholar who befriends a ruler is a thief."*

### Legacy

Sufyan al-Thawri bridged the worlds of hadith scholarship and spiritual asceticism, demonstrating that the greatest scholars were also the most detached from worldly power. His independence from political authority became a model for later Sufi masters.

## Chapter 9: Abdullah ibn al-Mubarak (عبد الله بن المبارك)

**Full Name:** Abu Abd al-Rahman Abdullah ibn al-Mubarak al-Hanzali al-Marwazi
**Born:** 118 AH / 736 CE, Merv (Khorasan)
**Died:** 181 AH / 797 CE, Hit (Iraq)
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Titles:** The Scholar-Warrior-Ascetic
**Teachers:** Sufyan al-Thawri, Abu Hanifa, Malik ibn Anas, and many others
**Major Works:** Kitab al-Zuhd (Book of Asceticism), Kitab al-Jihad

### Biography

Abdullah ibn al-Mubarak was one of the most remarkable figures of the second Islamic century — a man who combined in his person the roles of scholar, warrior, merchant, and ascetic. Born in Merv (present-day Turkmenistan) to a Turkish father, he became one of the most widely traveled scholars of his age, studying under nearly every major authority of his time.

He was fabulously wealthy from trade, yet spent his fortune on charity, supporting scholars and equipping military expeditions on the Byzantine frontier. He would alternate between years of hajj and years of ribat (frontier warfare), seeing both as forms of worship.

### Key Teachings

Ibn al-Mubarak's Kitab al-Zuhd is one of the earliest surviving compilations of ascetic traditions, gathering sayings of the Prophet ﷺ, the Companions, and the Tabi'in on the subject of renunciation and spiritual detachment. His life demonstrated that zuhd did not require withdrawal from the world but rather inner detachment while engaged in worldly affairs.

### Famous Stories

**The Companion of the Frontier and the Worshipper of Mecca:** Ibn al-Mubarak wrote a famous poem contrasting the soldier guarding the Muslim frontier — exposed to dust, sun, and danger — with the comfortable worshipper in Mecca: *"O worshipper of the Two Holy Mosques, if you could see us, you would know that your worship is mere play compared to ours. You perfume yourself with fragrance; we are perfumed with our blood."*

**His Generosity:** It was said that he never turned away anyone who asked for help. He would secretly pay off people's debts, fund scholars' travels, and provide for widows and orphans — all while maintaining an outward appearance of an ordinary merchant.

### Legacy

Abdullah ibn al-Mubarak represents the ideal of the complete Muslim — scholar, warrior, merchant, and ascetic combined. His Kitab al-Zuhd became a foundational text for the ascetic tradition, and his personal example inspired centuries of Muslims who sought to integrate worldly engagement with spiritual depth.
