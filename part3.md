# PART THREE: THE AGE OF SYSTEMATIZATION — 5th–6th Century AH (1010–1200 CE)

## Historical Context

The fifth and sixth Islamic centuries represent a pivotal transformation in the history of tasawwuf — the era in which Sufism evolved from a loosely organized movement of individual masters into a structured tradition of formal orders (tariqas), systematic treatises, and institutional infrastructure. This transformation occurred against the backdrop of immense political upheaval and intellectual ferment across the Muslim world.

The Seljuk Turks, who swept out of Central Asia and conquered Baghdad in 447 AH (1055 CE), became the new guardians of Sunni orthodoxy. Their vizier Nizam al-Mulk established the famous Nizamiyya madrasas — a chain of state-sponsored institutions of learning that stretched from Baghdad to Nishapur. It was at the Nizamiyya of Baghdad that Abu Hamid al-Ghazali held his celebrated professorship before his spiritual crisis led him to abandon it.

The Crusades erupted in 489 AH (1096 CE) with the fall of Jerusalem, sending shockwaves through the Muslim world. In Egypt, the Fatimid Ismaili caliphate offered an alternative model of Islamic civilization, while the Assassins (Nizari Ismailis) operated from their mountain fortresses. It was a world of competing ideologies, military threats, and existential challenges.

Into this turmoil, Abu Hamid al-Ghazali achieved what is arguably the single most important intellectual feat in Islamic history: the definitive reconciliation of Sufism with mainstream Sunni theology and jurisprudence. His Ihya Ulum al-Din (Revival of the Religious Sciences) demonstrated that the spiritual dimension of Islam was not a dangerous innovation but the very heart of prophetic practice. After al-Ghazali, no serious Sunni scholar could dismiss Sufism as mere heresy.

This was also the century in which the first great tariqas were founded. Abdul Qadir al-Jilani established what would become the Qadiriyya — eventually the most widespread Sufi order in the world. Ahmad al-Rifa'i founded the Rifa'iyya. The Suhrawardiyya took shape. Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili would establish the Shadhiliyya. These were no longer informal circles around individual masters but organized institutions with formal initiation (bay'a), chains of succession, distinctive practices, and endowed properties.

The khanqah (Sufi lodge) became a fixture of urban life across the Muslim world. These institutions served as centers of worship, education, hospitality, and social welfare. The Seljuk and Ayyubid rulers patronized them generously, recognizing the Sufis' power to inspire loyalty, maintain social cohesion, and legitimize political authority.

Meanwhile, Sufism was beginning its great expansion beyond the Arab-Persian heartlands. Al-Hujwiri (Data Ganj Bakhsh) settled in Lahore, becoming the first major Sufi presence in the Indian subcontinent. Ahmad Yasawi carried the Sufi message to the Turkic peoples of Central Asia. These pioneers laid the groundwork for the massive missionary expansion that would characterize the next era.

## Chapter 21: Abu Abd al-Rahman al-Sulami (أبو عبد الرحمن السلمي)

**Full Name:** Abu Abd al-Rahman Muhammad ibn al-Husayn al-Sulami al-Naysaburi
**Born:** 325 AH / 937 CE, Nishapur
**Died:** 412 AH / 1021 CE, Nishapur
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Major Works:** Tabaqat al-Sufiyya (Generations of the Sufis), Haqaiq al-Tafsir (Realities of Quranic Exegesis)

### Biography

Abu Abd al-Rahman al-Sulami was the great Sufi biographer and compiler of Nishapur. His Tabaqat al-Sufiyya (Generations of the Sufis) is one of the earliest systematic biographical dictionaries of Sufi masters, providing invaluable information about figures whose own writings have been lost. He also composed a mystical commentary on the Quran (Haqaiq al-Tafsir) that collected the Sufi interpretations of Quranic verses from earlier masters.

Born into a family of scholars and Sufis — his grandfather Muhammad ibn Dawud was a well-known hadith transmitter and Sufi — al-Sulami studied under numerous masters in Nishapur, which was one of the great centers of Islamic learning in the eastern Muslim world. He is said to have compiled over a hundred works on various aspects of Sufism.

### Key Teachings

Al-Sulami's genius was as a collector and preserver rather than an innovator. He understood that the oral traditions of the early Sufi masters needed to be documented before they were lost. His compilations ensured that the sayings of Junaid, Bayazid, Dhul-Nun, and others survived for posterity.

### Legacy

Without al-Sulami, much of early Sufi history would be lost. His Tabaqat was the model for later biographical works, including those by al-Qushayri, al-Hujwiri, Attar, and Jami. He is the great preserver of the Sufi heritage.

## Chapter 22: Abu al-Qasim al-Qushayri (أبو القاسم القشيري)

**Full Name:** Abu al-Qasim Abd al-Karim ibn Hawazin al-Qushayri al-Naysaburi
**Born:** 376 AH / 986 CE, Ustawa (near Nishapur)
**Died:** 465 AH / 1072 CE, Nishapur
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era; studied under Abu Ali al-Daqqaq
**Titles:** A leading Ash'ari theologian and Shafi'i jurist
**Teachers:** Abu Ali al-Daqqaq (his father-in-law and spiritual master)
**Major Work:** al-Risala al-Qushayriyya (The Qushayri Treatise)

### Biography

Abu al-Qasim al-Qushayri authored what is arguably the most important single manual of Sufism ever written — the Risala al-Qushayriyya. This masterwork, composed around 437 AH (1045 CE), was addressed to the Sufis of his time and served both as a defense of authentic tasawwuf and as a comprehensive guide to its teachings.

Al-Qushayri was born into an Arab family and studied under the great Sufi master Abu Ali al-Daqqaq in Nishapur, eventually marrying his daughter. He was also a distinguished Ash'ari theologian and Shafi'i jurist, embodying the ideal of combining exoteric and esoteric knowledge.

The Risala opens with biographical entries on the major Sufi masters, then systematically treats the terminology, stations (maqamat), and states (ahwal) of the spiritual path. Each topic is illustrated with sayings from the masters and grounded in Quranic verses and hadith. It became the standard textbook of Sufi studies across the Muslim world and remains in use to this day.

### Key Teachings

Al-Qushayri insisted that genuine Sufism is inseparable from adherence to the Sharia. He wrote the Risala partly in response to what he perceived as the decline of authentic tasawwuf — charlatans who used Sufi terminology without genuine spiritual realization or proper scholarly training.

### Famous Stories

**The Address to the Sufis:** In the introduction to the Risala, al-Qushayri writes with evident concern: *"I have seen the manners of this group decline in our time. The carpet of the Sharia has been folded up... They have abandoned scrupulousness and piety, and consider neither the lawful nor the forbidden."* This passionate plea for authenticity resonates across the centuries.

### Legacy

The Risala al-Qushayriyya became the definitive reference work for Sufi terminology and doctrine. It was studied, commented upon, and taught across every region of the Muslim world. Along with al-Ghazali's Ihya, it remains one of the two most important texts in the Sufi canon.

## Chapter 23: Ali ibn Uthman al-Hujwiri — Data Ganj Bakhsh (علي بن عثمان الهجويري)

**Full Name:** Abu al-Hasan Ali ibn Uthman ibn Abi Ali al-Jullabi al-Hujwiri al-Ghaznawi
**Born:** c. 390 AH / 1000 CE, Hujwir (near Ghazni, present-day Afghanistan)
**Died:** c. 465 AH / 1072 CE (dates approximate), Lahore
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era; in the Junaydi tradition
**Titles:** Data Ganj Bakhsh (داتا گنج بخش — "The Bestower of Treasures"), Patron Saint of Lahore
**Teachers:** Abu al-Fadl Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Khuttali
**Major Work:** Kashf al-Mahjub (Revelation of the Veiled) — the oldest Sufi treatise in Persian

### Biography

Ali al-Hujwiri — universally known as Data Ganj Bakhsh (The Bestower of Treasures) — holds a unique place in Islamic history as the first major Sufi master to settle permanently in the Indian subcontinent. Born near Ghazni in present-day Afghanistan, he traveled extensively across the Muslim world before being divinely guided to settle in Lahore.

His masterwork, Kashf al-Mahjub (Revelation of the Veiled), is the oldest surviving Sufi treatise in the Persian language. It is a comprehensive survey of Sufi doctrine, history, and practice, covering the lives of major masters, the stations and states of the spiritual path, and the major theological questions debated among Sufis. Written in an accessible yet profound style, it has served as the introductory text on Sufism for countless seekers over nearly a millennium.

Hujwiri arrived in Lahore during the Ghaznavid period and established himself there, becoming the spiritual patron of the city. His shrine in Lahore — Data Darbar — is one of the most visited sacred sites in all of South Asia, drawing millions of pilgrims annually from all faiths and backgrounds.

### Key Teachings

Hujwiri discussed extensively the difference between the Junaydi (sober) and Bistami (ecstatic) approaches to Sufism, ultimately advocating for a balanced path. He classified Sufi schools of thought into twelve groups and evaluated their positions with remarkable fairness and scholarship.

He taught that the goal of Sufism is the direct knowledge (kashf) of divine realities that are "veiled" (mahjub) from ordinary perception — hence the title of his book.

### Famous Stories

**The Divine Command to Go to Lahore:** Hujwiri initially did not wish to go to Lahore, preferring to remain in the heartlands of Islamic civilization. But he received a spiritual command directing him there. Upon arriving, he found that another saint — Shaykh Husayn Zanjani — had recently died. Hujwiri understood that he had been sent as his spiritual successor.

**Moinuddin Chishti's Visit:** Over a century after Hujwiri's death, the great Chishti saint Moinuddin Chishti visited his tomb upon arriving in the subcontinent and declared: *"Ganj Bakhsh fayz-i alam, mazhar-i nur-i Khuda / Na-qisan ra pir-i kamil, kamilan ra rahnuma"* — "Bestower of treasures, beneficence of the world, manifestation of God's light / A perfect guide for the imperfect, a guide for the perfect." This tribute from one great saint to another cemented Hujwiri's status.

### Legacy

Data Ganj Bakhsh is the patron saint of Lahore and one of the most beloved saints of the entire Indian subcontinent. His Data Darbar shrine is thronged daily with devotees. The Kashf al-Mahjub remains a standard text in Sufi studies. He opened the door for the massive Sufi missionary expansion into South Asia that would follow in the coming centuries.

## Chapter 24: Abdullah al-Ansari al-Harawi (عبد الله الأنصاري الهروي)

**Full Name:** Abu Isma'il Abdullah ibn Muhammad al-Ansari al-Harawi
**Born:** 396 AH / 1006 CE, Herat (present-day Afghanistan)
**Died:** 481 AH / 1089 CE, Herat
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era; Hanbali scholar and Sufi
**Titles:** Shaykh al-Islam, Pir-i Herat (Sage of Herat)
**Major Works:** Manazil al-Sa'irin (Stations of the Wayfarers), Tabaqat al-Sufiyya (in Persian), Munajat (Intimate Supplications)

### Biography

Abdullah al-Ansari was one of the most remarkable scholars of medieval Islam — a fierce Hanbali theologian who was also one of the greatest Sufi masters of his era. Born in Herat (present-day Afghanistan), he claimed descent from the Companion Abu Ayyub al-Ansari and spent his entire life in his beloved city, which he helped transform into a major center of Islamic scholarship.

His Manazil al-Sa'irin (Stations of the Wayfarers) is one of the most sophisticated and demanding manuals of the spiritual path ever written — a concise guide that maps out one hundred spiritual stations arranged in ten groups of ten. Its density and profundity inspired one of the most important commentaries in Sufi literature: Ibn al-Qayyim al-Jawziyya's Madarij al-Salikin.

His Munajat (Intimate Supplications) — lyrical prayers addressed directly to God — are among the most beautiful devotional texts in the Persian language and continue to be recited widely.

### Key Teachings

Al-Ansari's spiritual path culminates in the station of tawhid (divine unity), where all distinctions dissolve — including the distinction between the wayfarer and the destination. His approach was uncompromising and intellectually demanding, reflecting his Hanbali insistence on textual fidelity combined with profound mystical insight.

### Famous Stories

**His Exiles and Returns:** Al-Ansari was exiled from Herat multiple times — at least six — due to theological disputes with the Ash'ari establishment. Each time, popular pressure and his sheer spiritual authority forced his recall. His fiery temperament and refusal to compromise on theological points made him both controversial and deeply revered.

### Legacy

Al-Ansari is proof that Sufism transcends theological school boundaries. A committed Hanbali — the school often associated with opposition to Sufism — he was simultaneously one of the tradition's greatest masters. His Manazil al-Sa'irin, through Ibn al-Qayyim's commentary, influenced even scholars who were otherwise critical of Sufi practices. His Munajat remain among the jewels of Persian devotional literature.

## Chapter 25: Abu Hamid al-Ghazali (أبو حامد الغزالي)

**Full Name:** Abu Hamid Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Tusi al-Ghazali
**Born:** 450 AH / 1058 CE, Tus (near Mashhad, Iran)
**Died:** 14 Jumada al-Thani 505 AH / 19 December 1111 CE, Tus
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era; later associated with various traditions
**Titles:** Hujjat al-Islam (Proof of Islam), Zayn al-Din (Ornament of the Faith), Mujaddid of the 5th Century
**Teachers:** Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni (in theology/jurisprudence), Abu Ali al-Farmadhi (in tasawwuf)
**Major Works:** Ihya Ulum al-Din, al-Munqidh min al-Dalal, Kimiya-yi Sa'adat, Mishkat al-Anwar

### Biography

Abu Hamid al-Ghazali is arguably the most influential Muslim thinker after the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself. His intellectual and spiritual achievement — the definitive reconciliation of Sufism with mainstream Sunni Islam — reshaped the entire landscape of Islamic civilization and earned him the unique title Hujjat al-Islam (The Proof of Islam).

Born in Tus, Khorasan, al-Ghazali was educated at the Nizamiyya madrasa in Nishapur under the great Ash'ari theologian Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni. By his early thirties, he was the most celebrated scholar in Baghdad, holding the prestigious chair at the Nizamiyya of Baghdad — the most important academic position in the Sunni world. He had mastered theology (kalam), jurisprudence (fiqh), philosophy (falsafa), and the art of debate.

Then, at the height of his fame and success, al-Ghazali experienced a devastating spiritual crisis. He realized that all his learning was intellectual, not experiential — he knew about God but did not know God. He was paralyzed by doubt. In 488 AH (1095 CE), he dramatically abandoned his professorship, left his family, and became a wandering Sufi, spending years in seclusion in Damascus, Jerusalem, Hebron, Medina, and Mecca.

During these years of wandering and spiritual practice, al-Ghazali experienced the transformation he had been seeking. He discovered that the Sufi path — direct experience of God through purification of the heart and spiritual discipline — provided the certainty that philosophy and theology could not.

He returned to write his magnum opus, the Ihya Ulum al-Din (Revival of the Religious Sciences) — a monumental work in four volumes covering worship, daily life, the destructive vices, and the saving virtues. The Ihya demonstrated that every aspect of Islamic practice, from prayer to commerce to marriage, has an outer form (governed by fiqh) and an inner reality (governed by tasawwuf). Neither is complete without the other.

### Key Teachings

Al-Ghazali's central insight is that Islam has an outer dimension (the Sharia) and an inner dimension (the Haqiqa/Tariqa), and both are essential. A religion of mere outward observance without inner transformation is empty; a mysticism without grounding in sacred law is dangerous. The two must be united.

He also authored the devastating Tahafut al-Falasifa (Incoherence of the Philosophers), which demolished the pretensions of purely rational philosophy to reach certainty about ultimate questions. Only the path of spiritual experience (dhawq) — the Sufi path — can provide genuine certainty.

### Famous Stories

**The Spiritual Crisis:** Al-Ghazali describes in his autobiographical al-Munqidh min al-Dalal (Deliverance from Error) how, at the height of his career, he was struck by a paralyzing doubt about the foundations of all knowledge. He could not eat or speak. His tongue literally would not move to deliver lectures. Physicians examined him and declared that the cause was spiritual, not physical. Finally, he fled Baghdad in secret, pretending he was going on pilgrimage.

**The Years of Wandering:** For approximately eleven years, al-Ghazali lived as a wandering Sufi. He spent time in seclusion in the great mosque of Damascus, performed the Hajj, visited the Prophet's ﷺ tomb in Medina, and lived in Jerusalem near the Dome of the Rock. He wore the woolen garb of the Sufis, performed dhikr, and practiced the spiritual disciplines he had previously only studied intellectually.

**The Return:** When al-Ghazali was urged to return to teaching, he initially refused. He had found what he was looking for and had no desire for academic fame. But eventually, convinced that God required him to share what he had learned, he returned — not to the old intellectual debates but to teach the way of the heart.

### Legacy

Al-Ghazali's impact is almost impossible to overstate. The Ihya Ulum al-Din became the most widely read and studied text in the Islamic world after the Quran and hadith collections. It was said: *"If all the books of Islam were destroyed, the Ihya alone would suffice."* He made Sufism not merely acceptable but essential to mainstream Islam. After al-Ghazali, the question was no longer "Is Sufism legitimate?" but "How does one practice it properly?"

## Chapter 26: Ahmad al-Ghazali (أحمد الغزالي)

**Full Name:** Abu al-Futuh Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Ghazali
**Born:** c. 453 AH / 1061 CE, Tus
**Died:** 520 AH / 1126 CE, Qazvin
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era
**Titles:** Master of the Doctrine of Love
**Teachers:** Abu Bakr al-Nassaj (in tasawwuf)
**Notable Students:** Ayn al-Qudat Hamadani
**Major Work:** Sawanih al-Ushshaq (Inspirations of the Lovers)

### Biography

Ahmad al-Ghazali, the younger brother of the great Hujjat al-Islam, was in many ways his spiritual counterpart and complement. Where Abu Hamid reconciled Sufism with law and theology through systematic scholarship, Ahmad dove directly into the ocean of divine love and emerged with one of the most intoxicating treatises ever written on the subject — the Sawanih al-Ushshaq.

Ahmad was the more naturally gifted Sufi of the two brothers. When Abu Hamid abandoned his Baghdad professorship for the spiritual path, it was Ahmad who took over his teaching post. Yet Ahmad's own spiritual orientation was entirely different — he was a master of 'ishq (passionate love), teaching that love is the supreme reality of existence and the only path to God.

### Key Teachings

Ahmad al-Ghazali's doctrine of love ('ishq) is radical: he taught that love is not merely an attribute of the seeker but the fundamental nature of existence itself. God created the world through love and for love. The lover, the beloved, and love itself are ultimately one. This teaching influenced the entire Persian mystical poetry tradition, flowing directly into Rumi through the chain of Ahmad → Ayn al-Qudat → the Kubrawiyya tradition.

### Famous Stories

**The Nightingale Sermon:** Ahmad once delivered a sermon on divine love so powerful that a nightingale that had been perched on the window fell dead from the intensity of the spiritual state. This story became an iconic illustration of the overwhelming power of love.

### Legacy

Ahmad al-Ghazali's Sawanih is the foundational text of the "love mysticism" (madhhab al-'ishq) tradition in Sufism. His influence on Ayn al-Qudat Hamadani, Ruzbihan Baqli, and ultimately Rumi makes him one of the most important — if underappreciated — figures in Islamic intellectual history.

## Chapter 27: Ayn al-Qudat Hamadani (عين القضاة همداني)

**Full Name:** Abu al-Ma'ali Abdullah ibn Muhammad ibn Ali al-Miyanji
**Born:** 492 AH / 1098 CE, Hamadan
**Died:** 7 Jumada al-Thani 525 AH / 6 May 1131 CE, Hamadan (executed at age 33)
**Sufi Order:** Pre-tariqa era; student of Ahmad al-Ghazali
**Titles:** The Young Martyr Mystic
**Teachers:** Ahmad al-Ghazali
**Major Works:** Tamhidat (Preludes), Zubdat al-Haqa'iq (Cream of Realities)

### Biography

Ayn al-Qudat Hamadani — "The Eye of the Judges" — was a brilliant young scholar and mystic who was executed at the age of thirty-three, making him one of the most tragic and luminous figures in Sufi history. A prodigy who mastered Islamic jurisprudence while still in his teens, he became a judge (qadi) before encountering Ahmad al-Ghazali, whose teachings on divine love transformed him utterly.

His writings are among the most daring in Sufi literature. He defended al-Hallaj, argued for the spiritual status of Iblis (Satan) as a lover of God who refused to bow to anyone but God, and explored the boundaries of mystical theology with a fearlessness that ultimately cost him his life.

### Key Teachings

Ayn al-Qudat taught that love knows no boundaries and that even figures conventionally considered "fallen" — like Iblis — may have a hidden spiritual reality. His defense of al-Hallaj and his exploration of the paradox of divine predestination made him controversial.

### Famous Stories

**The Execution at Thirty-Three:** Ayn al-Qudat was accused of heresy, tried, and executed by crucifixion and burning at the age of thirty-three — the same method and the same charges as al-Hallaj two centuries earlier. Before his death, he wrote a heartbreaking apologia, the Shakwa al-Gharib (Complaint of the Stranger), in which he protested his innocence while accepting his fate. He wrote: *"I know that my words will be understood only after my death, just as the words of al-Hallaj were understood only after his."*

### Legacy

Ayn al-Qudat's Tamhidat is considered one of the masterpieces of Persian mystical prose. His premature martyrdom — and the intellectual brilliance of his surviving works — have ensured his place as one of the most poignant figures in Sufi history.

## Chapter 28: Yusuf al-Hamadhani (يوسف الهمذاني)

**Full Name:** Abu Ya'qub Yusuf ibn Ayyub al-Hamadhani
**Born:** 440 AH / 1048 CE, Hamadan
**Died:** 535 AH / 1140 CE, Merv (Khorasan)
**Sufi Order:** Key link in the Khwajagan/Naqshbandi silsila
**Teachers:** Abu Ali al-Farmadhi (who was also al-Ghazali's Sufi teacher)
**Notable Students:** Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani, Ahmad Yasawi

### Biography

Yusuf al-Hamadhani is one of the most important links in the chain that would eventually produce the Naqshbandiyya — one of the most influential Sufi orders in history. Born in Hamadan, he studied in Baghdad under Abu Ali al-Farmadhi (who was, significantly, also the Sufi master of Abu Hamid al-Ghazali). He then settled in Central Asia, where he trained the next generation of masters who would carry the tradition forward.

His two most important students were Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani — who formulated the "eight principles" of the Khwajagan (Masters) tradition that became the foundation of Naqshbandi practice — and Ahmad Yasawi, who would carry Islam to the Turkic peoples of the steppes.

### Key Teachings

Yusuf al-Hamadhani emphasized the combination of outward adherence to the Sharia with inward spiritual discipline. He taught silent dhikr (remembrance of God) as opposed to vocal dhikr — a practice that would become the hallmark of the Naqshbandiyya.

### Legacy

Without Yusuf al-Hamadhani, neither the Naqshbandiyya nor the Yasawiyya would exist in their present forms. He is the node from which two of Central Asia's most important spiritual movements branched.

## Chapter 29: Ahmad Yasawi (أحمد يسوي)

**Full Name:** Khwaja Ahmad ibn Ibrahim ibn Ilyas Yasawi
**Born:** c. 1093 CE, Sayram (present-day Kazakhstan)
**Died:** 562 AH / 1166 CE, Yasi (present-day Turkistan, Kazakhstan)
**Sufi Order:** Founder of the Yasawiyya
**Titles:** Pir-i Turkistan (Master of Turkestan), Hazrat Sultan
**Teachers:** Yusuf al-Hamadhani, Arslan Bab (according to tradition)
**Major Work:** Diwan-i Hikmat (Collection of Wisdom)

### Biography

Ahmad Yasawi is the great apostle of Islam to the Turkic peoples of Central Asia. Born in the region of present-day southern Kazakhstan, he studied under Yusuf al-Hamadhani in Bukhara and then returned to his homeland, settling in the city of Yasi. There he established the Yasawiyya order, which became the primary vehicle for the spread of Islam among the nomadic and semi-nomadic Turkic peoples of the vast Central Asian steppes.

Yasawi's genius was linguistic and cultural. He composed his Diwan-i Hikmat (Collection of Wisdom) in Turkic rather than Arabic or Persian — a revolutionary decision that made Islamic spiritual teachings accessible to the common Turkic-speaking people who could not understand the classical languages of Islamic scholarship. His simple, passionate poems — called hikmats — expressed the deepest truths of the spiritual path in the language of the steppe.

According to tradition, when Yasawi reached the age of sixty-three — the age at which the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ died — he retreated to an underground cell beneath his mosque and spent the rest of his life in seclusion there, saying it was not fitting for him to remain above ground longer than the Prophet ﷺ had lived.

### Famous Stories

**The Underground Cell:** At age sixty-three, Yasawi descended into a small underground room (chilakhana) beneath his mosque and remained there in worship and seclusion until his death. This dramatic act of devotion — choosing to live buried alive out of love for the Prophet ﷺ — became legendary across Central Asia.

**Timur's Tribute:** Over two centuries after Yasawi's death, the great conqueror Timur (Tamerlane) built a magnificent mausoleum over his grave — one of the largest and most beautiful Islamic structures in Central Asia. The unfinished mausoleum in Turkistan, Kazakhstan, remains a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a major pilgrimage destination.

### Legacy

Ahmad Yasawi is the father of Turkic Islamic spirituality. His Yasawiyya order spread Islam across the entire Turkic world — from Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan to Anatolia and beyond. The Bektashi order, which later became the spiritual order of the Ottoman Janissaries, traces its lineage partly to Yasawi. He is the most revered saint of Central Asia.

## Chapter 30: Abdul Qadir al-Jilani (عبد القادر الجيلاني)

**Full Name:** Abu Muhammad Abdul Qadir ibn Abi Salih Musa Jangidost al-Jilani al-Hasani al-Husayni
**Born:** 1 Ramadan 470 AH / 1077 CE, Jilan (Gilan, Iran)
**Died:** 11 Rabi al-Thani 561 AH / 1166 CE, Baghdad
**Sufi Order:** Founder of the Qadiriyya — the most widespread Sufi order in the world
**Titles:** Ghawth al-Azam (The Greatest Helper), Sultan al-Awliya (Sultan of the Saints), Muhyi al-Din (Reviver of the Religion), Piran-i Pir (Master of Masters), al-Baz al-Ashhab (The Grey Falcon)
**Teachers:** Abu al-Khayr Hammad al-Dabbas, Abu Sa'id al-Mubarak al-Mukharrami (in fiqh), various hadith scholars
**Notable Students:** His sons (Abdul Wahhab, Abdul Razzaq, Isa, Musa, Abdul Aziz), and thousands of disciples
**Burial:** Baghdad — his shrine is one of the most visited in the Islamic world

### Biography

Abdul Qadir al-Jilani is the most universally venerated saint in Sunni Islam and the founder of the Qadiriyya — the most geographically widespread Sufi order in history, with millions of followers across the Arab world, Africa, South Asia, Southeast Asia, and beyond. His titles — Ghawth al-Azam (The Greatest Helper), Sultan al-Awliya (Sultan of the Saints), Muhyi al-Din (Reviver of the Religion) — reflect the unparalleled reverence in which he is held.

Born in the province of Gilan (Jilan) in northern Iran on the first of Ramadan, 470 AH, into a family of sayyids (descendants of the Prophet ﷺ through both Hasan and Husayn), Abdul Qadir came to Baghdad at the age of eighteen to study. He mastered the Islamic sciences — Hanbali jurisprudence, hadith, Quranic exegesis, theology — before undergoing a prolonged period of spiritual training that lasted approximately twenty-five years.

During these years of spiritual preparation, Abdul Qadir wandered the deserts and ruins of Iraq, enduring extreme privation, spiritual tests, and prolonged seclusion. He is said to have spent years in the Iraqi wilderness, eating almost nothing, sleeping on the ground, and struggling with his lower self.

When he finally emerged to teach, the effect was volcanic. His sermons at his madrasa in Baghdad drew thousands. He spoke with an authority and power that contemporaries described as unlike anything they had witnessed. People converted to Islam in droves at his gatherings — accounts speak of hundreds of Jews and Christians accepting Islam after hearing him preach, and thousands of sinners and criminals repenting.

### Key Teachings

Abdul Qadir's teachings, preserved in his collected sermons — Futuh al-Ghayb (Revelations of the Unseen) and al-Fath al-Rabbani (The Sublime Revelation) — combine strict adherence to the Sharia with deep mystical insight. He insisted that the Sufi path must be walked in total conformity with Islamic law. He taught that the spiritual seeker must pass through three stages: abandoning the world, abandoning the self, and abandoning everything for God.

He said: *"This world is the abode of affliction, and the next world is the abode of fear. The servant should not feel at ease in either — he should feel at ease only with his Lord."*

### Famous Stories

**The Highway Robbers:** When young Abdul Qadir set out from Gilan to Baghdad, his mother sewed gold coins into the lining of his garment for the journey. She made him promise to always tell the truth. On the road, his caravan was attacked by bandits. When they asked him if he had anything valuable, he said: "Yes, I have forty gold coins sewn into my garment." The robbers were astonished — no one had ever told them the truth before. Their leader wept and repented on the spot, and his entire band followed suit, becoming Abdul Qadir's first "converts."

**"My Foot Is on the Neck of Every Saint":** At the height of his spiritual authority, Abdul Qadir publicly declared: *"Qadami hadhihi 'ala raqabati kulli waliyy"* — "This foot of mine is on the neck of every saint." This bold claim, rather than being rejected, was accepted by the great masters of his time, who bowed to his spiritual supremacy. It is said that when he made this declaration, every living saint across the Muslim world spiritually acknowledged his authority.

**The Conversion of the Masses:** His public sermons in Baghdad were legendary. It is reported that at a single gathering, hundreds would repent of their sins, and scores of non-Muslims would accept Islam. His madrasa became the spiritual center of Baghdad and, by extension, of the Islamic world.

### Major Works

- **Futuh al-Ghayb** (Revelations of the Unseen) — 78 collected discourses
- **Al-Fath al-Rabbani** (The Sublime Revelation) — 62 sermons
- **Al-Ghunya li-Talibi Tariq al-Haqq** (Sufficient Provision for Seekers of the Path of Truth) — a comprehensive manual of Islamic practice and Sufi ethics

### Legacy

The Qadiriyya order spread across the entire Muslim world — from Morocco to Indonesia, from the Caucasus to South Africa. Abdul Qadir's shrine in Baghdad remains one of the most visited holy sites in Islam. The annual celebration of his birth (Giyarwin Sharif) is observed by millions. He is invoked by devotees in times of need more than perhaps any other figure in Islamic history besides the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. His influence is incalculable.

## Chapter 31: Abu Madyan Shu'ayb (أبو مدين شعيب)

**Full Name:** Abu Madyan Shu'ayb ibn al-Husayn al-Ansari al-Andalusi
**Born:** c. 509 AH / 1115 CE, Cantillana (near Seville, al-Andalus)
**Died:** 594 AH / 1198 CE, near Tlemcen (present-day Algeria)
**Sufi Order:** His own tradition, linking the Suhrawardiyya and proto-Shadhiliyya
**Titles:** Qutb al-Ghawth (Pole of Help), Sayyid al-Salikin (Master of the Wayfarers), "Teacher of Teachers"
**Teachers:** Abu Ya'za al-Maghribi (a Berber saint), and scholars of Fez and Bougie

### Biography

Abu Madyan is the patron saint of Tlemcen and the greatest Sufi master of the medieval Maghreb. Born in Andalusia, orphaned young, he crossed to North Africa and studied under various masters in Morocco and Algeria before settling near Tlemcen, where his fame drew disciples from across the Muslim West.

His spiritual authority was so great that the Almohad rulers of the Maghreb became alarmed. When the sultan summoned him to Marrakesh, Abu Madyan set out but died on the road near Tlemcen — whether from illness or, as some traditions suggest, from poison. The sultan, regretting his action, built a magnificent shrine over his tomb.

### Key Teachings

Abu Madyan taught a Sufism of balance — combining spiritual intoxication with adherence to the Sharia, love of God with service to creation. His famous saying: *"He who does not benefit people is not a true Sufi."*

### Famous Stories

**The Apprenticeship of Poverty:** Abu Madyan grew up in extreme poverty after being orphaned. He worked as a shepherd and a weaver's apprentice. This experience of deprivation became the foundation of his spiritual empathy and his insistence that Sufism must serve the poor.

### Legacy

Abu Madyan's spiritual lineage flowed directly into the Shadhiliyya order — Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili's teacher, Abd al-Salam ibn Mashish, was in Abu Madyan's chain. Thus, one of the largest Sufi orders in the world traces its spiritual heritage through him.

## Chapter 32: Shihabuddin Umar al-Suhrawardi (شهاب الدين عمر السهروردي)

**Full Name:** Abu Hafs Shihab al-Din Umar ibn Muhammad al-Suhrawardi al-Baghdadi
**Born:** 539 AH / 1145 CE, Suhraward (near Zanjan, Iran)
**Died:** 632 AH / 1234 CE, Baghdad
**Sufi Order:** Systematizer of the Suhrawardiyya order (originally established by his uncle Abu al-Najib)
**Titles:** Shaykh al-Shuyukh (Master of Masters — an official Abbasid title)
**Teachers:** His uncle Abu al-Najib al-Suhrawardi, Abdul Qadir al-Jilani
**Major Work:** Awarif al-Ma'arif (Gifts of Gnosis)

### Biography

Shihabuddin Umar al-Suhrawardi was a towering figure who combined spiritual authority with political influence to a degree rarely seen in Sufi history. Based in Baghdad, he served as the Abbasid caliph's spiritual advisor and diplomatic envoy, traveling to the courts of the Seljuk sultans and the Ayyubids while simultaneously running a major khanqah and producing one of the most important Sufi manuals ever written.

His Awarif al-Ma'arif (Gifts of Gnosis) became the standard handbook for khanqah life — covering everything from the etiquette of the master-disciple relationship to the rules of communal living, from the practice of dhikr to the proper conduct of sama' (spiritual audition/music). It was so widely adopted that it was taught as a textbook at al-Azhar University in Cairo.

### Key Teachings

Al-Suhrawardi advocated for what might be called "engaged Sufism" — the Sufi who participates actively in society rather than withdrawing from it. Unlike the Chishtis, who generally avoided political power, the Suhrawardiyya model embraced engagement with rulers as a means of influencing society for good.

### Legacy

The Suhrawardiyya order spread across the Muslim world, becoming particularly important in the Indian subcontinent through Bahauddin Zakariya Multani. The Awarif al-Ma'arif remains a foundational text of Sufi institutional life.

## Chapter 33: Najm al-Din Kubra (نجم الدين كبرى)

**Full Name:** Abu al-Jannab Ahmad ibn Umar Najm al-Din al-Kubra al-Khiwaqi
**Born:** 540 AH / 1145 CE, Khiva (Khwarezm, present-day Uzbekistan)
**Died:** 618 AH / 1221 CE, Khiva (killed during the Mongol invasion)
**Sufi Order:** Founder of the Kubrawiyya order
**Titles:** Wali-Tarash (The Saint-Maker) — so named because so many of his students became saints; al-Tammat al-Kubra (The Greatest Calamity — a title given during a scholarly debate)
**Teachers:** Various masters including Ruzbihan al-Wazzan al-Misri, Ismail al-Qasri, Ammar al-Bidlisi

### Biography

Najm al-Din Kubra was one of the most remarkable Sufi masters of the pre-Mongol period and the founder of the Kubrawiyya order, which produced many of Central Asia's greatest saints. Born in Khiva in the Khwarezm region, he traveled widely for education before returning to his homeland, where he established a major center of Sufi training.

His title "Wali-Tarash" (The Saint-Maker) speaks to his extraordinary ability as a spiritual teacher — an unusually large number of his students went on to become major saints and masters in their own right, including Majd al-Din al-Baghdadi, Najm al-Din al-Razi ("Daya"), and Sa'd al-Din al-Hammu'i.

Najm al-Din Kubra's most distinctive contribution was his detailed investigation of the visionary experiences that occur during Sufi meditation — particularly the perception of colored lights (anwar). He systematically mapped the colors, sounds, and visions that mark different stages of spiritual progress, creating what might be called a "phenomenology of mystical experience."

### Key Teachings

Kubra taught that the inner world of the mystic mirrors the outer cosmos — "what is above is like what is below." He mapped the lata'if (subtle spiritual centers) of the human being and associated each with specific colors of light that appear during meditation. This "color mysticism" was unique to his school and influenced later traditions.

### Famous Stories

**Martyrdom Against the Mongols:** When the Mongol armies of Genghis Khan besieged Khwarezm in 1221 CE, Najm al-Din Kubra — by then an elderly shaykh — refused to flee despite being offered safe passage. He took up arms and fought alongside the defenders, dying in battle. His martyrdom became a powerful symbol of the Sufi who combines spiritual mastery with physical courage.

### Legacy

The Kubrawiyya order, though smaller than the Qadiriyya or Naqshbandiyya, was one of the most intellectually influential Sufi traditions, particularly in Iran and Central Asia. Kubra's investigation of visionary experience influenced the Naqshbandiyya and other later orders. His martyrdom fighting the Mongols made him a hero of Islamic resistance.

## Chapter 34: Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili (أبو الحسن الشاذلي)

**Full Name:** Abu al-Hasan Ali ibn Abdullah ibn Abd al-Jabbar al-Shadhili al-Hasani
**Born:** c. 593 AH / 1196 CE, Ghumara region (northern Morocco)
**Died:** 656 AH / 1258 CE, Humaythira (near the Red Sea, Egypt, while on the way to Hajj)
**Sufi Order:** Founder of the Shadhiliyya order
**Titles:** Qutb (Pole), Imam of the Shadhiliyya
**Teachers:** Abd al-Salam ibn Mashish (his primary master, himself in the chain of Abu Madyan)

### Biography

Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili is the founder of the Shadhiliyya order — one of the largest and most influential Sufi traditions in the world, with millions of followers across North Africa, Egypt, the Levant, and increasingly in the West. His approach to Sufism was distinctive and revolutionary: he insisted that the spiritual path does not require withdrawal from the world, poverty, or distinctive clothing. His disciples were merchants, scholars, judges, and craftsmen who maintained their worldly occupations while pursuing the inner path.

Born in Morocco, al-Shadhili studied under the great master Abd al-Salam ibn Mashish in the Rif mountains before migrating to Tunis and eventually to Alexandria, Egypt, where he established the main center of his order. He lived as a respected member of society, dressed well, and rejected ostentatious asceticism.

### Key Teachings

Al-Shadhili taught that the Sufi should be inwardly detached but outwardly engaged. He rejected the wearing of patched garments (khirqa) as a sign of spiritual status: *"Piety is in the heart, not in the garment."* He emphasized gratitude (shukr) over patience (sabr), and taught his followers to see the blessings of God in every situation.

His spiritual litanies (ahzab) — including the Hizb al-Bahr (Litany of the Sea) — are among the most widely recited prayers in the Islamic world and are credited with extraordinary spiritual power.

### Famous Stories

**The Hizb al-Bahr:** Al-Shadhili composed the Hizb al-Bahr (Litany of the Sea) during a sea voyage when his ship was threatened by a storm. He said: *"I did not compose it except through divine dictation. If a thousand armies were to line up against you, they could not harm you if you recited it."* This litany has been recited by Muslims for protection in times of danger for over seven centuries.

**Meeting with Ibn Mashish:** When al-Shadhili climbed the mountain to meet his teacher Abd al-Salam ibn Mashish, he prepared himself ceremonially and carried his scholarly credentials. Ibn Mashish told him: *"O Ali, you have come to us decorated with your knowledge and deeds — strip yourself of all that and come to us poor."* This encounter transformed al-Shadhili's understanding of the path.

### Legacy

The Shadhiliyya is one of the most adaptable and widespread Sufi orders. Its branches include the Darqawiyya, Alawiyya, Jazuliyya, and many others. It has been particularly successful in the modern era, attracting many Western converts. The Shadhili emphasis on combining worldly engagement with inner spirituality resonates strongly with contemporary seekers.

## Chapter 35: Ahmad al-Rifa'i (أحمد الرفاعي)

**Full Name:** Abu al-Abbas Ahmad ibn Ali ibn Ahmad ibn Yahya al-Rifa'i al-Husayni
**Born:** 512 AH / 1118 CE, Umm Ubayda (Batiha marshlands, southern Iraq)
**Died:** 578 AH / 1182 CE, Umm Ubayda
**Sufi Order:** Founder of the Rifa'iyya order (also known as the Ahmadiyya or Bata'ihiyya)
**Titles:** Abu al-Alamayn (Father of the Two Banners — referring to his dual descent from Hasan and Husayn), al-Sayyid al-Kabir
**Teachers:** His maternal uncle Mansur al-Bata'ihi, and through the chain to Junaid
**Notable Lineage:** A sayyid — descendant of the Prophet ﷺ through Husayn

### Biography

Ahmad al-Rifa'i is the founder of the Rifa'iyya order, one of the oldest organized tariqas in Sufi history. Born in the marshlands of southern Iraq, he grew up in extreme humility and spent his entire life in the rural Batiha region, never seeking the great cities or the company of rulers. His humility was legendary — he was known to sit among the poor and disabled, personally tending to lepers and the sick.

Despite his remote location, his fame spread across the Muslim world through his students and the remarkable spiritual experiences attributed to his gatherings — including demonstrations of invulnerability to fire, serpents, and other physical harms that became characteristic of the Rifa'iyya tradition.

### Key Teachings

Ahmad al-Rifa'i taught extreme humility (tawadu') as the highest spiritual station. He said: *"The highest path is the path of humility. Be humble and you will be elevated."* He rejected any form of spiritual arrogance and insisted that his followers serve the poorest members of society.

### Famous Stories

**The Prophet's Hand:** The most famous story associated with Ahmad al-Rifa'i occurred during his pilgrimage to Medina. Standing before the Prophet's ﷺ tomb, overcome with love, he recited: *"In the state of distance, I used to send my spirit to kiss the earth on my behalf. Now that I am present, extend your hand so that my lips may receive the blessing."* According to the tradition, the Prophet's ﷺ blessed hand emerged from the tomb, and Ahmad al-Rifa'i kissed it in the presence of thousands of witnesses. This event is commemorated annually by the Rifa'iyya.

**His Humility:** When Ahmad al-Rifa'i visited a town, he would go first to the lepers' colony and the homes of the sick, sitting with them, serving them food, and washing their wounds. He told his followers: *"He who is not humbled by the sight of the afflicted has no place among us."*

### Legacy

The Rifa'iyya order spread widely across the Muslim world, particularly in Egypt, Syria, Turkey, and Southeast Asia. It became known for its dramatic practices — devotees demonstrating invulnerability to fire and serpents — though Ahmad al-Rifa'i himself emphasized inner humility over outward displays. The order remains active today, and Ahmad al-Rifa'i's shrine in the Batiha region of Iraq continues to attract pilgrims.
