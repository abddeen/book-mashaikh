Part 4

The Golden Age

7th–8th Century AH · Ch. 36–50, 101–102

Historical Context

The seventh and eighth Islamic centuries present the most extraordinary paradox in the history of human civilization: the very era in which the Muslim world suffered the most catastrophic physical destruction it had ever known — the Mongol invasions that obliterated cities, libraries, and entire populations — was simultaneously the era of the most magnificent flowering of mystical thought, poetry, and spiritual institution-building in the history of tasawwuf.

In 656 AH (1258 CE), the Mongol armies of Hulagu Khan sacked Baghdad — the greatest city in the medieval world, the seat of the Abbasid caliphate for five centuries, a metropolis of perhaps a million souls. The last Abbasid caliph, al-Musta'sim, was rolled in a carpet and trampled to death by horses. The House of Wisdom, with its incalculable treasures of manuscripts, was destroyed. The waters of the Tigris ran black with the ink of books and red with the blood of scholars. Before Baghdad, the Mongols had already devastated Bukhara, Samarkand, Nishapur, Herat, and Balkh — the great cities of Khorasan and Transoxiana, the heartlands of Islamic intellectual life. Entire civilizations were erased.

Yet it was precisely in response to this cataclysm that the human spirit produced some of its most luminous achievements. The great Persian Sufi poets — Farid al-Din Attar, Jalal al-Din Rumi, Sa'di of Shiraz, and later Hafiz — composed works of such transcendent beauty that they remain, eight centuries later, among the most widely read poetry on earth. It was as though the destruction of the outer world drove the spirit inward with unprecedented intensity, and what it found there was inexhaustible.

From the opposite end of the Muslim world, the Andalusian mystic Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi arrived in the eastern lands bearing a metaphysical revolution of staggering scope. His doctrine of Wahdat al-Wujud (the Unity of Being), his concept of al-Insan al-Kamil (the Perfect Human), and his visionary cosmology transformed all subsequent understanding of God, the cosmos, and the human being's place between them. No Sufi thinker after Ibn Arabi could avoid engaging with his ideas — whether in acceptance, development, or reaction.

This was also the era in which the great Sufi orders reached their full institutional form and began their spread across the known world. The Qadiriyya, already founded in Baghdad, expanded rapidly. The Chishtiyya established itself across the Indian subcontinent through Moinuddin Chishti and his successors. The Suhrawardiyya took root in Multan and Delhi. The Mevleviyya — Rumi's order — arose in Anatolia. The Shadhiliyya spread through Egypt and the Maghreb. The Naqshbandiyya consolidated in Central Asia. The khanqah became a pillar of social life — a center of worship, education, refuge, and charity in a world convulsed by political upheaval and military devastation. And Sufism, carried by these orders and their missionaries, spread far beyond the Arab-Persian heartlands to encompass the entire Indian subcontinent, Anatolia and the nascent Ottoman domains, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the islands of Southeast Asia — a missionary expansion of world-historical significance.

Chapter 36

Farid al-Din Attar

فريد الدين العطار
Full NameAbu Hamid Muhammad ibn Abu Bakr Ibrahim al-Naysaburi, known as Attar (The Perfumer/Apothecary)
Bornc. 540 AH / 1145 CE, Nishapur, Khorasan (present-day Iran)
Diedc. 618 AH / 1221 CE, Nishapur (killed during the Mongol sack of the city)
Sufi OrderKubrawiyya lineage; disciple of Majd al-Din al-Baghdadi
TitlesShaykh al-Mashaikh al-Shu'ara (Master of the Masters of Poets)
TeachersMajd al-Din al-Baghdadi (Kubrawiyya chain)
Major WorksMantiq al-Tayr (Conference of the Birds), Tadhkirat al-Awliya (Memorial of the Saints), Ilahi-nama (Book of God), Musibat-nama (Book of Affliction), Asrar-nama (Book of Secrets)
BurialNishapur — his shrine remains a major pilgrimage site to this day

Biography

Farid al-Din Attar — "The Perfumer" — is one of the supreme poets of the Persian language and one of the most influential voices in the entire history of Sufi literature. His name, Attar, derives from his profession: he was a prosperous pharmacist and apothecary in the great city of Nishapur, the jewel of Khorasan, running a shop where he dispensed herbs, medicines, perfumes, and remedies to the people of the city. It was a trade that brought him into daily contact with thousands of people from every station of life — scholars, soldiers, merchants, beggars, the sick and the desperate — and this intimate acquaintance with the full range of human suffering and longing would pour into his poetry with extraordinary empathy.

His spiritual awakening, according to the most famous account, came through a single devastating encounter. One day, as Attar sat in his shop surrounded by the jars, bottles, and bundles of his considerable trade, a wandering dervish entered and stood gazing at the heaped merchandise with an expression of infinite sadness. Attar asked the dervish what he wanted. The dervish looked at him and asked: "How will you die? How will you leave all of this behind?" Attar, irritated, told the man to mind his own business — dying was the dervish's problem, not his. The dervish replied calmly: "I will leave it easily — like this." And he lay down on the floor of the shop and died. Attar was so profoundly shaken — by the reality of death made suddenly, physically present amid all his accumulated goods — that he closed his shop, gave away his possessions, and set out on the spiritual path from which he would never return.

He became a disciple of Majd al-Din al-Baghdadi, the great master of the Kubrawiyya order (itself founded by Najm al-Din Kubra), and under his guidance underwent rigorous spiritual training. But Attar's genius expressed itself not primarily in the role of a teaching shaykh gathering disciples around him, but rather as a poet and storyteller of unparalleled power. Over the course of his long life, he composed an astonishing body of work — dozens of works totaling well over one hundred thousand verses of Persian poetry, along with prose works of lasting importance. His pen was as inexhaustible as his spiritual vision was deep.

The crowning achievement of his poetic career is the Mantiq al-Tayr — the Conference of the Birds — a vast allegorical poem in which the birds of the world gather to seek the Simurgh, the mythical king of birds. It is a work of such genius that it has been recognized as one of the masterpieces of world literature, transcending its Islamic origins to speak to seekers of truth in every tradition. His Tadhkirat al-Awliya (Memorial of the Saints), a prose work preserving the biographies and sayings of seventy-two Sufi masters, became the primary source through which later generations came to know the stories of the early saints — from Rabia al-Adawiyya to Bayazid Bistami to Mansur al-Hallaj.

Attar lived to an advanced age — approximately seventy-six to eighty years — before his life was ended by the catastrophe that consumed his beloved Nishapur. In 618 AH (1221 CE), the Mongol armies of Genghis Khan descended upon the city in one of the most brutal massacres of the entire invasion. Nishapur, a metropolis that had produced some of the greatest minds in Islamic civilization, was razed to the ground. Attar perished in the destruction, his death marking, with terrible symbolism, the end of an era.

Key Teachings

The Mantiq al-Tayr is the supreme allegory of the spiritual journey in all of Sufi literature. In it, the birds of the world — each representing a different human type, with its particular attachments and excuses — are summoned by the hoopoe (the spiritual guide) to undertake a perilous journey to find the Simurgh, the king of birds who dwells on the mountain of Qaf at the edge of the world. Thousands set out; most turn back, defeated by the hardships of the way. The birds who persevere must cross seven valleys: the Valley of Seeking, the Valley of Love, the Valley of Gnosis, the Valley of Detachment, the Valley of Unity, the Valley of Bewilderment, and the Valley of Poverty and Annihilation (fana). Each valley strips away another layer of the self. When the thirty surviving birds (si-murgh in Persian) finally arrive at the court of the Simurgh, they discover that the Simurgh is themselves — si-murgh means "thirty birds." They see their own faces reflected in the divine mirror. The seeker and the sought are one. The journey of seeking IS the destination, and God is not found in some distant heaven but in the depths of the annihilated self. This supreme pun — si-murgh / Simurgh — contains the entire teaching of Sufi metaphysics in a single word.

Attar's Tadhkirat al-Awliya, meanwhile, performed the indispensable service of preserving the stories, sayings, and spiritual teachings of seventy-two earlier Sufi masters in vivid, accessible prose. Without this work, the biographies of Rabia, Ibrahim ibn Adham, Bayazid Bistami, Dhul-Nun al-Misri, and many others would be far less known and far less richly detailed. Attar understood that the stories of the saints are not merely historical records but living teachings — each anecdote a door through which the reader may enter the spiritual world. His other great theme, woven through all his works, is the total consumption of the lover by love: the moth does not merely approach the flame — it plunges in and is annihilated, and in that annihilation discovers what it truly is.

Famous Stories

The Dervish's Death: The story of Attar's conversion is one of the most famous in Sufi literature. A wandering dervish entered his bustling apothecary shop in Nishapur and stood looking around at the shelves piled high with bottles, jars, herbs, and perfumes — the accumulated wealth of a successful merchant. Attar asked what he wanted. The dervish looked at him with piercing eyes and asked: "How will you die? How will you leave all of this?" Attar, busy with his trade and annoyed by the intrusion, told the dervish to worry about his own death. The dervish said simply: "I will leave it easily — like this." He lay down on the floor of the shop, said the shahada, and died. The perfumer stood frozen among his wares, the corpse of a stranger at his feet, and in that instant the entire edifice of his worldly life collapsed. He understood — not intellectually but in his very marrow — that everything he had accumulated was already lost, that every customer who entered his shop was on the way to the same destination as the dervish. He closed the shop and never returned.

The Encounter with the Young Rumi: Tradition preserves a luminous encounter between the aged Attar and the young Jalal al-Din, who would grow up to become Rumi. The boy was traveling with his father, the scholar and preacher Baha al-Din Walad, who was leading his family westward from Balkh — fleeing, as it happened, the same Mongol onslaught that would soon consume Nishapur. Attar met them on the road. He watched the distinguished father walking ahead, and behind him the young boy — perhaps twelve years old — with eyes of unusual depth. Attar turned to those around him and said: "How strange — here is a great ocean walking behind a mere river." He recognized, with the insight of a saint, that the boy would surpass his father as the sea surpasses a stream. He gave the young Jalal al-Din a copy of his Asrar-nama (Book of Secrets) as a gift. Rumi would carry that book for the rest of his life, and the fire it lit in him would eventually blaze into the Masnavi.

The Thirty Birds: The climax of the Mantiq al-Tayr is among the most celebrated moments in all of mystical literature. After crossing the seven valleys — each one stripping away another veil of selfhood — only thirty birds out of the original thousands remain. Exhausted, broken, emptied of everything they once were, they arrive at the court of the Simurgh. A chamberlain turns them away: "What are you? You are nothing." But they persist. When they are finally admitted to the presence, they find no throne, no king, no separate being awaiting them — only a vast mirror in which they see themselves. Thirty birds — si-murgh — behold the Simurgh. The seeker is the sought. The lover is the beloved. The drop discovers it was always the ocean.

His Death in the Mongol Sack: When the Mongol armies besieged Nishapur in 618 AH (1221 CE), the destruction was total — the entire population was put to the sword, and the city was leveled so completely that crops were planted over the ruins. Attar, by then an elderly man of nearly eighty, did not flee. Tradition preserves a haunting account of his final moments: he was captured by a Mongol soldier, who considered selling him. Someone came forward and offered a bag of straw as ransom for the old man's life. Attar is said to have told the soldier: "Do not sell me for this — you can get a better price." The soldier, hoping for more, waited. No higher offer came. In a rage, the soldier killed him. In another version, Attar was simply enslaved and worked until death. Either way, the greatest Sufi poet of his generation perished in the same catastrophe that destroyed the civilization that had produced him — his death a final parable of the impermanence he had spent a lifetime illuminating.

Major Works

  • Mantiq al-Tayr (Conference of the Birds) — The supreme allegorical poem of the Sufi tradition: the journey of the birds through seven valleys to discover that they themselves are the divine they seek. Approximately 4,500 couplets interwoven with hundreds of parables, stories, and digressions, each illuminating a facet of the spiritual path.
  • Tadhkirat al-Awliya (Memorial of the Saints) — A prose hagiography of seventy-two Sufi masters from the earliest period through al-Hallaj, preserving their biographies, sayings, miracles, and teachings. The primary biographical source for dozens of early saints.
  • Ilahi-nama (Book of God) — A mystical poem framed as a dialogue between a king and his six sons, each representing a worldly attachment that must be transcended.
  • Musibat-nama (Book of Affliction) — An allegorical poem tracing the journey of the spirit (ruh) through forty stages of trial and purification.
  • Asrar-nama (Book of Secrets) — A shorter mystical poem on the nature of the soul's journey, famously gifted to the young Rumi.

Legacy

Rumi himself — the poet whom the Western world has made the best-selling poet in the English language — offered the most famous tribute to his predecessor: "Attar has traversed the seven cities of love / We are still at the bend of one alley." This extraordinary admission from the greatest mystical poet of all time reveals the depth of Attar's achievement and the reverence in which he was held by those most qualified to judge.

His Tadhkirat al-Awliya became the foundational biographical source for the early Sufi masters. Without Attar's painstaking collection and vivid retelling, the stories of Rabia al-Adawiyya, Ibrahim ibn Adham, Bayazid Bistami, Dhul-Nun al-Misri, Mansur al-Hallaj, and many others would survive only as scattered fragments in earlier, more technical compilations. It was Attar who gave these saints their narrative life — who made them characters in a great spiritual drama that continues to inspire. The Mantiq al-Tayr, meanwhile, has been translated into every major world language and adapted into drama, opera, visual art, and film. Its central insight — that the divine is not found in distant transcendence but in the annihilated self — remains as radical and as necessary as it was eight centuries ago.

Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi

Born17 Ramadan 560 AH / 28 July 1165 CE, Murcia, al-Andalus (present-day Spain)
Died22 Rabi' al-Thani 638 AH / 16 November 1240 CE, Damascus
Sufi OrderHis own universal chain; received initiations from multiple masters across the Islamic world
Titlesal-Shaykh al-Akbar (The Greatest Sheikh), Muhyiddin (Reviver of the Faith), Ibn Aflatun (Son of Plato — for his philosophical depth), al-Kibrit al-Ahmar (The Red Sulphur — the rarest and most precious substance in alchemy, signifying that which transforms everything it touches)
TeachersStudents of Abu Madyan in al-Andalus; Yusuf ibn Yakhlaf al-Kumi; the remarkable female saint Fatima bint Ibn al-Muthanna of Seville (who was in her nineties when he studied with her)

BurialDamascus — his shrine was honored by the Ottoman Sultan Selim I, who built a mosque and mausoleum over it in 1516 CE

Biography

Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi — al-Shaykh al-Akbar, the Greatest Sheikh — is the most monumental intellectual figure in the history of Islamic mysticism and arguably the most profound metaphysical thinker produced by any civilization. His writings transformed the entire landscape of Sufi thought so fundamentally that the tradition can be divided into "before Ibn Arabi" and "after Ibn Arabi." No serious Sufi thinker of the last eight centuries has been able to avoid engaging with his ideas — whether in passionate acceptance, creative development, or vigorous rejection. He was called al-Kibrit al-Ahmar — the Red Sulphur — because, like that legendary alchemical substance, he was infinitely rare and transformed everything he touched.

He was born on 17 Ramadan 560 AH (28 July 1165 CE) in Murcia, in the southeast of al-Andalus, into a distinguished Arab family of the Ta'i tribe — the same tribe as the legendary pre-Islamic poet Hatim al-Ta'i, renowned for his generosity. His father, Ali ibn Muhammad, was a man of some standing who served the Almohad rulers. The family moved to Seville when Ibn Arabi was a child, and it was in that glittering Andalusian capital — a city of poets, philosophers, and saints — that his extraordinary mind first opened.

As a young man, Ibn Arabi was so intellectually precocious that the great philosopher Abu al-Walid ibn Rushd (Averroes) — the supreme rationalist thinker of the medieval Islamic world, whose commentaries on Aristotle would later transform European philosophy — specifically requested a meeting with the young mystic. The encounter that followed became one of the most famous in all of Islamic intellectual history. Ibn Rushd greeted the youth with the word "Yes?" — meaning, "Have you found through spiritual unveiling what we philosophers find through rational demonstration?" Ibn Arabi replied: "Yes." For a moment, joy appeared on the philosopher's face — confirmation of reason's power. Then Ibn Arabi said: "No." Ibn Rushd's color changed and he trembled visibly. He asked the young man directly: "What have you found through unveiling and divine inspiration? Is it the same as what rational speculation gives us?" Ibn Arabi replied with devastating clarity: "Yes and no. Between the yes and the no, spirits fly from their matter and heads fly from their bodies." Ibn Rushd turned pale. In later years, Ibn Arabi reported that when he visited the philosopher again, Ibn Rushd said: "I spent my whole life pursuing knowledge through reason — and this young man has arrived at the same place through a completely different path." The philosopher wept. This encounter encapsulates the central claim of Ibn Arabi's entire project: mystical unveiling (kashf) reaches the same truths as rational demonstration (burhan), but reaches them experientially, directly, and lives within them — while the philosopher can only point at them from outside.

In al-Andalus and Morocco, Ibn Arabi studied under a remarkable array of teachers, but none left a deeper mark on him than Fatima bint Ibn al-Muthanna of Seville. She was in her nineties when the young Ibn Arabi came to study with her — a woman of extraordinary spiritual attainment whom he served as a disciple for two years. He built her a hut of reeds with his own hands. Of her he wrote: "She was one of the greatest spiritual masters I ever knew... In her company, I was as a little child sitting at the feet of a towering spiritual woman." He later referred to her as "one of my spiritual fathers" — using the masculine form deliberately to indicate that spiritual rank transcends gender. His openness to learning from women saints — he studied with several — was remarkable for his era and any era, and reflected his deep conviction that the divine manifests through all forms.

In 590 AH (1194 CE), Ibn Arabi departed al-Andalus, never to return. He embarked on a journey eastward that would take him through the breadth of the Islamic world — Tunis, Cairo, Mecca, Medina, Baghdad, Mosul, Malatya, Konya, and finally Damascus. In Mecca in 598 AH (1202 CE), two transformative experiences occurred. First, while performing tawaf (circumambulation) around the Ka'ba, he had a vision of a mysterious figure — al-fata al-dawwar, the Rotating Young Man — a spiritual being who appeared as a youth of extraordinary beauty circling the House of God. This figure commanded him to begin writing what would become the al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya (The Meccan Openings), the most vast and encyclopedic work on esoteric knowledge ever composed in any language. Second, in Mecca he met Nizam — the beautiful and learned daughter of a Persian scholar — whose grace and intelligence inspired the love poems of Tarjuman al-Ashwaq (The Interpreter of Desires). When critics accused him of writing profane love poetry, Ibn Arabi composed a second edition with a detailed spiritual commentary, demonstrating that every verse about earthly beauty was simultaneously a hymn to the divine Beauty that shines through all forms.

Also while in Mecca, Ibn Arabi received the most consequential dream-vision of his life: the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ appeared to him and handed him a book, saying: "This is the Fusus al-Hikam — take it out to people that they may benefit from it." Ibn Arabi woke and set about writing the work exactly as he had received it, claiming never to have revised a single word, considering the text to be divinely dictated. The Fusus al-Hikam (Bezels of Wisdom) — twenty-seven chapters, each devoted to the spiritual reality (fass — literally "bezel" or "seal-stone") of a prophet from Adam to Muhammad ﷺ — would become the most commented-upon, debated, and controversial work in the history of Sufi metaphysics.

Ibn Arabi settled permanently in Damascus in 620 AH (1223 CE), under the patronage of the Ibn Zaki family, and spent the last seventeen years of his life there — teaching, writing, and revising the ever-expanding Futuhat. His literary output defies comprehension: the Futuhat alone, in 560 chapters spanning every dimension of Islamic esoteric knowledge, may be the longest single work produced by any thinker in the medieval world, running to some two and a half million words. He died on 22 Rabi' al-Thani 638 AH (16 November 1240 CE) and was buried on Mount Qasiyun overlooking the city he had made his home.

Key Teachings

The doctrine most associated with Ibn Arabi — and the most frequently misunderstood — is Wahdat al-Wujud (the Unity of Being). Though Ibn Arabi himself never used this exact phrase as a technical term (it was formalized by his student Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi and later interpreters), it accurately captures his central metaphysical insight: there is, ultimately, only one Real existence (wujud), and that is God. All created things are not independent beings existing alongside God but rather "self-disclosures" (tajalliyyat) of the one Divine Reality. The world is God showing Himself to Himself through an infinity of forms. This is not pantheism — Ibn Arabi never said "everything is God" — but rather a far more subtle position: everything is a manifestation of God, yet God infinitely transcends everything He manifests. His formula is "Huwa/la Huwa" — "He/not He." Every created thing is simultaneously God-manifesting and not-God — a paradox that cannot be resolved by the rational mind but is lived in direct mystical experience. Multiplicity is real as the play of divine Names and Attributes, but it has no independent existence apart from the one Reality that sustains it.

Inseparable from this metaphysics is the concept of al-Insan al-Kamil — the Perfect Human. For Ibn Arabi, the human being occupies a unique station in the cosmos: the human is the isthmus (barzakh) between the infinite Divine and the finite world, the mirror in which God contemplates Himself with complete and total comprehension. The prophets and the greatest saints are the most perfect of these mirrors — through them, every divine Name finds its full expression. The concept of the Qutb (Pole) — the hidden saint who sustains the spiritual order of the cosmos in every age — is central to Ibn Arabi's cosmology. He also advanced the deeply controversial doctrine of the Khatm al-Wilaya al-Muhammadiyya — the Seal of Muhammadan Sainthood — a figure who bears the same relation to the hierarchy of saints as the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ bears to the line of prophets. His identification of this figure — which many interpreted as a claim about himself — generated controversy that continues to this day. Finally, Ibn Arabi's concept of the Imagination (khayal) as an ontological realm — not mere fantasy but the intermediate world (alam al-mithal) where spiritual realities take visible form and material realities reveal their spiritual essence — opened an entirely new dimension of Islamic thought. All mystical visions, the barzakh between this world and the next, dreams, and the forms seen by prophets and saints exist in this realm of Creative Imagination.

Famous Stories

The Meeting with Ibn Rushd: The encounter between the young mystic and the aged philosopher is one of the defining moments of Islamic intellectual history. Abu al-Walid ibn Rushd — Averroes — was the supreme champion of Aristotelian rationalism in the Islamic world. He had heard reports of this remarkable youth in Seville who was claiming spiritual experiences beyond the reach of philosophy, and he asked Ibn Arabi's father to arrange a meeting. When they sat together, Ibn Rushd opened with "Yes?" — and the conversation that followed shattered him. Ibn Arabi later recounted: "I saw that he was overtaken by a trembling, and his face changed color. He said: 'Yes' — confirming what I had said. Then I saw him move his lips and heard him whisper: 'Subhan Allah' — Glory be to God." Later, when Ibn Rushd was on his deathbed, and his coffin was loaded onto a donkey with his books piled on the other side for counterweight, Ibn Arabi witnessed the scene and composed the famous verse: "On one side the master, on the other his books — tell me, were his hopes at last fulfilled?" The philosopher's books balanced his corpse — but could they balance his soul?

The Female Teacher: Ibn Arabi's tribute to Fatima bint Ibn al-Muthanna of Seville is one of the most remarkable passages in Sufi literature. She was ninety-five years old when he first came to her; he was a young man still at the beginning of his path. He served her for two years, building her a reed hut with his own hands and attending to her needs. He wrote: "I served as a disciple to a woman of Seville named Fatima bint Ibn al-Muthanna. I served her for several years, she being in her nineties... She used to say: 'I marvel at anyone who claims to love God and yet is not overjoyed with Him.' When I built the reed hut for her, she looked at me and said: 'I am your spiritual mother.' And indeed she was — among the greatest spiritual figures I ever encountered." In an age when women's spiritual authority was often questioned, Ibn Arabi's frank acknowledgment of Fatima as one of his most important teachers — and his consistent recognition throughout his writings that women can attain the highest spiritual stations — represents a profound testament to his intellectual honesty and the universality of his vision.

The Dream Vision of the Fusus: In one of the most extraordinary claims in the history of religious literature, Ibn Arabi relates that in a dream in the year 627 AH (1230 CE), he saw the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ holding a book in his hand. The Prophet ﷺ said to him: "This is the Kitab Fusus al-Hikam — take it and bring it out to the people, that they may benefit from it." Ibn Arabi said: "I received it from his hand and resolved to carry out his wish. I set about writing this book exactly as the Messenger of God ﷺ delimited it for me, without adding or subtracting a single thing." Whether one regards this claim as literal truth, visionary experience, or literary convention, the Fusus al-Hikam that resulted — twenty-seven dense, luminous chapters exploring the spiritual essence of each prophet from Adam through Muhammad ﷺ — became the most studied, commented upon, and fiercely debated work in the Sufi canon. More commentaries have been written on the Fusus than on virtually any other Islamic text besides the Quran itself.

His Tomb's Recovery: After Ibn Arabi's death, his tomb on Mount Qasiyun in Damascus was honored for generations. But during the later Mamluk period, hostility from scholars who condemned his writings as heretical led to the neglect and near-disappearance of the burial site. Centuries later, when the Ottoman Sultan Selim I conquered Syria in 922 AH (1516 CE), he had a remarkable vision: Ibn Arabi appeared to him in a dream and guided him to the location of his forgotten grave. Selim ordered the site excavated, and the tomb was indeed found where the vision had indicated. The sultan — one of the most powerful men in the world — then commissioned the construction of a magnificent mosque, mausoleum, and charitable complex over the grave of the Andalusian mystic who had died nearly three centuries earlier. The shrine stands to this day, a testament to the enduring power of Ibn Arabi's spiritual presence — an Ottoman sultan from Anatolia honoring a Murci from al-Andalus buried in Damascus, the threads of an entire civilization woven together at a single grave.

Major Works

  • al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya (The Meccan Openings) — 560 chapters encompassing virtually every dimension of Islamic esoteric knowledge: cosmology, angelology, prophetology, the science of letters, the spiritual meaning of worship, the stations of the saints, the nature of time, the structure of the cosmos. Begun in Mecca in 598 AH and revised throughout Ibn Arabi's life, it may be the longest and most comprehensive work ever composed by a single author in the medieval world.
  • Fusus al-Hikam (Bezels of Wisdom) — 27 chapters, each named after a prophet (from "The Bezel of Divine Wisdom in the Word of Adam" to "The Bezel of Singular Wisdom in the Word of Muhammad"), exploring the unique spiritual reality that each prophet embodies. The most commented-upon work in the Sufi tradition.
  • Tarjuman al-Ashwaq (The Interpreter of Desires) — A collection of love poems inspired by Nizam, the daughter of a Persian scholar in Mecca, later given a detailed spiritual commentary by Ibn Arabi himself demonstrating the mystical meaning behind each verse of earthly longing.

Legacy

Ibn Arabi is the most debated figure in Islamic intellectual history. His admirers — who include the vast majority of Sufi masters from every order — call him al-Shaykh al-Akbar, the Greatest Sheikh, and regard his writings as second only to the Quran and hadith in spiritual authority. His critics — from certain Hanbali scholars to various modern movements — call him al-Shaykh al-Akfar (the Most Heretical Sheikh), accusing his doctrine of Wahdat al-Wujud of pantheism and his claim to the Seal of Sainthood of spiritual arrogance. This debate has raged for eight centuries without resolution, a testament to the provocative depth of his thought. The great scholar Ibn Taymiyya condemned him; the great scholar al-Sha'rani defended him. The Naqshbandi master Ahmad Sirhindi sought to "correct" his metaphysics with the rival doctrine of Wahdat al-Shuhud (Unity of Witnessing); the Shadhili masters embraced him as the perfection of gnosis.

His influence on all subsequent Sufism is incalculable — virtually every Sufi thinker after him either built upon, developed, modified, or reacted against his ideas, but none could ignore them. His concept of al-Insan al-Kamil shaped the Mevlevi, Naqshbandi, Shadhili, and every other major tradition. His metaphysics of Wahdat al-Wujud became the dominant — though never uncontested — framework of Sufi cosmology. Through Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi, his ideas entered into dialogue with the philosophical tradition of Mulla Sadra and Illuminationist philosophy. In the modern world, his works have been translated into every major European language, studied at universities from Tokyo to Toronto, and engaged with by thinkers as diverse as Toshihiko Izutsu, Henry Corbin, and William Chittick. He has been called not only the greatest Islamic mystic but one of the greatest mystics of any tradition — a mind of such scope and depth that, like the ocean to which his admirers perpetually compare him, one can sail upon it for a lifetime and never sight the far shore.

Chapter 38

Ruzbihan Baqli

روزبهان بقلي
Full NameAbu Muhammad Ruzbihan ibn Abi al-Nasr Baqli al-Shirazi
Born522 AH / 1128 CE, Fasa (Fars province, Iran)
Died606 AH / 1209 CE, Shiraz (Iran)
Sufi OrderHis own tradition rooted in the Junaidi lineage
TitlesShaykh-i Shattah (Master of Ecstatic Utterances), "The Jasmine of the Mystics," "The Drunken One of Shiraz"
TeachersJamal al-Din Abu al-Wafa' (his primary shaykh)
Notable StudentsHis son Shams al-Din
Major WorksSharh-i Shathiyyat (Commentary on Ecstatic Utterances), Abhar al-Ashiqin (Jasmine of the Lovers), Kashf al-Asrar (The Unveiling of Secrets), Ara'is al-Bayan (Brides of Exposition)

Biography

Ruzbihan Baqli was born in the town of Fasa in the Fars province of Iran, but it was the luminous city of Shiraz that claimed him as its own. He settled there early in life and spent virtually his entire existence within its walls, transforming Shiraz into a beacon of mystical knowledge. His spiritual awakening came at the age of fifteen, when he encountered a wandering dervish who offered him a piece of bread. As Ruzbihan ate, he felt the blessing of divine love flood through him — the eye of his heart opened, and from that moment he began to perceive realities hidden from ordinary sight. It was an initiation as simple as bread and as vast as the heavens.

Even in his youth, Ruzbihan experienced what he described as direct visions of God, reporting that he saw the divine reality "like the sun in a cloudless sky." These were not isolated events but the beginning of a lifelong current of mystical perception that would distinguish him from nearly every other figure in Sufi history. Under the guidance of his shaykh Jamal al-Din Abu al-Wafa', Ruzbihan refined his spiritual capacities, learning to navigate the overwhelming ocean of divine disclosure while maintaining the scholarly precision that would characterize his written works. He established a major khanqah in Shiraz that became one of the great centers of Sufi training in Persia, where hundreds of disciples gathered to learn from the master whose inner life was an unceasing conversation with the unseen.

What sets Ruzbihan apart from all other Sufi masters is his extraordinary spiritual diary, the Kashf al-Asrar (Unveiling of Secrets). Over the course of decades, he meticulously recorded his inner experiences — over one hundred thousand visions of the Prophet Muhammad, of divine light, of angels, of the celestial throne, and of God Himself. No other Sufi in the entire history of Islamic mysticism left such a detailed, firsthand account of the interior spiritual life sustained over an entire lifetime. Where other masters spoke in generalities about the stations and states of the path, Ruzbihan provided a day-by-day map of what the journey actually looks like from the inside — an achievement of both spiritual depth and literary courage.

His intellectual legacy is equally remarkable. His Sharh-i Shathiyyat (Commentary on Ecstatic Utterances) is the definitive scholarly defense of the controversial declarations made by earlier masters such as al-Hallaj and Bayazid al-Bistami. Rather than dismissing these utterances as heresy or defending them with mere apologetics, Ruzbihan subjected each one to rigorous theological and mystical analysis, demonstrating that when understood in their proper spiritual context, these statements are not blasphemy but the inevitable overflow of a consciousness annihilated in God. His doctrine of "witnessing divine beauty in created forms" — the teaching that God's beauty can be perceived in beautiful faces, in nature, and in the sensory world — made him controversial among the orthodox but profoundly influential upon the entire tradition of Persian mystical poetry.

Ruzbihan lived to approximately eighty years of age, spending his final decades as the undisputed spiritual authority of Shiraz. When he died in 606 AH / 1209 CE, the city lost not merely a scholar but a living window into the divine. His tomb in Shiraz became a place of pilgrimage, and the fragrance of his legacy — intellectual, spiritual, and poetic — has continued to perfume the tradition of Islamic mysticism down to the present day.

Key Teachings

Ruzbihan's central contribution to Sufi thought is his defense and systematic interpretation of the ecstatic utterances (shathiyyat). He taught that statements like al-Hallaj's "Ana al-Haqq" (I am the Truth) and Bayazid's "Subhani" (Glory be to Me) are not the words of men claiming divinity but the words of God Himself, spoken through vessels whose individual consciousness has been completely annihilated in the state of fana'. The mystic who utters such words is not a blasphemer but a trumpet through which the divine breath blows — when the self has been utterly consumed, what remains is not the servant speaking but the Lord declaring Himself through an emptied vessel. This framework transformed the shathiyyat from apparent heresies into windows onto the highest spiritual truths, providing future generations with a way to honor both the law and the mystical experience without sacrificing either.

His second great teaching concerns the path of divine beauty (jamal). Ruzbihan taught that the perception of earthly beauty — in human faces, in nature, in music and art — can serve as a legitimate vehicle for encountering the beauty of God, provided the lover's heart is purified and the vision is directed upward toward its divine source. This "witnessing" (shuhud) is not indulgence or idolatry but a form of sacred contemplation, for all beauty in creation is a reflection of the one Beauty that has no limit. This doctrine profoundly shaped the Persian poetic tradition, providing the theological foundation for the language of earthly love as a metaphor — and more than metaphor — for divine love that runs through the works of Rumi, Hafiz, and countless others.

Famous Stories

The Diary of Visions: Ruzbihan's Kashf al-Asrar opens with a passage that has astonished scholars for centuries: "When I was fifteen, I met a wandering dervish who gave me a piece of bread. The taste of that bread opened the eye of my heart, and from that moment I began to see what others cannot see." What follows is not a treatise or a theological argument but a decades-long record of daily mystical experience — visions of the Prophet, encounters with angels, immersions in divine light, and direct perceptions of the divine majesty. He records: "I saw the divine majesty appear before me like a sea without shore, and within it I saw all created things as foam upon the surface." These visions came to him not in retreat or seclusion but in the midst of ordinary life — while teaching, while walking through the markets of Shiraz, while eating his evening meal. The diary stands as the most intimate and sustained record of mystical experience in Islamic history, a testament to a life lived simultaneously in this world and in the presence of God.

The Defense of al-Hallaj: When critics confronted Ruzbihan and demanded to know how he could defend al-Hallaj's infamous declaration "Ana al-Haqq" — a statement for which al-Hallaj had been executed — Ruzbihan replied with devastating clarity: "You heard the words of a man. I heard the words of God. Al-Hallaj had been so completely consumed that nothing of him remained — what you heard was not al-Hallaj speaking but the Truth declaring itself through the emptied vessel." He then proceeded, in his Sharh-i Shathiyyat, to interpret every famous ecstatic utterance in Sufi history with the same systematic rigor, showing that each was the natural consequence of the highest spiritual states. Through this work, Ruzbihan accomplished what no one before him had managed: he rehabilitated the ecstatic tradition within the framework of orthodox scholarship, honoring the law and the mystics simultaneously.

The Jasmine Vision: It is said that when Ruzbihan died, the entire city of Shiraz was filled with the scent of jasmine, though it was not the season for the flower to bloom. The fragrance was so pervasive and unmistakable that people emerged from their homes in wonder, and those who understood knew that the great master had departed. Disciples gathered from across Persia to attend his funeral, many weeping that the source of spiritual fragrance had left the world — hence the title by which he is remembered: "the Jasmine of the Mystics." The story speaks to what Ruzbihan's students believed about him: that his entire life had been a kind of flowering, and that even in death, the sweetness of his presence could not be contained.

Major Works

  • Kashf al-Asrar (Unveiling of Secrets): His remarkable spiritual autobiography and diary — a unique document in Islamic mystical literature, recording over one hundred thousand visions experienced across decades of daily mystical life. No comparable work exists in the Sufi tradition.
  • Sharh-i Shathiyyat (Commentary on Ecstatic Utterances): The definitive scholarly defense and systematic interpretation of the great ecstatic declarations in Sufi history, particularly those of al-Hallaj and Bayazid al-Bistami. This work provided the intellectual framework through which future generations could understand and honor the ecstatic tradition.
  • Abhar al-Ashiqin (Jasmine of the Lovers): A treatise on divine love and its stages, combining philosophical depth with lyrical beauty. The work maps the journey of the lover through the stations of desire, longing, intimacy, and union with breathtaking precision and tenderness.
  • Ara'is al-Bayan (Brides of Exposition): A massive twenty-volume mystical commentary on the entire Quran, revealing the inner dimensions of every verse through the lens of direct spiritual experience. It stands among the most ambitious works of Sufi exegesis ever composed.

Legacy

Ruzbihan Baqli represents the sophisticated middle path between the ecstatic utterance and the scholarly defense — neither suppressing the raw power of mystical experience nor abandoning it to the accusations of the literalists. In his person and his writings, the wild cry of al-Hallaj and the careful reasoning of the theologian found their reconciliation. His Kashf al-Asrar provides an unparalleled window into the daily inner life of a great mystic over the span of decades, offering future generations of Sufis not abstract theory but a detailed map of what the spiritual path actually looks like from the inside — the visions, the terrors, the consolations, and the overwhelming beauty that characterize the soul's journey to God.

His doctrine of witnessing divine beauty in created forms reverberated far beyond the walls of his khanqah, influencing the entire tradition of Persian love poetry and the mystical symbolism of the beloved (ma'shuq) and the "idol" (but/sanam) as reflections of divine beauty. The great poets of Shiraz who came after him — above all Hafiz, who lived in the same city two centuries later — inherited a spiritual atmosphere that Ruzbihan had decisively shaped. His works were preserved and studied primarily in Shiraz and across Iran, but in recent decades they have attracted increasing attention from Western scholars such as Paul Nwyia and Carl Ernst, who have recognized in Ruzbihan one of the most original and daring minds in the history of Islamic mysticism — a man who lived his entire life at the intersection of the visible and the invisible, and who had the courage to write down what he saw.

Moinuddin Chishti

معين الدین چشتی

Born536 AH / 1141 CE, Sijistan (Sistan, eastern Iran) — some sources say Chisht (Afghanistan)
Died6 Rajab 633 AH / 1236 CE, Ajmer (Rajasthan, India)
Sufi OrderChishtiyya — received from Uthman Harwani, traced through Mawdud Chishti to the town of Chisht in Afghanistan
TitlesKhwaja Gharib Nawaz (Benefactor of the Poor), Sultan al-Hind (Sultan of India), Ata-e Rasul (Gift of the Prophet)
TeachersUthman Harwani (primary shaykh — twenty years of companionship); also connected to the lineage of Yusuf al-Hamadhani
Notable StudentsQutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki (his primary spiritual successor)
Major WorksNo major written works survive — his legacy is embodied in the Chishtiyya order, oral tradition, and recorded sayings
BurialAjmer, Rajasthan, India — Dargah Ajmer Sharif (Dargah Khwaja Sahib), visited by millions annually from all faiths

Biography

Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti was born in Sijistan in the eastern reaches of the Iranian world, into a family of learning and piety. Orphaned at a young age, the course of his life was transformed by an encounter that reads like a parable: a wandering dervish named Ibrahim Qandozi appeared before the young boy, offered him some grapes, and touched his head. From that moment, Moinuddin abandoned everything — inheritance, comfort, worldly ambition — and devoted himself entirely to the spiritual path. It was the beginning of a journey that would carry him across the entire breadth of the Islamic world and culminate in the spiritual transformation of an entire subcontinent.

He pursued his education in Samarkand and Bukhara, the great centers of Islamic learning, then traveled westward to Nishapur, where he encountered the masters of the Kubrawiyya tradition. But it was in Uthman Harwani that he found his true teacher. For approximately twenty years, Moinuddin remained in his master's company, traveling with him across Central Asia, through Baghdad, and onward to the holy cities of Mecca, Medina, and Jerusalem. Under Harwani's rigorous guidance, Moinuddin was forged into a vessel capable of carrying an extraordinary spiritual mission. The nature of that mission was revealed to him in Medina, near the tomb of the Prophet Muhammad. In a state of deep spiritual communion, he received a direct command: "O Moinuddin, you are the support of Our faith. Go to India, for darkness has covered that land and your light is needed there." Some accounts describe this as a vision of the Prophet himself. Moinuddin did not hesitate — he set out immediately for the subcontinent, stopping at no earthly authority for permission.

He arrived in Ajmer, in Rajasthan, during the reign of the Hindu Rajput king Prithviraj Chauhan. The encounter between the wandering dervish and the powerful monarch set the tone for everything that would follow. Tradition holds that when Moinuddin and his small party camped near the royal lake, the king's servants ordered them away — the lake was reserved for the king's horses. What followed was one of the most famous episodes in Indian Sufi hagiography: the king's finest horses found themselves unable to rise from the ground until the dervish was shown respect. But it was not through displays of power that Moinuddin conquered hearts. It was through a ministry of absolute, radical openness — welcoming people of all castes, all faiths, and all backgrounds with equal love and equal dignity. His most famous principle distills his entire approach: "Love all, hate none. Mercy for all, malice toward none."

For the remainder of his long life — he lived to approximately ninety-seven years of age — Moinuddin made Ajmer his home, transforming the city through his spiritual presence, his charitable work, and the example of a life lived entirely in the service of others. Thousands embraced Islam not through his preaching but through his practice — through witnessing a man who fed the hungry without asking their religion, who comforted the grieving without inquiring about their caste, and who treated every human being as a vessel of divine dignity. He maintained a langar (free kitchen) that fed thousands daily, establishing a tradition of open-handed generosity that defines the Chishtiyya order to this day.

He died on 6 Rajab 633 AH / 1236 CE, having spent his final days in seclusion, asking not to be disturbed. When his room was at last opened, he was found in a state of perfect peace, his face illuminated with light. According to tradition, the words "He was a beloved of God" were found inscribed upon his forehead. He was buried in Ajmer, where his tomb — the Dargah Ajmer Sharif — became one of the most visited sacred sites in the world, drawing millions of pilgrims annually from every faith and every walk of life.

Key Teachings

The Chishtiyya path as established by Moinuddin Chishti is distinguished by five inseparable principles that together constitute a complete way of life: absolute focus on the love (mahabbah) of God and of all creation as the foundation of everything; the embrace of music and spiritual audition (sama') as a primary form of worship and a doorway to divine presence; radical openness to all people regardless of their religious background, caste, or social standing; firm rejection of political connections and worldly power, keeping the spiritual path uncontaminated by the compromises of governance; and above all, service of the poor as the highest form of worship. These were not abstract ideals but lived realities — Moinuddin embodied each one with a consistency that made his life itself his most persuasive argument.

His most celebrated saying captures the essence of his teaching: "Of all the worship, the noblest is to give comfort to the heart of a broken person." For Moinuddin, the spiritual path was not primarily about visions, states, or metaphysical knowledge — it was about the transformation of the heart into an instrument of mercy. "The river does not stop flowing because some drink from it," he taught. "A saint's generosity is like a river." This emphasis on love, service, and unconditional welcome created a spiritual tradition that proved uniquely suited to the diverse, multi-religious landscape of the Indian subcontinent, where the Chishtiyya order flourished precisely because it met people where they were rather than demanding that they first become something else.

Famous Stories

The Divine Commission: Before departing for India, Moinuddin was in Medina near the blessed tomb of the Prophet Muhammad when he entered a state of profound spiritual communion. In that state, he received the command that would define his life's work: "O Moinuddin, you are the support of Our faith. Go to India, for darkness has covered that land and your light is needed there." Some accounts describe this as a direct vision of the Prophet himself, radiant and unmistakable. Moinuddin did not consult scholars, did not seek the patronage of kings, did not wait for favorable circumstances. He set out immediately for a land he had never seen, carrying nothing but his trust in the One who had sent him. The story encapsulates the Chishtiyya spirit: when the divine command comes, obedience is instant and total, and no worldly consideration can stand in the way.

The Miracle of the Horses: When Moinuddin first arrived in Ajmer, he and his small party of followers set up camp near the royal lake. King Prithviraj's servants descended upon them with contempt — this was reserved for the king's horses, and common dervishes had no right to be there. Moinuddin said simply, "We will move when God wills." The next morning, the king's magnificent horses — dozens of the finest animals in all of India — found themselves unable to rise from the ground. They lay on their sides, immovable, and no veterinarian or horseman could explain it. A courtier suggested that the affliction might be connected to the foreign dervish who had been turned away from the lake. The king sent an envoy with apologies and gifts, begging the holy man to allow the horses to recover. Moinuddin said: "Rise, by the permission of God" — and every horse stood immediately. The story, whether taken literally or as a parable, speaks to the central Chishtiyya conviction: spiritual authority is not diminished by outward poverty, and the power of a saint who relies on God alone surpasses the power of kings.

The Open Door: Moinuddin is said to have established a rule from which he never wavered: no one who came to his door hungry was ever turned away, regardless of faith, caste, or station. He maintained a langar — a free kitchen — that fed thousands daily, and the doors of his khanqah were never closed. When asked how he could possibly afford such generosity, he replied: "I give what I have. When what I have is gone, God provides more. He has never failed me yet." This was not a metaphor — the langar operated every day of his long life in Ajmer, and the tradition continues at his dargah and at every Chishtiyya shrine across the Indian subcontinent to this day. The open kitchen became the physical embodiment of the Chishtiyya teaching: that the door of mercy must never be closed, and that feeding a hungry person is a form of worship that surpasses years of prayer.

Major Works

  • Recorded Sayings and Teachings: Moinuddin Chishti did not leave behind written treatises in the manner of scholars. His legacy is preserved in the oral tradition of the Chishtiyya order, in collections of his sayings (malfuzat) recorded by his disciples, and above all in the living practice of the order he founded. His most famous statements — "Love all, hate none," "Of all the worship, the noblest is to give comfort to the heart of a broken person," and "The river does not stop flowing because some drink from it" — have been transmitted across eight centuries with the force of scripture.
  • The Chishtiyya Order Itself: His greatest "work" is the spiritual lineage he established. Through his primary successor Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki, the chain of transmission continued unbroken, producing the greatest saints of the Indian subcontinent in an uninterrupted succession of spiritual mastery.

Legacy

Moinuddin Chishti is the single most important figure in the spread of Islam across the Indian subcontinent — and his method was not conquest, coercion, or theological argument but love, service, and spiritual example. In a land of extraordinary religious diversity, he demonstrated that the mystical heart of Islam could speak to all people without demanding that they first abandon what they were. His Urs (death anniversary) in Ajmer draws millions of pilgrims annually — Muslims, Hindus, Sikhs, Christians, and people of no particular faith at all — making it one of the largest interfaith religious gatherings in the world. The Dargah Ajmer Sharif stands as a living monument to his vision: a place where the doors are never closed and where the only qualification for entry is the need of the human heart.

The Chishtiyya order he established became the dominant Sufi tradition in South Asia, producing a lineage of saints whose names are woven into the spiritual fabric of the subcontinent: Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki in Delhi, Baba Farid in Punjab, Nizamuddin Auliya in Delhi, and the poet-mystic Amir Khusraw, among dozens of others. His emphasis on music and sama' as a vehicle for divine encounter gave birth to the qawwali tradition — the devotional music that has shaped the entire cultural heritage of South Asia and has become known worldwide through artists such as Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan. Eight centuries after his death, the langar still operates at his shrine, the music still plays, and the doors remain open — a testament to the enduring power of a man who believed that the highest worship is to comfort a broken heart.

Bahauddin Zakariya Multani

بهاء الدين زكريا الملتاني
Full NameAbu Muhammad Baha al-Din Zakariya ibn Kamal al-Din al-Qurashi al-Multani
Born578 AH / 1182 CE, Kot Karor (near Multan, present-day Pakistan)
Died7 Safar 661 AH / 1262 CE, Multan
Sufi OrderSuhrawardiyya (received directly from Shihabuddin Umar al-Suhrawardi in Baghdad)
TitlesShaykh al-Islam, Baha al-Haqq wa al-Din (Splendor of Truth and Faith), al-Ghawth (The Helper)
TeachersShihabuddin Umar al-Suhrawardi (Baghdad); also studied in Medina, Jerusalem, and Bukhara
Notable StudentsHis son Sadr al-Din Arif, Fakhr al-Din Iraqi (the mystic poet)
Major WorksOral teachings transmitted through his khanqah; discourses compiled by students
BurialMultan, Pakistan — Darbar Baha al-Din Zakariya, a major shrine visited by thousands daily

Biography

Bahauddin Zakariya was born near Multan into a family of Qurashi lineage. He displayed extraordinary intellectual gifts from an early age, and his thirst for knowledge drew him far beyond the frontiers of his homeland. As a young man, he embarked on a sweeping journey across the great centers of Islamic learning — studying first in Bukhara, the jewel of Central Asian scholarship, then traveling to Medina to sit at the feet of the scholars of the Prophet's city, and eventually arriving in Baghdad, the seat of the Abbasid caliphate and the beating heart of the Sunni scholarly world.

It was in Baghdad that the trajectory of his life was sealed. There he met the great Shaykh Shihabuddin Umar al-Suhrawardi — the master who had systematized the Suhrawardiyya order and authored the Awarif al-Ma'arif, the definitive manual of institutional Sufism. Shihabuddin recognized in the young man from Multan an extraordinary spiritual capacity. He initiated him into the Suhrawardiyya, investing him with the khirqa — the cloak of spiritual succession — and commissioned him to return to his homeland as the order's representative in the Indian subcontinent. On his return journey, Bahauddin also spent time in Jerusalem, absorbing the spiritual atmosphere of al-Aqsa.

Arriving back in Multan, Bahauddin established what would become one of the most influential khanqahs in the history of South Asian Islam. Multan was already known as Madinat al-Awliya — the City of Saints — and Bahauddin's towering presence cemented this reputation beyond dispute. His approach to Sufism was distinctively Suhrawardi: he accepted gifts from the sultanate, owned property, maintained a large and well-appointed lodge, and engaged directly with rulers and wealthy patrons. He saw this worldly involvement not as a compromise but as a legitimate and even necessary method of channeling resources toward the welfare of the community and the propagation of the faith.

This model stood in vivid contrast to the Chishti ideal embodied by his great contemporary, Fariduddin Ganjshakar — Baba Farid — who lived in severe material poverty in Pakpattan. The two saints of Punjab knew each other and maintained a complex but profoundly respectful relationship. Together, they represented two complementary poles of Sufi spirituality in the subcontinent: Bahauddin, the model of inner detachment amid outward prosperity; Baba Farid, the model of inner and outer renunciation alike. Their contrasting paths would define the two great streams of Sufi life in the Punjab for centuries to come.

Bahauddin's spiritual authority was so immense that it transcended even the political chaos of the age. When Mongol armies threatened the region, his intercession is credited with protecting Multan from the worst of the devastation. His khanqah attracted seekers from across the Muslim world, among them the great Persian mystic poet Fakhr al-Din Iraqi, who spent twenty-five years in the master's company and composed his luminous Lama'at (Divine Flashes) under the spiritual influence of that atmosphere.

Key Teachings

Bahauddin Zakariya taught what might be called the Suhrawardi path of engaged spirituality. The Sufi, in his view, should not withdraw from the world but should transform it from within. Accepting wealth and political connections is permissible — even commendable — so long as the heart remains entirely detached from them. True zuhd (renunciation) is an interior state, not an outward performance of poverty. A man who wears silk while his heart is with God is closer to the truth than a man who wears wool while his heart craves the admiration of others. This teaching provided the theological foundation for a model of Sufism deeply interwoven with social, economic, and political life.

Equally central to his teaching was the insistence on combining strict adherence to the Sharia with deep spiritual realization. The exoteric and esoteric dimensions of Islam must be unified in a single life. A Sufi who neglects jurisprudence is as dangerously incomplete as a jurist who neglects the purification of the heart. This dual emphasis — worldly engagement grounded in law, and spiritual depth grounded in love — became the hallmark of the Suhrawardiyya in the subcontinent.

Famous Stories

The Baghdad Initiation: When Bahauddin arrived in Baghdad as a young scholar and presented himself before Shihabuddin Umar al-Suhrawardi, the great shaykh looked at him for a long time in silence. Then he said: "Welcome, O ocean of light that has come from Multan." He immediately initiated him without the customary years of service, saying: "What would take others a decade, God has already accomplished in you." He dressed him in the khirqa and sent him back to Multan with the words: "Go — your city awaits you, and I await news of your greatness."

The Contrast with Baba Farid: When Baba Farid and Bahauddin Zakariya met, observers were struck by the contrast between them: Baba Farid in his patched wool garments, Bahauddin in fine clothing. Someone asked Baba Farid whether this disparity troubled him. He replied: "Our paths are different, not our destinations. He has removed the world from his heart; I have removed it from my hands. Both methods are valid for those whom God has guided to them." The two saints embraced, and their followers have coexisted harmoniously in the Punjab ever since.

The Mongol Encounter: When news reached Multan that Mongol armies were approaching, the citizens fell into panic. Bahauddin told them to remain calm and retired to prayer. The Mongol general, upon arriving at the outskirts of the city, is said to have seen a vision of an old man of terrifying spiritual authority who warned him not to harm the inhabitants. The general sent a message to Bahauddin acknowledging his power, and Multan was largely spared.

Major Works

  • Oral Teachings and Discourses: Bahauddin's teachings were primarily transmitted orally through the daily life of his khanqah. His discourses were recorded and compiled by students, though no single major treatise is attributed directly to him. His method was the living word, the direct transmission from master to disciple.
  • The Lama'at Connection: While not his own composition, the spiritual atmosphere of his khanqah directly produced Fakhr al-Din Iraqi's Lama'at (Divine Flashes) — one of the greatest Sufi prose-poems ever written, a distillation of Ibn Arabi's metaphysics rendered in exquisite Persian prose, composed in Multan under the master's spiritual influence.

Legacy

Bahauddin Zakariya established the Suhrawardiyya as the dominant Sufi presence in Multan and the wider Sindh-Punjab region, creating an institutional model that endured for centuries. His tomb in Multan — surviving over 750 years of history, including invasions, conquests, and the passage of empires — remains one of Pakistan's most important religious sites, drawing pilgrims from across the subcontinent. His model of "engaged Sufism" — spirituality combined with worldly involvement, inner poverty amid outer prosperity — has been enormously influential in South Asian Islam, providing a powerful counterbalance to the Chishti ideal of absolute material renunciation.

Through his disciple Fakhr al-Din Iraqi, his spiritual inheritance flowed into the great stream of Persian mystical literature. Through his son and successor Sadr al-Din Arif, the Suhrawardi silsila continued in Multan and branched across the subcontinent. Bahauddin Zakariya remains the living proof that the Sufi path admits many mansions — that the saint in the palace and the saint in the desert may both be drinking from the same spring.

Jalal al-Din Rumi

جلال الدين الرومي
Full NameMawlana Jalal al-Din Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Husayn al-Balkhi, known as Rumi ("from Rum/Anatolia")
Born6 Rabi' al-Awwal 604 AH / 30 September 1207 CE, Balkh (present-day Afghanistan)
Died5 Jumada al-Thani 672 AH / 17 December 1273 CE, Konya (Anatolia, present-day Turkey)

TitlesMawlana (Our Master), Khodawandgar (Lord and Master), Molla Khudavendigar
TeachersHis father Baha al-Din Walad; Sayyid Burhanuddin Muhaqqiq al-Tirmidhi (nine years); Shams al-Din Tabrizi — the transformative encounter of 642 AH / 1244 CE
Notable StudentsHusam al-Din Chalabi (scribe and inspiration for the Masnavi), his son Sultan Walad (organizer of the Mevlevi order), Salahaddin Zarkub

BurialKonya, Turkey — Mevlana Museum (his tekke and tomb), UNESCO World Heritage Site, one of the most visited pilgrimage sites in the world

Biography

Jalal al-Din Muhammad was born in 604 AH / 1207 CE in the great city of Balkh, in the eastern reaches of Khorasan — then one of the most illustrious centers of Islamic civilization. His father, Baha al-Din Walad, was a distinguished scholar and preacher whose theological disputes with the philosopher Fakhr al-Din Razi had made his position in Balkh untenable. The family departed on a long westward migration that would take them through Nishapur — where, according to tradition, the aged Farid al-Din Attar met the young Rumi and, recognizing the fire within him, presented him with a copy of his Asrar-nama (Book of Secrets) — through Baghdad, to Mecca for the pilgrimage, and eventually to Anatolia, settling in Konya around 626 AH / 1228 CE under the patronage of the Seljuk Sultan Ala al-Din Kayqubad. They had fled Balkh just before the Mongol cataclysm that would annihilate Central Asia; the family's departure saved them from the destruction that consumed everything they had known.

Baha al-Din Walad died when Rumi was twenty-four, leaving him both a scholarly inheritance and an unfinished spiritual education. For nine years, Rumi studied under his father's former disciple, Sayyid Burhanuddin Muhaqqiq al-Tirmidhi, who guided him through the deeper dimensions of his father's teachings and sent him to Aleppo and Damascus to complete his training in jurisprudence and the Islamic sciences. By his late thirties, Rumi was an eminent and respected scholar in Konya — teaching Hanafi fiqh and theology, surrounded by hundreds of students, admired by the Seljuk court. He was learned, settled, prosperous. But he had not yet undergone the experience that would shatter the scholar and birth the poet.

On 26 Jumada al-Akhira 642 AH (November 1244 CE), a wandering dervish named Shams al-Din Muhammad Tabrizi arrived in Konya. The meeting between Rumi and Shams is the most celebrated encounter in the history of Sufism. The details are disputed — in one version, Shams seized the bridle of Rumi's horse in the marketplace and posed him a devastating question; in another, he threw Rumi's books into a pool of water, saying: "You are drowning in knowledge — it is time for you to live." In the most famous account, Shams asked: "Who was greater — the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ or Bayazid Bistami?" Rumi replied without hesitation: "The Prophet ﷺ." Shams pressed: "Then why did the Prophet say, 'We have not known You as You truly deserve to be known,' while Bayazid declared, 'Glory be to Me, how great is My majesty'?" At this, Rumi fell unconscious. When he awoke, the scholar was dead and the lover was born. He abandoned his lectures, withdrew from his students, and devoted himself entirely to Shams. For months, the two were inseparable — locked together in a cell of spiritual conversation (sohbet) that transformed them both.

Rumi's students, deprived of their master and consumed by jealousy, drove Shams away. He fled to Damascus. Rumi, devastated, sent his son Sultan Walad to bring him back, and Shams returned briefly. But the disciples' hostility only intensified, and in 645 AH / 1248 CE, Shams disappeared for good — almost certainly murdered, perhaps with the involvement of Rumi's own son Alauddin. Rumi's grief was oceanic. He traveled twice to Damascus searching for Shams, wandering through the streets calling his name. And then — at the far shore of his despair — the transformation completed itself. Rumi realized that Shams was not gone but had become inseparable from his own being. "Why do I seek him outside? He lives within me — I am he!" The poems that poured forth were not written about Shams but as Shams — the entire Diwan bears Shams's name as its author, for Rumi had annihilated himself in his beloved. He found another mirror of the divine friend in the simple goldsmith Salahaddin Zarkub; one day, passing through the goldsmiths' bazaar in Konya, the rhythmic hammering of gold on anvil seized him and he began to turn — slowly, then faster — in the spontaneous movement that would become the sema of the Mevlevi order.

In the final decades of his life, Rumi composed his supreme masterwork. His beloved disciple Husam al-Din Chalabi one day showed him some verses he had written in the style of Sanai and Attar and said: "O Master, if you were to compose something in this manner, your disciples would have a treasure to study for generations." Rumi reached into the folds of his turban and produced eighteen verses — the opening of the Masnavi, the immortal "Song of the Reed": "Listen to the reed, how it tells its tale, crying of the pain of separation..." Husam wept, pledged his life to the work, and for the remaining years became Rumi's scribe — Rumi dictating in states of ecstasy while walking, while whirling, while in the bath, at any hour of day or night, and Husam writing every word, reading it back for correction, preparing it for circulation. The result was 25,700 verses across six books — the Masnavi-yi Ma'navi, universally acknowledged as the greatest single work of mystical literature in any language. Rumi died in Konya on 5 Jumada al-Thani 672 AH / 17 December 1273 CE. His funeral was attended by Muslims, Christians, and Jews alike, each community claiming him as their own. The night of his death is celebrated by the Mevlevi order as the Shab-i Arus — the Wedding Night — for Rumi himself had said: "Do not grieve at my death. It is my wedding with eternity."

Key Teachings

The Masnavi opens with the image that contains the whole of Rumi's teaching: a reed flute (ney) cut from the reed bed, crying in longing for its origin. This is the human soul — severed from its divine source at the moment of creation, placed in the body as in a cage, and crying out in every desire, every love, every restless yearning for a home it only dimly remembers. All human longing — for beauty, for union, for meaning — is ultimately this single longing of the part for the Whole, the drop for the Ocean. "Listen to the reed, how it tells a tale, crying of the pain of separation: 'Since I was cut from the reed bed, my lament has caused men and women to weep.'" For Rumi, this longing is not a problem to be solved but the very engine of the spiritual life. It is through the wound of separation that the light enters.

Above all, Rumi taught that love — ishq — is the only path that can carry the seeker across the abyss between the human and the divine. The intellect can map the territory but cannot walk it. Reason is a gift, but it reaches a shore beyond which only love can swim. "The intellect says: 'The six directions are the boundary — there is no way out.' Love says: 'There is a way. I have traveled it many times.'" This was the revolution that Shams ignited in him: the transition from knowledge about God to direct experience of God, from the religion of the head to the religion of the heart. Rumi did not reject scholarship — he was himself deeply learned — but he insisted that learning without love is a body without a soul. "Sell your cleverness and buy bewilderment. Cleverness is mere opinion; bewilderment is intuition."

Famous Stories

The First Meeting with Shams: The most famous version: Rumi was riding through the marketplace of Konya with his students when a wild-looking stranger seized the bridle of his horse and asked: "Who was greater — the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ or Bayazid Bistami?" Rumi replied: "The Prophet ﷺ — without question." Shams pressed: "Then why did the Prophet say, 'I have not known You as You deserve to be known,' while Bayazid cried, 'Glory be to Me, how great is My glory'?" At this, Rumi fell from his horse in a spiritual swoon, remaining unconscious for an hour. When he recovered, he looked at Shams and said: "Come." They went together into a cell and were not seen for forty days, after which Rumi had been transformed from a scholar into a lover.

The Whirling in the Goldsmith's Bazaar: After Shams's disappearance, Rumi became close to the goldsmith Salahaddin Zarkub. One day, walking through the goldsmith's market in Konya, Rumi heard the rhythmic hammering of gold on anvil and was seized by a force beyond himself. He began to turn — slowly at first, then with gathering speed — arms outstretched, one hand raised to heaven and one turned toward the earth, his robe fanning outward like a great wheel. His disciples gathered around him in a circle; some began to turn with him. This moment is traditionally regarded as the origin of the Mevlevi sema ceremony — the sacred whirling that would become one of the most recognizable spiritual practices in the world.

The Funeral: When Rumi died on December 17, 1273, the entire city of Konya — its Muslims, Christians, and Jews — poured into the streets to mourn. The Christians claimed him, saying: "He was like Jesus to us." The Jews claimed him, saying: "He was our Moses." The Muslims replied: "He belongs to Islam." A Christian priest who had known Rumi was asked why he mourned the Muslim saint as his own. He replied: "We understood the meaning of Jesus's love only through him. His Quran was our New Testament." Rumi himself had anticipated this universality: "Come, come, whoever you are — wanderer, worshipper, lover of leaving — it doesn't matter. Ours is not a caravan of despair. Come, even if you have broken your vows a thousand times. Come, yet again, come, come."

Major Works

  • Masnavi-yi Ma'navi (Spiritual Couplets): 25,700 verses across six books — the greatest Persian poem and the supreme masterwork of Sufi literature, called "the Quran in Persian" by the poet Jami. An inexhaustible ocean of stories, parables, theology, philosophy, humor, and prayer, the Masnavi defies categorization. It ranges from bawdy folk tales to the most sublime metaphysical poetry ever composed, all woven together by Rumi's conviction that every story, properly understood, is a story about God.
  • Diwan-i Shams-i Tabrizi (Kulliyyat-i Shams): Approximately 40,000 verses of lyric ghazals and quatrains — one of the great collections of love poetry in world literature. Written in the voice of Shams or addressed to him, these poems capture the ecstasy, anguish, and bewilderment of divine love with an intensity that has never been surpassed.
  • Fihi Ma Fihi (In It What Is In It): Prose discourses recorded by students during informal gatherings — a more accessible guide to Rumi's thought, covering topics from the nature of prayer to the psychology of jealousy, in his characteristic style of luminous simplicity.
  • Maktubat (Letters): Letters to rulers, disciples, and friends, revealing the practical dimension of Rumi's wisdom — his concern for the daily lives of those around him, his diplomacy, his humor, and his unfailing compassion.

Legacy

Rumi is today the most widely read poet in the English-speaking world and among the best-selling poets on earth — a fact that would have astonished the thirteenth-century scholar from Balkh. Translations and adaptations of his work sell millions of copies annually, though scholars note that many popular versions take considerable liberties with the original Persian, stripping away the Islamic framework that is inseparable from his meaning. The authentic Rumi is not a vague spiritual self-help guru but a Muslim mystic of extraordinary depth, whose every verse is rooted in the Quran, the hadith, and the lived tradition of Islamic spirituality. The challenge for future generations is to encounter Rumi as he actually was — not diminished by projection, but allowed to speak in his own voice.

The Mevlevi order's sema ceremony — the sacred whirling meditation — is recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. Mevlevi lodges continue to operate in Istanbul, Konya, and dozens of countries around the world. Rumi's influence on Persian and Turkish literature is beyond measurement — he transformed both languages, set the standard for mystical poetry that no subsequent poet has equaled, and created in the Masnavi a work so vast and so deep that seven centuries of commentary have not exhausted it. The poet Jami, himself among the greatest of Persian writers, declared: "He is not a prophet, but he has brought a scripture." On the night of December 17 each year, thousands gather in Konya for the Shab-i Arus — the Wedding Night — to commemorate not the death of Mawlana but his reunion with the Beloved he had been seeking all his life.

---## Chapter 42: Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki (قطب الدين بختيار كاكي)

Full Name: Abu Bakr Qutb al-Din Bakhtiyar Kaki al-Ushi al-Hanafi Born: 569 AH / 1173 CE, Ush (Fergana Valley, present-day Kyrgyzstan) Died: 14 Rabi' al-Awwal 633 AH / 1235 CE, Delhi (India) Sufi Order: Chishtiyya (received from Moinuddin Chishti) Titles: Qutb al-Aqtab (Pole of Poles), Mehboob-e-Elahi (Beloved of God) Teachers: Moinuddin Chishti (primary master), connected through him to Uthman Harwani's chain Notable Students: Fariduddin Ganjshakar (Baba Farid), Hamiduddin Nagori Major Works: Oral teachings transmitted through his disciples; no major written corpus survives Burial: Mehrauli, Delhi — Dargah Qutb Sahib (near the Qutb Minar complex), one of Delhi's most sacred sites

Biography

Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki was born in the city of Ush in the Fergana Valley of Central Asia, a region that had long served as a crossroads of Islamic learning and Turkic culture. In his youth he set out westward in search of knowledge and spiritual guidance, traveling through the great centers of the Islamic world — Baghdad, the cities of Khorasan, and eventually the emerging spiritual landscape of northern India. His journey was not merely geographical but deeply interior: he was searching for the master who could guide him to the divine presence.

That master proved to be Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti of Ajmer, the founder of the Chishtiyya in India. When the young Qutbuddin arrived in Ajmer and presented himself before Moinuddin, the elder shaykh recognized in him an extraordinary spiritual capacity. Unlike many aspirants who required years of preparatory discipline before receiving full initiation, Qutbuddin's readiness was immediately apparent. Moinuddin took him as his closest disciple and transmitted to him the secrets of the Chishti path — the practices of dhikr, the discipline of service, and above all the transformative power of sama', the devotional music that was the distinctive mark of the Chishtiyya.

After completing his training under Moinuddin, Qutbuddin traveled to Delhi, where he would spend the remainder of his life. Delhi in the early thirteenth century was the seat of the newly established Sultanate, and the city was rapidly becoming the political and cultural capital of Muslim India. Qutbuddin established a khanqah that drew seekers from every station of life — scholars, soldiers, merchants, and the poor. Sultan Shams al-Din Iltutmish, one of the greatest rulers of the Delhi Sultanate (r. 1211–1236 CE), became a devoted admirer. The sultan would visit the khanqah regularly, sitting among ordinary seekers to receive the shaykh's blessings and counsel. Yet Qutbuddin, true to the Chishti principle of spiritual independence from political power, maintained a careful distance from the apparatus of the state. He accepted the sultan's patronage only insofar as it could be directed toward feeding the poor and supporting the needy.

The manner of Qutbuddin's death is among the most celebrated episodes in the entire history of Indian Sufism. He was attending a gathering of sama' — the devotional music assembly that the Chishtis regarded as a supreme form of worship — when a singer performed the Persian verse: "Kustagan-i khunjar-i taslim ra / har zaman az ghayb jan-i digar ast" — "Those slain by the sword of total surrender receive each moment a new soul from the unseen." The verse struck Qutbuddin with a force beyond anything physical. He entered a state of spiritual absorption so profound that he could not be recalled from it. His disciples gathered around him in alarm and then in awe. For several days he remained in this condition — eyes open but seeing nothing of this world, breathing but absent from all earthly awareness. Cold water, Quranic recitation, the voices of his closest companions — nothing could reach him. He died in that state of complete annihilation in the divine, his face radiant with an otherworldly peace.

When Sultan Iltutmish received news of the shaykh's passing, he came immediately and sat by the body, weeping for hours. So deeply was the sultan moved that he issued a remarkable decree: the very verse that had precipitated Qutbuddin's death should be inscribed upon the sultan's own tombstone. It was an extraordinary tribute — a ruler of an empire acknowledging that a man who possessed nothing had achieved something no worldly power could purchase.

Key Teachings

At the heart of Qutbuddin's spiritual teaching was the doctrine of taslim — total, unconditional surrender to the divine will. The verse that accompanied his death was not merely a beautiful piece of poetry but the summation of his entire spiritual method. The seeker, he taught, must offer the self completely to God, holding nothing back — not desire, not fear, not even the wish for spiritual advancement. Like a body laid beneath the executioner's sword, the mystic surrenders all claim to personal existence. And in that annihilation of the self, paradoxically, comes the most abundant life: "each moment a new soul from the unseen." The death of the ego is the birth of the spirit, renewed perpetually by divine grace. This was not a teaching Qutbuddin merely articulated in words — he demonstrated it with his life and, ultimately, with his death.

Equally central to his legacy was his profound commitment to the practice of sama'. For Qutbuddin and the Chishti masters who followed him, devotional music was not entertainment, nor even simply a permissible pleasure. It was a form of worship as potent as prayer, capable of opening doors in the heart that no amount of intellectual study could reach. But sama' demanded preparation: the listener must come with a purified intention, a heart already softened by dhikr and service. Without such preparation, music remained mere sound. With it, a single verse could shatter the barriers between the human and the divine — as Qutbuddin's own death so powerfully demonstrated. His example became the single most cited justification for the practice of sama' in the Chishti tradition, silencing critics who argued that music had no place in the spiritual life.

Famous Stories

Death Through Sama': The story of Qutbuddin's death is among the most beautiful in all of Sufi hagiography. He was attending a gathering of sama' in his khanqah in Delhi when the singer began to perform a Persian verse about those slain by the sword of divine surrender. The words penetrated Qutbuddin's heart like a lance of light. His face transformed; tears began to flow; and then he entered a state of profound, luminous stillness. His disciples, initially uncertain whether to intervene, gathered around him as minutes became hours and hours became days. They tried everything they knew — sprinkling cold water on his face, reciting verses of the Quran near his ear, calling his name in the voices he loved most. Nothing reached him. He had passed beyond the reach of this world. When at last his breathing ceased, his face bore an expression of such radiance that those present could not bring themselves to grieve. Sultan Iltutmish, informed of the death, came to the khanqah and sat beside the body in silence for a long time. Then he spoke: "Let that verse be inscribed on my tombstone, for the man who died through it was greater than any king who ever lived."

The Selection of Baba Farid: When the young Fariduddin Mas'ud — later to be known throughout the subcontinent as Baba Farid, one of the greatest saints in the history of Indian Islam — arrived from the Punjab to study with Qutbuddin, he was still an unknown youth, distinguished only by his burning sincerity. Qutbuddin, wishing to test his disciples, set them an unusual challenge: each was to go out into the world and bring back something that no one had ever seen. The disciples scattered, returning with rare objects, curiosities, and marvels. Baba Farid returned empty-handed. When Qutbuddin asked him what he had brought, the young man replied simply: "I could find nothing that God has not seen." A silence fell over the assembly. Qutbuddin rose, embraced the young man, and said to his other disciples: "This one will be the pillar of the Chishtiyya in the coming age." The prophecy proved exact: through Baba Farid, the Chishti lineage would reach its supreme flowering in the person of Nizamuddin Auliya.

Legacy

Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki stands as the second great link in the golden chain of the Indo-Chishti silsila: Moinuddin Chishti in Ajmer, then Qutbuddin in Delhi, then Baba Farid in the Punjab, then Nizamuddin Auliya again in Delhi — each transmission producing a master whose spiritual influence exceeded that of his predecessor. Without Qutbuddin, the Chishtiyya might have remained a regional phenomenon centered on Ajmer. By establishing the order in Delhi, the political heart of Muslim India, he ensured that the Chishti message of love, service, and devotional music would reach every corner of the subcontinent. His insistence on spiritual independence from political power — accepting the sultan's friendship while refusing to become his instrument — set the template that his successors, particularly Nizamuddin Auliya, would follow with even greater rigor.

His dargah in Mehrauli remains one of Delhi's most sacred and beloved sites, a place of pilgrimage for Muslims and non-Muslims alike. The shrine's significance is attested by the fact that Bahadur Shah Zafar, the last Mughal emperor, specifically requested to be buried in its vicinity — and his grave stands just steps from Qutbuddin's tomb, a final tribute from the last representative of a dynasty that had revered the Chishti saints for centuries. But Qutbuddin's most enduring legacy may be the story of his death itself. In a tradition that has always debated the permissibility and spiritual value of music, the image of a saint so overwhelmed by a verse of surrender that he passed from this world into the next became the ultimate argument — not through theology but through lived example — that sama' could be a genuine portal to the divine presence.

Chapter 43

Sadr al-Din Qunawi

صدر الدين القونوي
Full NameMuhammad ibn Ishaq Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi
Bornc. 607 AH / 1210 CE, Malatya (eastern Anatolia, present-day Turkey)
Died673 AH / 1274 CE, Konya (Anatolia)
Sufi OrderThe Akbarian school of Ibn Arabi; also received initiations in the Suhrawardiyya and Qadiriyya
TitlesShaykh al-Mashayikh (Master of Masters), The Seal of the Akbarian School, Khwandagar (Lord/Teacher — in Anatolia)
TeachersIbn Arabi (stepfather and primary spiritual master), Awhaduddin Kirmani, Shihab al-Din al-Suhrawardi

BurialKonya — his tomb stands near that of Rumi

Biography

Sadr al-Din Qunawi was born in Malatya, in the rugged highlands of eastern Anatolia, into a family of learning and piety. His father died while he was still young, and his mother subsequently married the man who would shape the entire course of his life and the history of Islamic mystical thought: Muhyi al-Din Ibn Arabi, the greatest Sufi metaphysician the tradition has ever produced. The young Sadr al-Din thus became the stepson, the intimate companion, and ultimately the closest disciple of a master whose writings would transform the intellectual landscape of the Islamic world. He grew up in Ibn Arabi's household, absorbing not only formal instruction but the daily presence of a mind that operated at the very boundary between human thought and divine revelation. He traveled with his stepfather through the cities of Anatolia and the Levant, and when Ibn Arabi settled in Damascus, it was Qunawi who received the most complete transmission of his teachings.

The relationship between Ibn Arabi and Qunawi was more than that of teacher and student — it was the deliberate transmission of an entire spiritual and intellectual inheritance. Ibn Arabi was explicit that Qunawi would be his primary heir, the one entrusted with the responsibility of preserving, systematizing, and transmitting the vast body of mystical knowledge that the Shaykh al-Akbar had received through inspiration and articulated in hundreds of works. This was no light burden. Ibn Arabi's writings — above all the Futuhat al-Makkiyya and the Fusus al-Hikam — were works of staggering complexity, written in a visionary language that defied easy comprehension. Someone was needed who could stand between this towering edifice and the generations to come, making its architecture visible and its chambers accessible. Qunawi accepted this mission as his life's work.

To prepare himself, Qunawi spent years in Mecca, Baghdad, and other great centers of Islamic learning, acquiring rigorous mastery of the exoteric sciences — jurisprudence, theology, Quranic commentary, and hadith scholarship — to complement the esoteric inheritance he had received from Ibn Arabi. This dual training was essential to his later achievement. When he wrote about the unity of existence or the nature of divine self-disclosure, he did so not as an unlettered visionary but as a scholar who could engage with the Aristotelian philosophers and the Ash'ari theologians on their own terms, demonstrating that Ibn Arabi's metaphysics was not poetic fancy but a coherent and rigorous account of reality.

He eventually settled in Konya, the capital of the Seljuk Sultanate of Rum and one of the most vibrant intellectual cities in the thirteenth-century Islamic world. There he established himself as one of the city's most respected spiritual authorities, presiding over a circle of advanced students who would themselves become the primary transmitters of the Akbarian tradition. And it was in Konya that the most remarkable friendship of his life unfolded — his relationship with Jalal al-Din Rumi. The two men represented radically different approaches to the highest spiritual reality: Qunawi the philosopher-mystic, working through precise conceptual language to map the architecture of divine self-manifestation; Rumi the ecstatic poet, shattering all conceptual frameworks in the fire of love. Yet they recognized each other as kindred spirits who had reached the same summit by different paths. Qunawi would sometimes lead Rumi in prayer; Rumi's poetry occasionally echoed themes from Qunawi's metaphysical teachings. Their mutual respect was a living demonstration that the mystical truth transcends any single mode of expression.

Qunawi's correspondence with the great philosopher and scientist Nasir al-Din al-Tusi represents one of the most sophisticated intellectual dialogues in medieval Islamic history. Tusi, a Shi'i philosopher in the Avicennan tradition who served the Mongol Ilkhanids and founded the famous observatory at Maragha, posed searching questions about the nature of divine knowledge and its relationship to the created world. Qunawi's replies demonstrated a mastery of both Aristotelian logic and Akbarian mystical theology, showing that the insights of spiritual realization could be articulated in terms that philosophical reason was compelled to respect, even when those insights pointed beyond reason's own horizon. The exchange stands as the highest point of dialogue between Islamic philosophy and Islamic mysticism — two great traditions meeting in the persons of two of their finest representatives.

Key Teachings

Qunawi's supreme intellectual achievement was the systematization of wahdat al-wujud — the doctrine of the unity of existence — that lies at the heart of Ibn Arabi's metaphysics. Ibn Arabi had articulated this vision through inspired writings of extraordinary depth and difficulty, works that moved between Quranic commentary, metaphysical speculation, and visionary narrative with dizzying speed. Qunawi took this vast and sometimes bewildering corpus and translated its core insights into philosophically rigorous language that could engage with the dominant Aristotelian tradition of Islamic philosophy. He demonstrated, with painstaking precision, that the Akbarian understanding of reality — in which all existence is a single divine self-manifestation, appearing in infinite forms while remaining essentially one — was not a vague mystical sentiment but a coherent ontological system with its own internal logic and its own criteria of truth. This systematization made it possible for subsequent generations to study, teach, and debate Ibn Arabi's ideas within the framework of formal Islamic scholarship.

Central to Qunawi's own contribution was his development of the concept of the barzakh — the isthmus or intermediate reality. Drawing on Ibn Arabi's teaching, Qunawi articulated with unprecedented clarity the idea that the Perfect Human (al-Insan al-Kamil) stands as the isthmus between the infinite divine reality and the finite created world. The Perfect Human is the mirror in which God contemplates Himself — the locus where the absolute, which in itself is beyond all form and determination, takes on the most complete and comprehensive form. This teaching, which Qunawi grounded in both scriptural evidence and philosophical argument, became the cornerstone of all subsequent Akbarian metaphysics. Through his students — al-Kashani, al-Jandi, al-Qaysari — it spread from Konya throughout the Islamic world, shaping the intellectual framework of Sufism from Ottoman Turkey to Mughal India.

Famous Stories

Death from Grief at Rumi's Passing: The story of Qunawi's death is one of the most moving testimonies to spiritual friendship in the entire Sufi tradition. When Rumi died on December 17, 1273, all of Konya gathered for the funeral — Muslims, Christians, and Jews alike, for Rumi had been loved by all. Qunawi, who had cherished Rumi with the deep love of one who recognizes a kindred soul, found that he could not bring himself to leave his house. The grief was beyond what his body could bear. When the vast funeral procession passed by his door, he heard the sound of weeping and the strains of music mingling in the winter air. He said to those around him: "He has passed. There is nothing left to wait for." He then turned to face the qibla, performed two cycles of prayer with exquisite care, and sat down in meditation. He did not rise again. Within hours of the funeral procession passing his door, Sadr al-Din Qunawi was dead. The metaphysician who had spent his life articulating the architecture of divine unity had, in the end, demonstrated its deepest truth in the simplest possible way: he could not live in a world from which his friend had departed. They were buried near each other in Konya, as they had lived — two luminaries of the same city, two travelers who had reached the same destination.

The Letter to Tusi: When the renowned philosopher Nasir al-Din al-Tusi wrote to Qunawi posing a series of searching questions about the nature of divine knowledge — how God knows particulars, whether divine knowledge is identical with divine essence, and how the eternal can relate to the temporal — Qunawi's replies displayed a breathtaking command of both the philosophical and mystical traditions. He met Tusi's Aristotelian arguments on their own ground, then gently showed where philosophical demonstration reaches its limit and a higher mode of knowing begins. He did not dismiss reason but honored it as a necessary stage in the ascent toward truth, while insisting that the highest truths are accessible only through a knowledge that is itself a divine gift — a tasting (dhawq) rather than a demonstration (burhan). Tusi, who had debated the finest minds of his age, is reported to have said upon reading Qunawi's letters: "I have debated with all the philosophers of our age. This man has shown me something I could not reach through demonstration alone — that there is a knowledge beyond knowledge." Their correspondence remains one of the treasures of Islamic intellectual history.

Major Works

  • Miftah al-Ghayb (Key of the Unseen): Qunawi's most systematic work, serving as an introduction to the methodology of Akbarian mystical theology. It lays out the principles by which Ibn Arabi's teachings should be approached and understood, and provides the conceptual vocabulary that subsequent generations of commentators would employ.
  • al-Fukuk (The Loosening): A detailed commentary on the chapter headings of Ibn Arabi's Fusus al-Hikam, designed to unlock the dense symbolism of the master's most concentrated and difficult work. It remains an essential companion text for any serious student of the Fusus.
  • I'jaz al-Bayan fi Tafsir Umm al-Quran: A mystical commentary on Surat al-Fatiha, the opening chapter of the Quran. In this remarkable work, Qunawi demonstrates how the seven verses of the Fatiha contain, in seed form, the entirety of divine self-disclosure — making it one of the most sophisticated short texts in Sufi literature.
  • Correspondence with Nasir al-Din al-Tusi: A series of letters constituting a profound philosophical dialogue between the mystical and rational traditions of Islamic thought, widely studied as a landmark in the history of Islamic intellectual exchange.

Legacy

Without Sadr al-Din Qunawi, the vast and difficult writings of Ibn Arabi might have remained the preserve of a small circle of advanced initiates, admired but largely inaccessible to the broader world of Islamic scholarship. Qunawi performed the essential and irreplaceable function of the systematic expositor — the one who takes the master's inspired but often cryptic utterances and renders them in a form that can be studied, taught, debated, and transmitted across generations. His school in Konya produced the scholars who would carry the Akbarian tradition into every corner of the Islamic world: Abd al-Razzaq al-Kashani, whose commentary on the Fusus al-Hikam became the standard reference; Mu'ayyid al-Din al-Jandi, who wrote the first comprehensive commentary on the same work; and Dawud al-Qaysari, who became the first rector of an Ottoman madrasa and made the Akbarian tradition officially respectable within the Ottoman scholarly establishment.

The intellectual framework that Qunawi systematized — the metaphysics of wahdat al-wujud, the doctrine of the Perfect Human as the cosmic isthmus, the methodology of spiritual hermeneutics — became the dominant paradigm for Sufi metaphysical thought for centuries to come. From the Ottoman Empire to Safavid Iran to the Mughal courts of India, the language and concepts that Qunawi had refined served as the common currency of mystical theology. His influence was not limited to Sufism alone: through scholars like al-Qaysari, Akbarian ideas entered the curriculum of formal Islamic education, shaping discussions in theology, philosophy, and Quranic commentary. In this sense, Qunawi's legacy extends far beyond the circles of practicing mystics — he helped to weave the insights of the greatest Sufi master into the very fabric of Islamic intellectual life.

Chapter 44

Fariduddin Ganjshakar — Baba Farid

فريد الدين گنج شكر
Full NameMasud ibn Ahmad Farid al-Din al-Qurashi (Baba Farid Ganjshakar)
Born569 AH / 1173 CE, Khotwal (near Multan, Punjab, present-day Pakistan)
Died5 Muharram 664 AH / 7 October 1265 CE, Pakpattan (Punjab, present-day Pakistan)
Sufi OrderChishtiyya — received from Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki in Delhi
TitlesBaba Farid (Father Farid — the beloved title by which he is universally known), Ganjshakar (Treasure of Sugar), Farid al-Haqq wa al-Din
TeachersQutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki (primary master), Shaykh Najib al-Din Mutawakkil (early training)
Notable StudentsNizamuddin Auliya (his greatest successor), Alauddin Sabir Kaliyari, Jamaluddin Hansawi, Badruddin Ishaq
Major WorksPunjabi shaloks (couplets) and kafis (lyrical poems), a selection of which are preserved in the Guru Granth Sahib
BurialPakpattan, Punjab, Pakistan — Darbar Baba Farid — enormous annual Urs attracts hundreds of thousands

Biography

Born in Khotwal near Multan in the Punjab, Fariduddin showed exceptional piety from childhood. He came from a family tracing their lineage to the Companion Umar ibn al-Khattab. From his earliest years he was drawn to prayer and fasting with an intensity unusual for a child. His mother, wanting to encourage her young son to pray the morning prayer, would secretly place a small amount of sugar beneath his prayer rug. Young Farid would find it after prayers and believe that God had rewarded his devotion with sweetness. This continued until one morning no sugar was placed — but Farid found sweetness anyway, as if God had placed it directly in his heart. From that day he was called Ganjshakar — Treasure of Sugar. Miracles of sugar would be associated with him throughout his life and beyond.

He made his way to Delhi to study under Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki, the great Chishti master. His initiation by Qutbuddin was the defining spiritual event of his life. After receiving the khirqa — the initiatory cloak — Baba Farid underwent years of extraordinary spiritual disciplines, including extended chilla (forty-day retreats) of great severity. In one of the most famous ascetic acts in Sufi history, he had himself suspended upside down in a well — the chah-e ulta — for forty days while continuously reciting the Quran, eating almost nothing. This act of radical self-discipline broke the remaining hold of his nafs (ego) and opened him to divine realities that would define his subsequent teaching.

After the death of Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki, Baba Farid was recognized as his successor in the Chishti silsila. He left Delhi and settled permanently in Ajodhan — present-day Pakpattan, Punjab, a name meaning "the ford of the pure ones." His khanqah there became a renowned center of spiritual life; the poor, the sick, and the seeking came from hundreds of miles in every direction. He lived a life of genuine poverty, sometimes going days without food while wealthy donors sent him gifts that he immediately distributed among the destitute. His literal embrace of faqr — spiritual poverty — was not a pose but the core of his being.

His relationship with the Delhi Sultanate was complex. True to Chishti principles, he generally avoided political involvement, yet several sultans visited him and sought his blessing. When Sultan Ghiyasuddin Balban's son Prince Muhammad Khan was killed in battle, the bereaved sultan traveled to Baba Farid seeking consolation, and the saint's words moved him deeply. His steadfast refusal of political entanglement, his literal poverty, and his complete identification with the common people made him beloved across all social boundaries — a saint who belonged to everyone and to no one.

Baba Farid's most remarkable contribution to posterity lies in his poetry. He composed his verses not in Persian or Arabic — the literary languages of the Muslim elite — but in Punjabi, the language of the common people of the Punjab. His shaloks and kafis are among the earliest surviving examples of Punjabi literature and some of the most beautiful devotional poetry ever written in any language. A selection of his verses was later incorporated into the Guru Granth Sahib — the sacred scripture of the Sikh religion — by Guru Nanak and subsequent Gurus. This means that Baba Farid, a Sufi Muslim saint, is one of the revered poets of the Sikh scriptural canon — perhaps the most striking example of spiritual commonality across religious traditions in all of South Asian history.

Key Teachings

Baba Farid embodied the Chishti doctrine of absolute poverty as the path to God. Unlike the Suhrawardi approach of his contemporary Bahauddin Zakariya of Multan, who accepted that a Sufi could live in comfort while maintaining inner detachment, Baba Farid maintained that true detachment requires literal abandonment of wealth and worldly comfort. His famous verse captures this stance: "Farida, the world's sweetness has become bitter to me / The taste of divine nearness has replaced all other tastes." He taught that every moment of worldly comfort is a moment of veiling from God, and that only through the complete stripping away of material attachment could the heart become transparent enough to receive divine light.

The second pillar of his teaching was patience — sabr — in affliction as the highest spiritual practice. His Punjabi poetry returns again and again to the theme of accepting suffering as a divine gift, a purifying fire that burns away everything except the essential relationship between the soul and its Creator. "Farida, those upon whose hearts the divine fire has fallen / They are the ones who truly live — the rest are merely alive." This was not a morbid fascination with pain but a profound understanding, rooted in his own experience of radical asceticism, that the ego's resistance to suffering is the final barrier between the human being and God. To accept affliction with gratitude, he taught, is to pass through the last door into the divine presence.

Famous Stories

The Sugar Miracle: One day, Baba Farid received a gift of wheat from a devotee. He gave it to his wife to grind and cook. The flour was ground and bread was baked. When people began to eat, they found the bread as sweet as candy — it tasted of pure sugar. The devotees looked at each other in astonishment. Baba Farid simply smiled and said: "This is the name that has followed me since childhood." Miracles of sugar transforming ordinary things into sweetness have been reported in various forms throughout his life and after his death — hence the title Ganjshakar, the Treasure of Sugar, which captures not merely a childhood story but the fundamental quality of his presence: the capacity to transmute the bitterness of the world into divine sweetness.

The Upside-Down Well: During one of his most severe spiritual retreats, Baba Farid had himself lowered upside down into a well. He hung there for forty days, reciting the Quran from memory, subsisting on almost nothing, performing the acts of worship normally done standing while completely inverted. When he was finally brought up, his face was luminous, and those who surrounded the well reported seeing light emanating from his body. He said simply: "I have found what I was seeking." The chah-e ulta became one of the most celebrated episodes in the entire history of Sufi asceticism — a symbol of the lengths to which a seeker must go to overturn the hold of the lower self and discover the reality that lies beneath all appearances.

The Verses in the Guru Granth Sahib: Centuries after Baba Farid's death, Guru Arjan Dev — the fifth Sikh Guru — incorporated his Punjabi poetry into the Guru Granth Sahib, the sacred scripture of the Sikhs. This was no accident. Guru Nanak himself had visited the Chishti shrine at Pakpattan and engaged in spiritual exchange with Baba Farid's successors. The decision to include a Muslim Sufi's verses in the Sikh scriptural canon reflects the Gurus' recognition that divine love transcends all religious boundaries, and that Baba Farid's Punjabi poetry expressed truths universal to all spiritual seekers. To this day, his shaloks are recited in Sikh gurdwaras alongside the compositions of the Gurus themselves — a living testimony to the unity of the heart's longing for God across all traditions.

Major Works

  • Punjabi shaloks and kafis — devotional couplets and lyrical poems composed in the Punjabi language, transmitted orally and later compiled by his disciples; a significant selection is preserved in the Guru Granth Sahib
  • No systematic prose works survive; his teachings were transmitted through spiritual example, oral instruction, and the living tradition of his disciples

Legacy

Baba Farid is the most beloved saint of the Punjab — a figure of transcendent importance for Pakistani and Indian culture alike, one whose reverence crosses all religious boundaries. His Punjabi poetry, preserved in the Guru Granth Sahib and in the living Sufi kalam traditions of the Punjab, constitutes some of the earliest and finest examples of the Punjabi language in literature. He demonstrated that the deepest spiritual truths need not be expressed in the elite languages of Persian and Arabic but could be spoken in the tongue of the people — a principle that his Chishti successors would carry forward across the entire Indian subcontinent.

Through his succession to Nizamuddin Auliya — the greatest of his disciples — Baba Farid became the spiritual grandfather of the entire Delhi Chishti tradition. Nizamuddin's own students spread the order across all of India, carrying with them the spirit of poverty, love, and service to the poor that Baba Farid had embodied. His dargah in Pakpattan remains one of Pakistan's most visited shrines, with the annual Urs drawing hundreds of thousands of devotees from every background. In a region often defined by partition and division, Baba Farid endures as a figure of unity — a saint claimed by Muslims and Sikhs, by Pakistanis and Indians, by scholars and illiterate peasants, by all who have tasted, even once, the inexplicable sweetness that earned him his name.

Sharaf al-Din al-Busiri

شرف الدين البوصيري
Full NameAbu Abdallah Sharaf al-Din Muhammad ibn Sa'id ibn Hammad al-Sanhaji al-Busiri al-Misri
Born608 AH / 1211 CE, Busir (Buhayra, Egypt)
Diedc. 694 AH / 1294 CE, Alexandria (Egypt)
Sufi OrderShadhiliyya — disciple of Abu al-Abbas al-Mursi, the primary successor of Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili
TitlesThe Composer of the Burda
TeachersAbu al-Abbas al-Mursi (Shadhili master of Alexandria)
Major Worksal-Kawakib al-Durriyya fi Madh Khayr al-Bariyya — universally known as Qasida al-Burda (The Poem of the Cloak); also al-Hamziyya (another great ode to the Prophet)
BurialAlexandria, Egypt

Biography

Al-Busiri was born in the village of Busir in the Egyptian delta and grew up to become a minor government official and poet in Egypt during the Mamluk period. He was gifted at poetry from an early age and composed panegyrics for various patrons, earning a modest reputation in the literary circles of his day. His spiritual life was fundamentally transformed when he encountered Abu al-Abbas al-Mursi — the foremost successor of Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili, founder of the Shadhiliyya order — in Alexandria. Under al-Mursi's guidance, he entered the Shadhili path and underwent the spiritual training that would prepare him for the event that made his name immortal.

The occasion of the Burda's composition was a crisis of the body that became a triumph of the spirit. Al-Busiri suffered a severe stroke that left him partially paralyzed — half of his body was rendered immobile. All conventional medicine failed him. In his desperation, he turned to the only recourse left to a believer: he composed a poem of praise and supplication addressed to the Prophet Muhammad, begging for intercession and healing. This poem — which he titled al-Kawakib al-Durriyya fi Madh Khayr al-Bariyya (The Celestial Stars in Praise of the Best of Creation) — would become the most famous religious poem in Islamic history. He composed it as an act of desperate love, not knowing whether it would be answered.

After completing the poem, al-Busiri fell asleep and dreamed that the Prophet Muhammad appeared to him. The Prophet asked him to recite the poem. Al-Busiri recited it, and the Prophet praised it. Then the Prophet took his own cloak — his burda — and draped it over al-Busiri's paralyzed side. Al-Busiri awoke to find himself completely healed; the paralysis was gone. He sat up, prostrated in gratitude, and wept. That same morning, a fellow dervish came to him and said: "I saw the Prophet last night, and he was wearing his cloak and praising a poem. I heard some of its lines and recognized them as yours." Al-Busiri realized that his companion had independently witnessed the same dream. The poem was thereafter called the Burda — The Cloak — after the garment with which the Prophet had healed him.

Al-Busiri spent the remainder of his long life in Alexandria as a devoted disciple of the Shadhiliyya, given over to spiritual practice and the service of the Prophet through poetry. He composed the Hamziyya — another major ode in praise of the Prophet — and various other poems, but none approached the Burda in its spiritual impact or its reach. He died in Alexandria at an advanced age, a man whose personal life would be almost entirely eclipsed by the radiance of his single greatest work.

Key Teachings

The Burda is the supreme literary expression of the Sunni doctrine of prophetic intercession — the belief that the Prophet Muhammad, by God's permission, intercedes for believers in times of need, both in this world and in the next. Al-Busiri's own healing served as dramatic personal evidence of this teaching. The poem's 160 verses move from the poet's own condition of longing and spiritual distress, through a detailed celebration of the Prophet's birth, character, miracles, and the Night Journey, to a final outpouring of supplication and hope for divine mercy. In this architecture, the Burda enacts the very process it describes: the turning of the broken human heart toward the Prophet as the gateway to God's compassion.

Beyond its theological content, the Burda established the definitive model for the adab — the etiquette — of prophetic love in Islamic literary culture. Its verses constitute a complete manual of proper reverence for the Prophet, describing his qualities and attributes with a precision that is simultaneously doctrinal and ecstatic. Every subsequent tradition of na't — prophetic praise poetry — in Arabic, Persian, Turkish, Urdu, Swahili, and dozens of other languages traces its lineage to the standard al-Busiri set. The poem demonstrated that the highest art and the deepest devotion are not merely compatible but identical: that a poem composed in extremity of need, with no thought of literary reputation, could become the most enduring monument of an entire civilization's love for its Prophet.

Famous Stories

The Dream of Healing: After composing his poem of praise and supplication, al-Busiri — paralyzed and abandoned by medicine — fell asleep and saw the Prophet Muhammad in a dream. The Prophet asked to hear the poem. Al-Busiri recited it from beginning to end. The Prophet's face showed pleasure, and he took his cloak and wrapped it around al-Busiri's stricken body. When al-Busiri awoke, the paralysis had vanished entirely. He rose, prostrated in gratitude, and wept with joy. A fellow dervish arrived that morning to tell him of a dream he had experienced the same night: he had seen the Prophet wearing his cloak and listening to a poem whose verses he recognized as al-Busiri's. The convergence of the two dreams confirmed the miracle and gave the poem its eternal name — the Burda, the Cloak.

The Reluctant Publication: Initially, al-Busiri kept the poem entirely private. It had been composed as a personal act of devotion — a cry from the depths of suffering, not intended for public display. But the dervish who had independently witnessed the dream in his own vision convinced him to share it. When the Burda was first recited publicly in Alexandria, the response was overwhelming. People wept; some reported entering spiritual states; one person claimed to be healed of an ailment during the recitation itself. Within months, the poem had traveled beyond Egypt's borders to the wider Muslim world, carried by pilgrims, merchants, and scholars who recognized in its verses something that transcended ordinary literature.

The Ottoman Sultan's Copy: It is recorded that when Sultan Selim I entered Cairo after his conquest of Egypt in 922 AH (1517 CE), he immediately sought out the finest manuscripts of the Burda. He had a copy prepared in the most exquisite calligraphy and kept it with him always. Ottoman sultans thereafter maintained the practice of keeping a copy of the Burda as a royal talisman — a tradition that spread the poem's reputation across the entire Ottoman world. To this day, multiple magnificently illuminated manuscript copies of the Burda are preserved in Istanbul's Topkapi Palace, testimony to the reverence in which the poem was held by the most powerful rulers of the Islamic world.

Major Works

  • Qasida al-Burda (The Poem of the Cloak) — 160 verses in 10 sections, covering: the poet's condition of longing, praise of the Prophet's birth and character, the excellence of the Quran, the Prophet's Night Journey and Ascension, the struggles of the early community, and a final supplication for mercy and forgiveness. The most recited poem in Islamic history.
  • al-Hamziyya — another major ode in praise of the Prophet, named for the letter hamza with which each verse concludes; also widely recited and celebrated, though standing always in the shadow of the Burda.

Legacy

The Burda is perhaps the most recited poem in human history. For over seven hundred years it has been chanted continuously across the entire Muslim world — from Morocco to Indonesia, from Turkey to West Africa, from the mosques of Central Asia to the madrasas of East Africa. Over ninety commentaries have been written on it in Arabic alone; it has been translated into dozens of languages and set to countless melodies. It is recited at births and deaths, at mawlid celebrations of the Prophet's birthday, in times of calamity and times of joy. Its 160 verses are memorized by millions of Muslims who may know nothing else of classical Arabic poetry. The entire tradition of na't — prophetic praise poetry — in every Muslim literary culture owes its model and its inspiration to the standard al-Busiri established.

Al-Busiri himself remains one of history's most paradoxical figures: a poet of modest worldly achievement whose single inspired work became the most beloved poem in the world's second-largest religion. His personal biography is almost unknown — eclipsed entirely by the radiance of the Burda — making him perhaps the most successful anonymous author in literary history. Yet this very anonymity is fitting: the Burda's power lies not in its author's fame but in the sincerity of a broken man's cry to the Prophet he loved, and in the miracle that answered it. Al-Busiri vanished behind his poem, and the poem became a door through which seven centuries of believers have passed into the presence of prophetic love.

Chapter 46

Nizamuddin Auliya

نظام الدين اولياء
Full NameMuhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Ali al-Bukhari al-Badayuni — Khwaja Nizam al-Din al-Auliya
Born636 AH / 1238 CE, Badaun (Uttar Pradesh, India)
Died18 Rabi' al-Awwal 725 AH / 3 April 1325 CE, Delhi
Sufi OrderChishtiyya (received from Baba Farid Ganjshakar)
TitlesMahbub-i Ilahi (Beloved of God), Sultan al-Auliya (Sultan of Saints), Hazrat Delhavi
TeachersBaba Farid Ganjshakar (primary master)
Notable StudentsAmir Khusraw, Amir Hasan Sijzi (compiler of Fawa'id al-Fu'ad), Nasir al-Din Chiragh-i Dilli (his successor in Delhi), Burhanuddin Gharib (who spread the Chishtiyya to the Deccan), Akhi Siraj (who spread it to Bengal)
Major WorksFawa'id al-Fu'ad (Morals of the Heart) — his discourses compiled by Amir Hasan Sijzi
BurialDelhi — Hazrat Nizamuddin Dargah (in the neighborhood now known as Nizamuddin), one of the most visited shrines in all of South Asia; qawwali performed nightly

Biography

Nizamuddin Auliya was born in Badaun, in what is now Uttar Pradesh, to a family of migrants who had come from Bukhara in Central Asia. He lost his father while still a child and was raised by his pious mother Bibi Zuleikha, a woman of extraordinary spiritual strength and self-sacrifice whose influence shaped his character profoundly. Educated in Delhi, the young Nizamuddin showed remarkable aptitude for Islamic scholarship and seemed destined for a conventional career as a jurist or theologian. But his life took a decisive turn when he encountered a disciple of Baba Farid Ganjshakar — the great Chishti master of Ajodhan in the Punjab — who spoke of his teacher with such love and reverence that Nizamuddin felt his heart pulled irresistibly toward the Chishti path. He resolved to set out for Ajodhan and present himself before Baba Farid.

His arrival at Baba Farid's khanqah became one of the great encounter stories of the Chishti tradition. Nizamuddin arrived late — the pilgrimage season was over and Baba Farid had closed his door to new initiates for the year. Undeterred, the young man sat at the door and waited. He sent a message in the form of a verse: "The fasting season is over — the doors of paradise are open / O Farid, my heart yearns..." When the aged master heard the verse, he laughed and called out to his attendants: "Open the door — the very person I have been waiting for has come." Nizamuddin was brought in and immediately received the khirqa — the initiatory cloak — from Baba Farid, who gave him his own wooden sandals (khadaon) as a mark of spiritual inheritance, saying: "This gift I give to you; preserve it." It was a gesture of extraordinary significance: Baba Farid was identifying this unknown young man from Badaun as the one who would carry the Chishti flame into its most brilliant age.

He returned to Delhi as Baba Farid's designated successor and established his khanqah at Ghiyaspur, a quarter on the outskirts of the capital that would eventually be renamed Nizamuddin in his honor. For forty years — from the late thirteenth century through the first quarter of the fourteenth — he served as the spiritual pole of the entire subcontinent, never leaving Delhi, never seeking worldly position, never turning away a single soul who came to his door. He ministered to an extraordinary range of humanity: sultans and scholars, merchants and soldiers, artisans and the destitute, Hindus and Muslims, people of every caste and condition. His rule was absolute and unvarying: anyone who came hungry was fed. Anyone who came with a broken heart was consoled. Anyone who sought God was guided. The khanqah at Ghiyaspur became the spiritual center of the Delhi Sultanate — not through any claim to authority, but through the sheer gravitational force of love.

His relationship with political power was the most principled and consistent in the entire history of the Chishtiyya. Sultans came to his door; he generally did not visit theirs. He neither sought royal patronage nor made a show of rejecting it — he simply lived according to a different order of reality, one in which the distinctions between ruler and subject, wealth and poverty, had no ultimate significance. The most famous test of this principle came during the reign of Sultan Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq, who was returning from a successful military campaign in Bengal. The sultan, troubled by what he perceived as the shaykh's excessive influence, sent a threatening message ordering Nizamuddin to leave Delhi before the royal army's return. Nizamuddin received the message calmly. His response became one of the most quoted lines in Indian history: "Hanooz Dilli door ast" — "Delhi is still far away." The sultan died in an accident before reaching the capital. This episode — repeated in various forms with different sultans throughout Nizamuddin's long ministry — established a principle that reverberated through the centuries: genuine spiritual authority is not subject to political power. The saints who came after him drew upon this precedent whenever worldly rulers sought to bend the sacred to serve the secular.

His final years were marked by the deaths of those he loved most — his nephew Taqiuddin, and above all his devoted disciple Amir Khusraw, the greatest poet of medieval India, who had been his companion for decades and who would die himself just six months later, unable to survive in a world without his master. Nizamuddin died on 18 Rabi' al-Awwal 725 AH — the 3rd of April, 1325 — at the age of eighty-seven, having served the people of Delhi for forty unbroken years. It is said that when he died, the nightly qawwali at his khanqah did not stop. It has continued, with only brief interruptions, for seven hundred years.

Key Teachings

The central axis of Nizamuddin's spiritual teaching was the absolute primacy of love. His discourses, recorded with loving fidelity by Amir Hasan Sijzi in the Fawa'id al-Fu'ad (Morals of the Heart), return to this theme with the persistence of a heartbeat: divine love is the beginning, the middle, and the end of the spiritual path. Not knowledge, not asceticism, not ritual precision — love. "Every lover is a beginner; every beginner is a lover," he taught, collapsing the distinction between the novice and the adept that other traditions so carefully maintained. In his practice, this meant that every visitor who came to his khanqah — whether sultan or beggar, scholar or illiterate — was received with the same gentle, complete respect. There were no ranks in the court of love. The divine gaze, as Nizamuddin understood it, rests upon all human beings with equal tenderness, and the task of the saint is to embody that gaze in the world.

Inseparable from this doctrine of love was an ethic of absolute service. Nizamuddin taught that the highest form of worship is not prayer or fasting but the act of serving other human beings. His rule for daily life became famous: "The morning of a saint belongs to God; the afternoon belongs to the people." He would spend the first half of each day in personal worship — in solitude, in dhikr, in intimate communion with the divine — and the second half in attending to all who came to him with their needs, their sorrows, their questions, their hunger. Neither half could be abandoned in favor of the other: without the morning's communion, the afternoon's service would lack spiritual power; without the afternoon's service, the morning's communion would lack compassion. This rhythm of worship and service, interior life and outward generosity, became the defining pattern of the mature Chishti tradition and the model that generations of Indian Sufis would strive to emulate.

Famous Stories

"Delhi Is Still Far Away": When Sultan Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq (r. 1320–1325) was returning from his successful military campaign in Bengal, he sent Nizamuddin a threatening message — ordering the shaykh to be gone from Delhi before the sultan's arrival. The messenger who delivered the ultimatum expected to find fear and panic. Instead, Nizamuddin received the message with perfect calm. He replied with words that have echoed through seven centuries of Indian history: "Hanooz Dilli door ast" — "Delhi is still far away." On the road back from Bengal, the sultan's welcoming pavilion — hastily constructed by his son Muhammad bin Tughlaq — collapsed upon him, killing him instantly. His son Muhammad, who succeeded him to the throne, was himself a great admirer of Nizamuddin. The pattern repeated itself across Nizamuddin's long ministry: sultans who came against the saint met ruin; those who came to him in humility were blessed. Contemporaries did not regard this as coincidence. They understood it as the visible demonstration of an invisible truth — that spiritual authority, rooted in the divine, is beyond the reach of any earthly power.

The Morning of the Beloved: Among Nizamuddin's rules for his khanqah, the most inviolable was this: in the early morning hours, before the dawn prayer, he would sit alone in complete silence, in a state of intimate presence with God. No one — however urgent the matter — was permitted to disturb him. One morning, a disciple accidentally walked into Nizamuddin's chamber during this sacred time. What he saw shook him to his core. The master was sitting perfectly still, his face transfigured, tears running silently down his cheeks, the room suffused with an indefinable fragrance that seemed to come from nowhere and everywhere. The disciple retreated immediately, barely able to breathe. When he later gathered the courage to ask Nizamuddin about what he had witnessed, the master answered with four words that contained an entire theology: "The morning belongs to the Beloved."

The Legacy of the Sandals: The wooden sandals (khadaon) that Baba Farid had given to Nizamuddin at Ajodhan became one of the most sacred relics in the Chishti tradition. They were not merely a personal gift but a symbol of unbroken spiritual transmission — the very sandals that Moinuddin Chishti had passed to Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki, who had passed them to Baba Farid, who had now entrusted them to Nizamuddin. When Nizamuddin's own death approached, he passed them in turn to Nasir al-Din Chiragh-i Dilli, his chosen successor. This passing of the sandals — from Moinuddin to Qutbuddin to Baba Farid to Nizamuddin to Chiragh — was the Chishti investiture ceremony, equivalent to the giving of a crown in a secular dynasty. But where a crown signifies power over others, the sandals signified something far greater: the authority to walk in the footsteps of the beloved, to carry the burden of love from one generation to the next.

Major Works

  • Fawa'id al-Fu'ad (Morals of the Heart): His discourses compiled over many years by his disciple Amir Hasan Sijzi — one of the great texts of Islamic spiritual literature and the primary source for Nizamuddin's teachings. It records his conversations, stories, and spiritual counsel with a vividness and intimacy that make it not merely a doctrinal text but a portrait of a living saint.
  • Nizamuddin left no systematic authored works. His tradition was transmitted not through writing but through personal presence, conversation, and spiritual example — the spoken word, the compassionate gaze, the hand extended to the needy. In this, he embodied the Chishti conviction that the deepest truths cannot be confined to the page.

Legacy

Nizamuddin Auliya is the greatest saint of the Delhi Sultanate period and arguably the most influential Sufi figure in the entire history of the Indian subcontinent. His ministry of forty years in Delhi — conducted without interruption through successive reigns, political upheavals, and invasions — shaped the spiritual character of a civilization. He did not merely teach a doctrine; he incarnated a way of being in the world, one in which love was the supreme law, service was the supreme worship, and every human being, regardless of birth or station, was worthy of respect and care. Through his disciples, the Chishtiyya spread across every region of India and beyond: Nasir al-Din Chiragh-i Dilli continued the tradition in Delhi; Burhanuddin Gharib established the Deccan Chishtiyya in the south; Akhi Siraj carried it to Bengal in the east. Within a generation of his death, there was scarcely a corner of South Asia untouched by the Chishti message.

His dargah in Delhi — where qawwali has been performed nightly for seven centuries — is not merely a shrine but a living institution that embodies everything Nizamuddin taught. It is a place where music and prayer are one, where the rich and the poor sit together on the same marble floor, where the fragrance of roses and the sound of devotional singing create an atmosphere in which the boundary between this world and the next grows thin. Millions visit each year — Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, seekers of every tradition and none — drawn by the same force that drew the people of fourteenth-century Delhi to a gentle, soft-spoken man in a simple khanqah on the outskirts of the city. In the seven hundred years since his death, that force has not diminished by a single degree. He was called Mahbub-i Ilahi — the Beloved of God — and the evidence for that title is the love that still pours from his tomb like a spring that has never run dry.

Chapter 47

Ibn Ata'illah al-Iskandari

ابن عطاء الله الإسكندري
Full NameAhmad ibn Muhammad ibn Abd al-Karim ibn Ata'illah al-Judhami al-Maliki al-Iskandari
Bornc. 658 AH / 1259 CE, Alexandria (Egypt)
Died709 AH / 1309 CE, Cairo (at al-Azhar, where he taught)
Sufi OrderShadhiliyya (received from Abu al-Abbas al-Mursi, successor of Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili)
TitlesThe Third Pole of the Shadhiliyya, The Tongue of the Shadhili Path, Lisan al-Din (The Tongue of the Faith)
TeachersAbu al-Abbas al-Mursi (primary spiritual master)
Notable StudentsDawud al-Bakhili, Ibn Abbad al-Rundi (through his works, not personal transmission), Taqi al-Din al-Subki; his influence extends to virtually every Shadhili branch
Major Worksal-Hikam al-Ata'iyyah (The Book of Wisdom), Miftah al-Falah wa Misbah al-Arwah (Key to Success and Lamp of Souls), al-Tanwir fi Isqat al-Tadbir (Illumination in Dropping Self-Management), Lata'if al-Minan (Subtle Gifts), Taj al-Arus (The Bride's Crown)
BurialQarafa cemetery, Cairo — his tomb is still visited

Biography

Ibn Ata'illah al-Iskandari was born in Alexandria to a distinguished family of Maliki jurists and scholars. He received the finest classical Islamic education that Egypt could offer — Maliki jurisprudence, hadith transmission, Arabic linguistics, and the formal sciences of the religious establishment. Everything in his upbringing prepared him for a conventional scholarly career in the tradition of his fathers. There was nothing in his background to suggest that he would one day become the voice of the Shadhili mystical tradition and the author of the most widely studied book of Sufi aphorisms in the Arabic language. Indeed, the young Ibn Ata'illah was not merely indifferent to Sufism — he was actively opposed to it. He considered the mystical claims of the Sufis to be dubious innovations, and he directed public criticism at Abu al-Abbas al-Mursi, the leading Shadhili master in Alexandria and the direct successor of Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili himself.

The encounter that shattered this opposition and redirected the entire course of his life became one of the most celebrated conversion stories in Sufi literature. Eventually — perhaps driven by intellectual curiosity, perhaps by a hidden stirring in his heart that he could not yet name — Ibn Ata'illah attended one of al-Mursi's gatherings. He went, by his own later admission, as an opponent. What he heard and saw in that session broke through every barrier that his legal training and theological caution had erected. He later described the experience in a single devastating sentence: "I went to him as his opponent and left as his slave." The transformation was not gradual — it was instantaneous, total, and irreversible. From that session forward, he devoted himself entirely to al-Mursi's guidance, sitting at the master's feet for years, absorbing the Shadhili transmission with the intensity of a man who had wasted precious time and knew it. The lesson embedded in his conversion would become one of his most enduring teachings: the experiential knowledge of the heart cannot be reached from the outside by criticism or intellectual argument; it requires surrender.

After his years of formation under al-Mursi, Ibn Ata'illah moved to Cairo and took up a teaching position at al-Azhar — the most prestigious Islamic institution in the world, the very citadel of formal religious scholarship. This was a remarkable achievement, not for him personally but for the tradition he represented. In bringing the Shadhili teaching into the mainstream of Azhari education, Ibn Ata'illah accomplished something that had eluded many great Sufis before him: the mystical path entered the house of formal Islamic learning through the front door, not as an exotic supplement to the curriculum but as a natural and necessary dimension of religious knowledge. His lectures at al-Azhar drew students who came for jurisprudence and left with something they had not expected — an awakening of the heart. His public debates with Ibn Taymiyya, the great Hanbali theologian and ardent critic of many Sufi practices, became famous in their day. The two scholars represented opposite poles of Islamic thought, yet they engaged in exchanges marked by sharpness of intellect and mutual recognition of sincerity. Ibn Ata'illah reportedly said of his adversary: "I have never met a man more devoted to God — but his knowledge has not been illuminated by love."

It was during his years in Cairo that Ibn Ata'illah composed the work for which he is immortally remembered: the al-Hikam al-Ata'iyyah, a collection of 264 aphorisms that constitutes the most concise and yet most comprehensive guide to the states of the heart ever written in Arabic. He described the process of their composition not as writing but as receiving — the aphorisms came to him as insights from a source beyond his own intellect, and he recorded them. Each one is a gem of such density that it rewards not merely reading but years of contemplation. "Bury your existence in the earth of obscurity — what grows without being planted does not reach its full fruit." "If you want to know your standing with God, look at what He is occupying you with." "The merciful one is he whom God has struck with the whip of affliction." These are not clever sayings; they are compressed transmissions of spiritual experience, each one containing an entire world that opens only to the one who sits with it long enough.

Ibn Ata'illah continued teaching, writing, and guiding disciples at al-Azhar until his death in Cairo in 709 AH (1309 CE). He was buried in the Qarafa cemetery, where his tomb is still visited by seekers and scholars. His life had spanned approximately fifty years — a relatively short span that produced a body of work whose influence on Islamic spirituality has extended across seven centuries and shows no sign of diminishing. He was the third pole of the Shadhiliyya — after al-Shadhili the founder and al-Mursi the transmitter — and it was through his pen that the Shadhili teaching found its permanent and universal voice.

Key Teachings

The central doctrine of the Hikam — and of Ibn Ata'illah's entire spiritual teaching — is isqat al-tadbir, the dropping of self-management. The fundamental disease of the spiritual seeker, he taught, is the belief that through his own planning, effort, and strategic management he can achieve spiritual results. The ego, even when it turns toward God, cannot resist the temptation to direct the process — to set goals, measure progress, devise methods, and congratulate itself on its achievements. But genuine tawakkul — reliance on God — means surrendering the very illusion of control. It means recognizing that the divine arrangement is already unfolding perfectly and that the seeker's frantic efforts to manage his own spiritual life are themselves the primary obstacle to the realization he seeks. "Do not demand from God that He accelerate for you; rather, look at what He is doing for you right now." This is the Hikam's most practical and most subversive wisdom: stop trying to get somewhere, and discover that you are already held.

Inseparable from this teaching is the Shadhili doctrine that affliction is a form of divine attention. "God afflicts the people He loves," Ibn Ata'illah taught, drawing on the deepest currents of Prophetic tradition and Shadhili experience. The Hikam returns to this theme with relentless consistency: divine love expresses itself through both ease and difficulty, through gifts and through trials. The heart that can see God's love only in moments of comfort and success has understood very little. The heart that can recognize His presence equally in affliction — in loss, in failure, in the stripping away of everything the ego clings to — has reached a station of profound intimacy. This is not a counsel of passive resignation; it is a radical reorientation of perception. When affliction comes, the question is not "Why is God punishing me?" but "What is God showing me? What is He freeing me from? What door is He opening by closing this one?" The one who learns to ask these questions has found the key to the entire Hikam.

Famous Stories

The Conversion: The story of Ibn Ata'illah's transformation from public critic to devoted disciple captures the essential character of the Shadhili path — and of spiritual awakening in general. He had been opposing Abu al-Abbas al-Mursi for some time, writing and speaking against him with the confidence of a trained jurist who knew exactly where the boundaries of orthodoxy lay and was certain the Sufis had crossed them. One day — driven by what he later acknowledged was something deeper than mere intellectual curiosity — he entered al-Mursi's gathering and sat down among the listeners. Al-Mursi did not acknowledge him, did not single him out, did not challenge him. He simply spoke of the divine realities as he always did, with the quiet authority of one who has tasted what he describes. Something in those words reached past all of Ibn Ata'illah's carefully constructed defenses. He said later: "My heart turned over. Everything I had thought I knew became, in an instant, a curtain over what I now saw." He sat motionless until the gathering ended. Then he approached al-Mursi, who embraced him warmly, as though he had been expected all along — as though the opposition, the criticism, and the final surrender had all been part of a single divine arrangement. Ibn Ata'illah never left. The scholar who had arrived to judge remained to learn, and the knowledge he received transformed not only his own life but the lives of countless seekers for centuries to come.

The Debate with Ibn Taymiyya: Ibn Taymiyya — the towering Hanbali scholar who vigorously opposed many Sufi practices and whose influence on Islamic reform movements would extend to the present day — and Ibn Ata'illah engaged in famous public exchanges in Cairo. The two men were giants of their respective traditions: Ibn Taymiyya representing the strictest adherence to textual evidence and the rejection of all that he deemed innovation, Ibn Ata'illah representing the Sufi conviction that the inner dimension of the faith is as essential as its outward forms. In one celebrated exchange, Ibn Taymiyya challenged him: "How can you claim that affliction is a form of divine love? Does a loving God afflict His servants?" Ibn Ata'illah replied without hesitation: "How do you explain the Prophet, peace be upon him, who suffered more than any of us? Does a father who pushes his child to walk, though the child falls, not love his child? The push IS the love." Ibn Taymiyya fell silent for a long moment, then reportedly said: "This man has been given the keys to the heart." Despite their irreconcilable disagreements on many points of doctrine and practice, each acknowledged the other's sincerity and devotion to God. Their exchanges remain a model of how profound disagreement can coexist with mutual respect — and a reminder that the house of Islam is large enough to contain both the rigor of the jurist and the tenderness of the mystic.

Major Works

  • al-Hikam al-Ata'iyyah (The Book of Wisdom): 264 aphorisms organized into a progressive guide to the states of the heart — from the initial awakening of the seeker through the stations of repentance, reliance, gratitude, and affliction, to the highest degrees of intimacy with God. It is the most studied Sufi text in the Arabic-speaking world, and hundreds of commentaries have been written on it across the centuries.
  • Miftah al-Falah wa Misbah al-Arwah (Key to Success and Lamp of Souls): A comprehensive guide to the practice of dhikr — the remembrance of God — covering its conditions, methods, benefits, and progressive stages. It serves as the practical companion to the Hikam's theoretical insights.
  • al-Tanwir fi Isqat al-Tadbir (Illumination in Dropping Self-Management): A short but profound treatise that develops the Shadhili doctrine of complete surrender to divine arrangement — the abandonment of the ego's compulsive need to plan and control, and the discovery of trust in what God has already ordained.
  • Lata'if al-Minan (Subtle Gifts): Biographical accounts of Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili and Abu al-Abbas al-Mursi, preserving their teachings, stories, and spiritual counsel. It is the primary source for information about the first two masters of the Shadhili lineage and thus an indispensable document of the order's founding generation.

Legacy

The Hikam al-Ata'iyyah is perhaps the most widely studied single text in Islamic spirituality after al-Ghazali's Ihya Ulum al-Din — and in some regions of the Muslim world, it surpasses even that great work in its influence on daily spiritual practice. Its 264 aphorisms are memorized by seekers across the Islamic world, from the zawiyyas of Morocco to the pesantren of Indonesia, from the study circles of Cairo to the Sufi gatherings of West Africa. Dozens of major commentaries have been written on the Hikam, each one a work of scholarship and spiritual insight in its own right — the commentaries of Ibn Abbad al-Rundi, Ahmad Zarruq, and Ibn Ajiba among the most celebrated — ensuring that the Shadhili teaching is transmitted afresh to each new generation. The text remains in active use in religious education throughout the Arab world, not as a historical curiosity but as a living guide to the heart's journey.

Ibn Ata'illah represents the Shadhili ideal at its purest: the union of rigorous scholarly training with deep spiritual experience, of intellectual precision with the tenderness of a heart opened by divine love. He demonstrated — in his own person and in the institution of his teaching at al-Azhar — that mysticism and orthodox scholarship need not be adversaries, that the same mind that masters the intricacies of Maliki jurisprudence can also receive the illumination of the Hikam. This model has made the Shadhiliyya one of the most intellectually respectable and widely embraced Sufi traditions in the Islamic world. Its influence in the modern era is enormous — particularly in North Africa and Egypt, but increasingly in the West as well — and it is largely through the Hikam that this influence is exercised. Seven centuries after his death, Ibn Ata'illah's aphorisms continue to do what they have always done: they meet the reader exactly where he is, speak to the precise condition of his heart, and point — with the gentleness and precision of a master physician — toward the cure.

Chapter 48

Amir Khusraw Dehlawi

أمير خسرو دهلوي
Full NameAbu al-Hasan Yamin al-Din Khusraw ibn Sayfuddin Mahmud (Amir Khusraw)
Born651 AH / 1253 CE, Patiyali (near Etah, Uttar Pradesh, India)
Died725 AH / 1325 CE, Delhi
Sufi OrderChishtiyya — devoted disciple of Nizamuddin Auliya
TitlesTuti-yi Hind (The Parrot of India), Khusraw of Khusraws, Amir (Prince)
TeachersNizamuddin Auliya (primary spiritual master)
Major WorksKhamsa (five long poems), Ghurrat al-Kamal, Nuh Sipehr, Tughluq-nama, Khazain al-Futuh; credited with developing khayal singing, the tabla, the sitar (or its ancestor), and qawwali as a devotional form; his Hindi/Urdu poetry makes him a pioneer of both Urdu and Braj Bhasha literary traditions
BurialDelhi — his tomb lies at the feet of Nizamuddin Auliya's dargah, exactly as he requested

Biography

Born to a father of Turkish origin who had come to India in service of the Delhi Sultanate, Amir Khusraw was raised in an atmosphere of military service and courtly culture. His father died when Khusraw was only eight years old, and the boy was raised thereafter by his maternal grandfather. He showed extraordinary literary gifts from childhood, composing creditable poetry by the age of eight and becoming recognized as a significant poet in his teens. His reputation grew rapidly in the circles of Delhi's nobility, and he entered the service of various Sultanate patrons, eventually becoming court poet to no fewer than seven Delhi sultans over the course of his long career — a record of literary longevity and political survival without parallel in the annals of Indo-Persian culture.

His encounter with Nizamuddin Auliya changed the direction of his life irrevocably. Khusraw was in his twenties when he presented himself at the khanqah of the great Chishti master and was accepted as a disciple. From that moment, his relationship with his shaykh became the central fact of his existence — deeper than any political loyalty, more consuming than any literary ambition. He served the Delhi sultans as a poet-historian, chronicling their conquests and celebrating their courts, but he lived his inner life at Nizamuddin's feet. The contrast between his two lives — the brilliant courtier who moved through palaces draped in silk, and the devoted mureed who sat in humble submission before his master — gave his poetry a unique double depth, a luminous tension between the world's splendor and the soul's longing.

Amir Khusraw's musical innovations represent one of the most far-reaching cultural contributions of any individual in South Asian history. He is traditionally credited with developing the khayal style of classical singing — more improvisatory and emotionally expressive than the older dhrupad style — and with adapting or creating the tabla, the paired drums that became fundamental to Hindustani music. He is also credited with developing an ancestor of the sitar, the instrument that would become the very emblem of North Indian classical music. Most significantly, he is credited with creating qawwali as a formal devotional genre, taking the Persian traditions of sama' and the local musical idioms of India and fusing them into the powerful, ecstatic devotional form that is still performed nightly at Sufi shrines across South Asia and has now reached audiences across the globe.

His literary output in Persian was staggering in both volume and quality: five long poems modeled on Nizami's Khamsa but adapted to Indian themes and sensibilities; four Persian divans totaling approximately five hundred thousand verses; historical poems recording the dramatic events of the Delhi Sultanate; and a vast output of riddles, letters, and occasional verse that displayed his bottomless facility with language. In the Hindi vernacular, his contributions were equally remarkable. He composed in Braj Bhasha, the forerunner of Hindi, and his verses in that language represent some of the earliest surviving literary Braj. He also composed in a mixed Hindi-Persian style that foreshadowed the development of Urdu as a literary language. He is therefore claimed as a founding father by both the Persian-language tradition and the Urdu-Hindi tradition — a range of literary parentage unmatched by any other poet.

When Nizamuddin Auliya died on 18 Rabi' al-Awwal 725 AH (April 3, 1325), Amir Khusraw was away from Delhi on a royal military campaign. When the devastating news reached him, he returned immediately. He sold all his possessions, distributed the proceeds to the poor, gave away his royal garments, dressed himself in the rough clothing of a dervish, and reportedly let his hair grow unkempt in mourning. He visited his master's tomb daily, weeping and reciting poetry of heartbreaking beauty. The elegant court poet who had charmed seven sultans was now a broken man whose only desire was to remain near the one he loved. He died approximately six months after his master. His last request was to be buried at his master's feet — a wish that was honored. He rests there still, exactly where he wished to be, at the dargah of Nizamuddin Auliya in Delhi.

Key Teachings

Amir Khusraw's life was itself his greatest teaching: the unity of spiritual love and artistic creation. He demonstrated through decades of lived practice that the highest artistic expression is inseparable from spiritual love — that the two are not merely compatible but are, at their deepest level, identical. His poetry, whether in Persian, Hindi, or the mixed style, was not composed for fame or patronage alone but arose from the overflow of divine love received through his master. "I am a singer; my songs are prayers," he declared. "My master's love has taught me that every beautiful thing points to the Most Beautiful." For Khusraw, the beauty of a well-turned verse, the rapture of a perfectly sustained musical phrase, and the ecstasy of the lover in the divine presence were all manifestations of the same reality — the irresistible self-disclosure of God through the medium of beauty.

His development of qawwali was not merely an artistic innovation but a deliberate method of spiritual transmission. Khusraw understood, from his own experience in the khanqah of Nizamuddin, that music could break open the heart and prepare it to receive the divine love that the Chishti masters taught. The qawwali gathering was not entertainment but a spiritual technology — a carefully constructed environment in which the power of melody, rhythm, and sacred text combined to induce states of spiritual opening in the listeners. This was the Chishti use of sama' brought to its highest development: music not as an accompaniment to the spiritual life but as one of its most potent instruments. Khusraw invested his genius in both the musical forms and the devotional texts of qawwali precisely because he saw them as vehicles of grace, capable of transmitting in a single evening what years of study alone might fail to achieve.

Famous Stories

The Stolen Cap: One day Khusraw arrived at the khanqah and found his master Nizamuddin Auliya sitting outdoors, apparently dozing, with his cap set beside him. In a playful act of devoted love — the kind of intimate spontaneity that characterized their relationship — Khusraw stole the cap and hid it behind his back. When Nizamuddin opened his eyes and noticed the missing cap, he called out: "Who has taken my cap?" Khusraw stepped forward, laughing. The master smiled and said: "Write me a poem for it." On the spot, without a moment's hesitation, Khusraw composed a quatrain of such beauty and tenderness that it became one of the most celebrated short poems in Urdu-Hindi literary history. Nizamuddin took back his cap and embraced his disciple. The story became an icon of the Chishti tradition — an emblem of the loving intimacy between master and disciple, the playfulness that can exist alongside the most profound devotion, and the spontaneous overflow of divine love into poetry.

The Six-Month Vigil: After Nizamuddin's death, eyewitnesses described a transformation in Khusraw so complete that those who had known him at court could scarcely recognize the man at the tomb. The elegant, fashionably dressed poet who had graced the gatherings of seven sultans was now seen at the shrine daily, his hair unkempt, wearing the white garments of mourning, reciting Hindi verses of heartbreaking beauty. Among these was the famous couplet: "Gori sove sej par, mukh par dale kes / Chal Khusrau ghar aapne, rein bhai sab des" — "My beloved sleeps on the bed with her face covered in hair / Khusraw, go home — night has fallen over all the land." The beloved asleep is the master gone; the night that has fallen is the darkness of a world without his presence. Khusraw died within months, apparently consumed by grief, and was buried exactly where he had wished — at his master's feet, as if to say that even in death, the disciple's only desire was nearness.

Major Works

  • Khamsa: Five long romantic and epic poems in Persian, modeled on Nizami's celebrated Khamsa but reimagined with Indian themes, settings, and sensibilities — including Hasht Bihisht, Matla' al-Anwar, Shirin wa Khusraw, Majnun wa Layla, and A'ina-yi Iskandari.
  • Tuhfa al-Sighar, Wastah al-Hayat, Ghurrat al-Kamal, Nihayat al-Kamal: Four Persian divans spanning his creative life, together comprising approximately half a million verses — an output that ranks him among the most prolific poets in the history of world literature.
  • Nuh Sipehr (Nine Spheres): A remarkable long poem containing the earliest sustained and detailed praise of India and Indian culture in Persian poetry — a document of extraordinary cross-cultural appreciation and a founding text of Indo-Persian literary identity.
  • I'jaz-i Khusrawi: A masterwork of Persian epistolary prose, demonstrating his command of artistic prose as well as verse.
  • Qawwali compositions: Many of the most beloved qawwali texts are attributed to Khusraw, including the famous Chaap Tilak in Hindi, which remains in the active performance repertoire at Sufi shrines and concert halls across the world.

Legacy

Amir Khusraw Dehlawi is simultaneously one of the greatest Persian poets, the father of Hindustani classical music, the creator of qawwali as a devotional form, and a pioneer of both Urdu and Hindi literature — a range of achievement unmatched by any other figure in the cultural history of South Asia. His qawwali compositions, particularly those in Hindi, are still performed nightly at Sufi shrines from Ajmer to Lahore; his music has reached global audiences through artists like Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan and the Sabri Brothers, who carried the tradition he created to concert halls in London, Paris, and New York. In Pakistani and Indian culture alike, he is claimed as an essential part of the national inheritance — his verses are learned by schoolchildren, his compositions performed at weddings and festivals, his spiritual poems recited at shrines. He belongs to no single nation because he belongs to the entire civilization that stretches from Delhi to Bukhara, from the Ganges to the Indus.

His burial at Nizamuddin's feet is perhaps the most eloquent symbol of the Chishti ideal of love. It is not the love of equals meeting on level ground but the surrender of the disciple who asks only to remain near his master, in death as in life. Every day, pilgrims who visit the great dargah of Nizamuddin Auliya in Delhi pass first by the tomb of Amir Khusraw — the poet who lies where he wished to lie, the singer whose songs are still prayers, the parrot of India whose voice, seven centuries after it fell silent, has never ceased to be heard.

Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani

عبد الخالق الغجدواني
Full NameAbu Abd al-Rahman Khwaja Abdul Khaliq ibn Abd al-Jamil al-Ghujduwani
Bornc. 490 AH / 1097 CE, Ghujduwan (near Bukhara, present-day Uzbekistan)
Died575 AH / 1179 CE, Ghujduwan
Sufi OrderThe Khwajagan (The Masters) — the Central Asian mystical tradition that became the direct precursor of the Naqshbandiyya
TitlesKhwaja-i Buzurg (The Great Khwaja), Arif-i Rabbani (The Lordly Gnostic), Master of the Silent Path
TeachersYusuf al-Hamadhani (primary); al-Khidr — who, according to the strongest tradition within the order, appeared to him in childhood and taught him the method of silent dhikr directly
Notable StudentsKhwaja Awliya-yi Kabir, Khwaja Ahmad Siddiq; his eight principles were transmitted through the Khwajagan chain and formed the foundation of Naqshbandi practice
BurialGhujduwan, near Bukhara — his tomb remains a place of pilgrimage

Biography

Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani was born in Ghujduwan, a small town set amid the irrigated farmlands near the great city of Bukhara in Central Asia — a region that for centuries had been one of the luminous centers of Islamic scholarship and mystical practice. According to the foundational narrative of the Naqshbandi tradition, Abdul Khaliq's spiritual life began not with an earthly teacher but with an extraordinary visitation: the prophet al-Khidr — the mysterious "Green One" mentioned in the Quran (18:65-82), associated with hidden spiritual knowledge that transcends the ordinary channels of transmission — appeared to him in childhood and taught him the method of dhikr al-qalb, the silent remembrance of the heart, directly and without earthly intermediary. This Uwaysi-style initiation — receiving spiritual instruction from a source beyond the visible chain of living teachers — established Abdul Khaliq as the recipient of a direct prophetic transmission, linking him to a knowledge that had passed from the Prophet Muhammad through Abu Bakr al-Siddiq and thence through the hidden channels of the spirit to its new custodian.

He subsequently studied under the great Khwaja Yusuf al-Hamadhani, the towering Central Asian master who was also the teacher of Ahmad Yasawi and the acknowledged head of the Khwajagan lineage. Under Yusuf al-Hamadhani, Abdul Khaliq received the formal training and the outward confirmation that complemented his inner initiation. But it was the method received from al-Khidr that became his primary and distinctive contribution to the tradition: the silent, internal dhikr of the heart (dhikr al-khafi), as opposed to the vocal, rhythmic dhikr practiced by most Sufi orders. Where other traditions sought God through the sound of His name on the tongue, Abdul Khaliq taught that the deepest remembrance occurs in the silent chamber of the heart, beyond the reach of sound, in a stillness that mirrors the divine silence from which all creation emerged. This "hidden dhikr" would become the defining characteristic of the Naqshbandiyya — the feature that distinguishes it from every other Sufi order.

His greatest contribution to the science of the path was the formulation of the Eight Principles (Usul al-Thamaniya) of the Khwajagan way — eight phrases in Persian that encapsulate the complete methodology of spiritual practice as understood by this lineage: Hush dar dam (Awareness in the breath — maintaining consciousness of God with every inhalation and exhalation); Nazar bar qadam (Watch your step — attending to each moment and action with full presence); Safar dar watan (Journey in the homeland — the inner journey toward God while outwardly remaining at home); Khalwat dar anjuman (Solitude in the crowd — inner isolation while outwardly engaged with people); Yad kard (Remembrance — the continuous practice of dhikr); Baz gasht (Return — returning to God in every moment of distraction); Nigahdasht (Watchfulness — guarding the heart from all that is other than God); and Yad dasht (Presence of heart — the unbroken consciousness of the divine presence).

These eight principles would later be expanded to eleven by Baha al-Din Naqshband, but the core eight remained the foundation upon which the entire Naqshbandi edifice was built. What is remarkable about them is their emphasis on continuity and integration — they do not describe special practices for retreat or seclusion but rather the transformation of every moment of ordinary life into a moment of worship. "Solitude in the crowd" is the master principle: the perfect practitioner maintains inner isolation from everything other than God while remaining outwardly and fully engaged with the world of commerce, scholarship, family, and governance. This emphasis on inner withdrawal combined with outer engagement would make the Naqshbandiyya uniquely suited to the world of affairs — and would produce a tradition of Sufi masters who were not recluses but statesmen, scholars, and social leaders.

Key Teachings

Abdul Khaliq taught that silent dhikr (dhikr al-khafi) is the supreme method of spiritual realization. Unlike the vocal dhikr of the Qadiri and Chishti traditions — which employs the tongue, the breath, and often physical movement to invoke the divine names — Abdul Khaliq insisted that the most powerful form of divine remembrance is the silent, internal repetition that occurs in the heart alone, invisible and inaudible to all but God. He argued that the heart's silent remembrance reflects the divine mystery itself: God works in silence; His most powerful acts are invisible; creation itself emerged from a stillness beyond all sound. "The tongue that speaks of God in public courts admiration," he taught. "The heart that remembers God in silence courts God." This was not a rejection of vocal dhikr — he acknowledged its validity for beginners and for those whose temperament inclined them toward it — but an insistence that the highest and most intimate form of communion with the divine occurs in the depths of the heart, beyond the reach of any external expression.

Equally fundamental was his teaching on the integration of spiritual practice with worldly life. The Eight Principles are specifically and deliberately designed for people who live in the world — who work, trade, govern, raise families, and move through the press of human society. The Khwajagan ideal, which the Naqshbandiyya inherited, is not the hermit in his cave or the dervish in his wandering but the fully engaged participant in the life of the community who maintains, at every moment and in every situation, an unbroken inner awareness of the divine presence. The shopkeeper weighing grain, the judge hearing a case, the scholar reading a text — each is to maintain the silent dhikr of the heart without a moment's interruption. This principle gave the Khwajagan-Naqshbandi tradition its distinctive flavor: a sober, engaged, worldly spirituality that sought God not by fleeing the world but by finding Him in every corner of it.

Famous Stories

The Teaching of al-Khidr: According to the Naqshbandi tradition, the young Abdul Khaliq was reciting the Quran with his teacher when he came to the verse (18:65): "And We taught him a knowledge from Our presence" — referring to al-Khidr. The boy paused and asked his teacher with the directness of childhood: "Is al-Khidr still alive? Could one still receive knowledge from him?" His teacher replied: "Yes, but this is exceedingly rare — it is granted only to those whom God chooses." That night, the young Abdul Khaliq performed the prayer of need (salat al-haja) and called upon God with the intensity of a heart that would accept no refusal. Al-Khidr appeared to him — a luminous figure of indeterminate age, radiating a green light. He said to the boy: "Open your mouth." Abdul Khaliq opened it, and al-Khidr breathed into it three times. Then he taught him the method of the silent heart-dhikr — the remembrance that requires no tongue, no sound, no movement, only the turning of the heart in perpetual awareness toward its Lord. And he told him: "This method was transmitted from the Prophet, peace be upon him, through Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, and now reaches you. Guard it, for it is the most precious thing that can be entrusted to a human heart."

The Principle of "Solitude in the Crowd": A student once came to Abdul Khaliq in perplexity and asked: "Master, how can one be inwardly alone while surrounded by people? The marketplace is full of noise, the mosque is full of worshippers, the home is full of family. Where is solitude to be found?" Abdul Khaliq replied: "Stand in the marketplace at the busiest hour of the day, when the noise is at its height and the press of bodies at its densest. Close your eyes for one moment and notice: the crowd disappears. Only your heart and its awareness of God remain. Now open your eyes — the crowd returns, but the awareness remains. This is khalwat dar anjuman: your inner solitude is not disturbed by the outer crowd, because you were never really with the crowd in the first place. Your body stands among them; your heart stands before God. When this becomes your permanent state — not a moment's exercise but the unbroken condition of your life — then you have understood the principle."

Legacy

Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani is the architect of the Naqshbandiyya's distinctive methodology — the silent dhikr and the Eight Principles that he formulated in a small town near Bukhara in the twelfth century are still practiced daily by millions of Naqshbandi followers across the globe, from the villages of Central Asia to the cities of Turkey, from the mountains of the Caucasus to the plains of India, from Southeast Asian archipelagos to immigrant communities in Europe and North America. The chain of transmission that he received from al-Khidr and passed through the Khwajagan masters across generations to Baha al-Din Naqshband — a transmission spanning two centuries and carried through both living teachers and Uwaysi contact with the spirits of the departed — established the Naqshbandiyya's claim to a uniquely direct prophetic lineage, one that traces itself not merely through a chain of human teachers but through the mysterious channels of divine election.

His emphasis on inner withdrawal combined with outer engagement has made the Naqshbandiyya the most politically active and socially engaged of all Sufi orders. Where other traditions have tended toward withdrawal from the world, the Naqshbandiyya has consistently produced masters who guided rulers, counseled statesmen, led popular movements, and shaped the political destiny of nations. From the courts of the Timurid and Mughal empires to the resistance of Imam Shamil against the Russian conquest of the Caucasus, from the struggle against Soviet atheism in Central Asia to the preservation of Islamic identity in modern China — wherever Naqshbandi masters have acted in the world, they have done so on the foundation that Abdul Khaliq laid: the conviction that the highest spirituality does not flee the world but transforms it from within, one silent heartbeat at a time.

Baha al-Din Naqshband

Biography

Baha al-Din Naqshband was born in Qasr al-Arifan — literally, "the Castle of the Gnostics" — a village near the great city of Bukhara that already bore the fragrance of centuries of Khwajagan spiritual tradition. His birth was foretold. The aged master Muhammad Baba al-Sammasi, who was the living head of the Khwajagan chain, visited the village before Baha al-Din's birth and declared: "From this village I smell the fragrance of a great friend of God who is to be born here." When the child was born, Sammasi took the infant on his lap and said with quiet certainty: "This child belongs to me." From the earliest days of his life, Baha al-Din's spiritual formation was not left to chance but was deliberately guided by the masters of a tradition that had been preparing for his arrival.

His formal spiritual training proceeded through two distinct channels, and the tension between them shaped the character of his teaching and his order. The first channel was the outer, living lineage: he received the disciplines of the path from Muhammad Baba al-Sammasi, and after Sammasi's death from Amir Kulal, both of whom stood in the Khwajagan chain descending through Yusuf al-Hamadhani. Under Amir Kulal, he underwent years of rigorous training in service, obedience, and the practices of the order. But Baha al-Din consistently reported that his primary and most essential instruction came from a source beyond the visible: Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani, who had died approximately two hundred years earlier, appeared to him in the Uwaysi mode — spirit to spirit, across the gulf of centuries — and taught him the silent dhikr of the heart, the method that had been received from al-Khidr and transmitted through the hidden channels of the unseen. This cross-time transmission, in which a master dead for two centuries became the living guide of a disciple not yet born when he departed the world, gave the Naqshbandiyya its distinctive character and its claim to a particularly elevated spiritual lineage.

The name "Naqshband" bears two meanings, and both illuminate the man who carried it. The literal meaning is "pattern-maker" or "engraver" — one who imprints a design upon a surface. Baha al-Din is reported to have worked as a craftsman in his youth, engraving patterns in metal, and he always insisted on earning his living through honest manual labor throughout his life. But the spiritual meaning of the name is "one who engraves the divine name upon the heart" — one whose remembrance of God is not a passing thought or an occasional practice but a permanent inscription, as indelible as a pattern carved in silver. His masters gave him this name to indicate his supreme capacity: the ability to engrave the divine remembrance so deeply upon the hearts of his disciples that it could never be effaced. He refused to accept gifts or payments for his spiritual guidance, insisting that the transmission of sacred knowledge must remain free from the taint of worldly exchange.

His spiritual methodology brought together the silent heart-dhikr of the Ghujduwani tradition with a set of additional principles, expanding the original eight to eleven, which became the complete methodological framework of the Naqshbandiyya. He placed particular emphasis on khalwat dar anjuman (solitude in the crowd) and on wuquf-i qalbi (the stopping of the heart) — the extraordinary experience in which the heart pauses, as it were, in the divine presence, suspended between beats in a moment of pure awareness that transcends time. His gatherings were notably different from those of other Sufi masters of his era: he preferred simple, unpretentious settings to elaborate khanqahs; he rejected the use of sama' (devotional music), insisting that inner states should be cultivated through silent remembrance rather than external stimulation; and he saw his role as that of a guide and companion, not a performer of miraculous spiritual states.

His influence reached the highest levels of the age. He was revered by Timur — Tamerlane — the most powerful ruler of the fourteenth-century world, a conqueror whose empire stretched from Anatolia to India. That such a man should seek the counsel and blessing of a craftsman-mystic from a village near Bukhara is itself a testament to the authority that Baha al-Din's spiritual presence commanded. After his death on 3 Rabi' al-Awwal 791 AH (March 2, 1389), the Naqshbandiyya spread with extraordinary rapidity across Central Asia, Persia, India, the Ottoman world, China, and Southeast Asia — becoming the most geographically extensive and politically engaged Sufi order in the history of Islam, a legacy that continues to unfold in the present day.

Key Teachings

Baha al-Din's supreme contribution was the consolidation of the Khwajagan silent dhikr into a complete and transmissible system of spiritual realization. The Eleven Principles of the Naqshbandiyya — expanding Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani's original eight with three additional principles (wuquf-i zamani, awareness of how one's time is spent; wuquf-i adadi, awareness of the number of repetitions in dhikr; and wuquf-i qalbi, the heart's pause in the divine presence) — constitute a comprehensive map of the spiritual life as the Naqshbandi tradition understands it. Each principle describes an aspect of the continuous, unbroken awareness of God that the perfected practitioner maintains in every moment and in every action, whether performing the ritual prayer or conducting business in the marketplace, whether sitting in solitary meditation or moving through the densest human crowd. Taken together, the eleven principles amount to nothing less than the transformation of the entirety of human experience into an uninterrupted act of worship.

A defining feature of Baha al-Din's teaching was his rejection of sama' — the devotional music that was central to the practice of the Chishtiyya and other Sufi orders. Where the Chishtis held that music could shatter the barriers of the ego and open the heart to divine love, Baha al-Din insisted that authentic inner states should arise from the silent work of the heart alone, not from external stimulation. This was not a condemnation of music per se but a methodological conviction: the Naqshbandi path cultivates a sobriety and inwardness that guards against dependence on any external means. The practitioner must learn to find God in silence, in the ordinary, in the undramatic moments of daily existence — not in the extraordinary heights of ecstatic experience. This difference in method has remained one of the most significant points of contrast between the Naqshbandiyya and the Chishtiyya, the two great Sufi traditions that have shaped the spiritual landscape of South and Central Asia.

Famous Stories

The Three-Year Silence: During one period of his spiritual training under Amir Kulal, Baha al-Din was instructed to serve the dervishes of the khanqah in the most menial capacity — cooking their meals, cleaning their quarters, carrying water from the well, scrubbing the latrines. He was to perform these tasks for three years without complaint, without asking when the period would end, and without seeking any recognition or advancement. Baha al-Din undertook this service with an absorption so complete that visitors to the khanqah would remark upon the light that seemed to emanate from his face even as he performed the most humble labor. He cleaned the latrines with the same attention and the same inner joy as he performed the ritual prayer, for in his understanding there was no distinction — both were acts of worship, both were performed in the presence of God. At the end of the three years, Amir Kulal called him forward before the assembled dervishes and said: "The one who serves the servants of God has served God Himself. You have learned the foundation upon which everything else will be built."

The Words That Made Him Famous: Baha al-Din's most celebrated teaching is encapsulated in a single phrase that became the motto of the entire Naqshbandi order: "Tariqatuna al-suhbah, wa al-khayr fi al-jam'iyyah" — "Our path is companionship, and the good is in the gathering of hearts." This deceptively simple statement contains the essence of the Naqshbandi method. The path is transmitted not primarily through texts, techniques, or rituals but through suhba — the transformative spiritual companionship of master and disciple, the subtle and mysterious influence that passes from a perfected heart to a receptive one through the medium of simple presence. And the good — the grace, the blessing, the transformative power — resides not in any individual achievement but in the gathering of sincere hearts oriented toward God. When such hearts come together, something greater than any individual emerges: a collective spiritual field in which the divine presence becomes palpable. This teaching made the Naqshbandi gathering — quiet, sober, without music or ecstatic movement — one of the most powerful vehicles of spiritual transmission in the Islamic world.

Visiting the Graves of the Khwajagan: Throughout his life, Baha al-Din made a regular practice of visiting the graves of the earlier Khwajagan masters — particularly the tomb of Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani, the architect of the silent dhikr. At these graves, he reported, the connection with the departed masters was not merely commemorative but living and active: he received guidance, correction, and fresh spiritual knowledge from the spirits of those who had passed from this world. The graves were not memorials but meeting places. He told his disciples: "Visit the graves of your spiritual ancestors. Sit with them. Speak to them. The connection does not end at death — it deepens. They see more clearly now than they did in the life of the body, and their love for those who walk their path has not diminished but grown." This practice — the cultivation of a living relationship with the departed masters of the lineage — became a hallmark of the Naqshbandi tradition and an expression of its fundamental conviction that the spiritual chain is not merely a historical record but an active, living reality that transcends the boundary between the living and the dead.

Major Works

  • Baha al-Din deliberately left no written works, preferring direct oral transmission through suhba — the transformative companionship of master and disciple. He distrusted the written word as a vehicle for conveying the deepest realities of the path, insisting that what passes between hearts cannot be captured on paper.
  • Risala-yi Qudsiyya (Holy Treatise): His teachings were preserved by his closest disciple Muhammad Parsa, who recorded his master's sayings, spiritual instructions, and accounts of his states in this foundational text of the Naqshbandi tradition.
  • The Eleven Principles of the Naqshbandiyya: Formulated in his tradition and attributed jointly to him and to Abdul Khaliq al-Ghujduwani, these principles constitute the complete methodological framework of the order and remain the basis of Naqshbandi practice worldwide.

Legacy

The Naqshbandiyya became, under Baha al-Din's impetus, the most politically engaged and geographically extensive Sufi order in the history of Islam. Its major branches — the Mujaddidiyya, founded by Ahmad Sirhindi in Mughal India; the Khalidiyya, founded by Khalid al-Baghdadi and spreading across the entire Ottoman world; and numerous other regional branches — span from Morocco to China, from Siberia to Southeast Asia. In each of these regions, the Naqshbandiyya has played a role that goes far beyond the private cultivation of mystical states: Naqshbandi masters have guided sultans and emperors, led popular uprisings, resisted colonial and communist oppression, and shaped the political destiny of nations. Imam Shamil led the Naqshbandi resistance to Russian conquest of the Caucasus for twenty-five years; Naqshbandi networks sustained Islamic identity through decades of Soviet suppression in Central Asia; and in modern Turkey, China, India, and the Malay world, the order continues to serve as a living channel of spiritual guidance and social cohesion.

Baha al-Din's tomb in Qasr al-Arifan, near Bukhara, is the most important pilgrimage site of the Naqshbandi world. The magnificent mosque-complex built there during the Timurid period — a testament to the reverence in which the master was held by the rulers who followed Timur — was carefully preserved even during the Soviet era, when nearly every other expression of Islamic life in Central Asia was suppressed or destroyed. Today it attracts pilgrims from across the Muslim world: Turks and Uzbeks, Malays and Pakistanis, Kurds and Chechens — all drawn to the grave of the quiet craftsman from a village near Bukhara who engraved the divine name so deeply upon the hearts of his disciples that it has never been effaced. His motto echoes still wherever Naqshbandis gather: "Our path is companionship, and the good is in the gathering of hearts."

Biography

In the arid valleys of Hadhramaut — that long, narrow corridor of palms and limestone that cuts through the southern Arabian plateau — there arose in the late twelfth century a man whose decision at a single crossroads would reshape the spiritual landscape of the Indian Ocean world for eight centuries. Muhammad ibn Ali Ba'alawi, known to posterity as al-Faqih al-Muqaddam (the Foremost Jurist), was born in Tarim in 574 AH / 1178 CE into a family whose lineage traced, through an unbroken chain of scholars and saints, to the Prophet Muhammad through his grandson al-Husayn. The Banu Alawi of Hadhramaut were a community of sayyids who had arrived in the valley generations earlier from Basra; they had built, in Tarim and the surrounding towns, a civilization of learning and piety that was at once deeply rooted in the Shafi'i legal tradition and increasingly drawn toward the Sufi interior sciences.

Al-Faqih al-Muqaddam received a complete Islamic education in Hadhramaut, becoming a jurist (faqih) of recognized excellence — hence his title. But his ambitions extended beyond jurisprudence. He traveled to Mecca and Medina, then the great nodes of Islamic learning, where he sought out not only masters of hadith and Quran but the spiritual transmitters whose silsilas connected him to the prophetic interior. He received authorization in the Sufi path from multiple teachers, most notably from masters who transmitted what the Ba'alawi tradition would later call the "way of the Messenger" — a path oriented not around any single later Sufi master's method but around direct modeling of the Prophet's own inner life as preserved in the living chain of transmission.

The decisive moment of his career came upon his return to Hadhramaut. The Banu Alawi had long combined religious authority with some degree of tribal engagement, and weapons were a normal part of the social world in which they moved. The transmitted account records that al-Faqih al-Muqaddam received a spiritual command — variously described as coming through a dream, a vision, or the insistence of his teachers — to renounce the sword entirely. In an act of irreversible self-definition, he broke his sword, publicly and permanently placing himself and his lineage on the side of the spiritual sciences rather than armed power. "We shall conquer," the tradition records him as saying, "with the Quran and with spiritual states, not with the sword."

This act was not mere theater. It redefined the identity of the Banu Alawi community for all subsequent generations. The Hadhrami sayyids would spread across the Indian Ocean world — to East Africa, Southeast Asia, South Asia, and the Swahili coast — not as warriors or merchants of force but as bearers of prophetic lineage, spiritual guidance, and Islamic learning. The model of the scholar-saint who travels, teaches, marries into local communities, and transmits the Ba'alawi path peacefully became the mechanism of one of the most remarkable expansions of Islam in history. Al-Faqih al-Muqaddam did not cause this diaspora himself, but he gave it its spiritual grammar.

He spent the remaining decades of his life in Tarim, teaching and guiding a community whose interior formation became the central concern of his effort. He died in 653 AH / 1255 CE and was buried in the Zanbal cemetery, which would over subsequent centuries become a site of pilgrimage for those who traced their spiritual lineage to him — a lineage that would eventually include millions of Muslims across four continents.

Key Teachings

Al-Faqih al-Muqaddam's central teaching was that the prophetic example (Sunnah) contained within it an interior dimension that could only be transmitted through a living chain of qualified masters. The exterior of the Sunnah — the prayers, the legal rulings, the ethical conduct — was accessible through the scholars and jurists. But the interior — the spiritual states, the direct experience of divine proximity, the transformations of character that the Prophet's companions had undergone in his presence — was transmitted through the silsila, the chain of hearts connected to the prophetic heart. His breaking of the sword was the symbolic enactment of this conviction: that the real power was spiritual, not physical.

He taught that the path of the Banu Alawi was not a new spiritual method invented by later masters but a direct continuation of the prophetic way, purified of later additions and excesses. The Alawi tariqa was, in his conception, not one Sufi order among many but the original order — the method of the Prophet himself, preserved in Hadhramaut and carried outward through those who bore his blood and his spiritual transmission. Whether or not this claim is historically provable, it gave the Ba'alawi tradition a unique self-understanding: a combination of prophetic lineage, legal rigor, and spiritual depth that distinguished it from orders that traced their authority to later masters.

Famous Stories

The Breaking of the Sword: The transmission preserves the account in this form: Al-Faqih al-Muqaddam had returned from his journeys and was considering how best to serve his community. His teachers had told him that the full transmission of the path required a complete commitment — that the way of the sword and the way of the spirit could not coexist in the same hand. He took his sword out into the open air of Tarim, held it for a moment, then broke it. He is reported to have said: "With this sword I have opened the door of the inner path." His students understood that he meant not destruction but transformation — the warrior's energy redirected entirely toward the conquest of the lower self. The broken sword became, in Ba'alawi tradition, the symbol of a community that chose the harder warfare.

Major Works

  • The Alawi Methodological Inheritance: Though al-Faqih al-Muqaddam left no single written text that has been universally attributed to him, he is credited with the transmission and articulation of what became known as the "Alawi method" (al-tariq al-Alawiyya) — a synthesis of prophetic modeling, Shafi'i jurisprudence, and Sufi interior training that became the foundational framework for all subsequent Ba'alawi masters.

Legacy

Al-Faqih al-Muqaddam's legacy is coextensive with the entire Ba'alawi tradition — one of the most geographically extensive and historically significant Sufi lineages in the world. The hundreds of millions of Muslims in Southeast Asia, East Africa, and South Asia who are connected to the Ba'alawi tradition trace their spiritual lineage back, through a succession of masters, to this single man in Tarim. His descendants — the Banu Alawi, the sayyids of Hadhramaut — carried his path to Java and Sumatra in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, to the Swahili coast in the fifteenth and sixteenth, to India and Pakistan in multiple waves. The Ba'alawi zawiya model — the scholar-saint resident in a community, teaching, arbitrating, guiding — became the dominant form of Islamic authority across vast regions.

More precisely, he created the spiritual grammar of a civilization. The Hadhrami sayyids who spread Islam across the Indian Ocean did so with a particular combination of prophetic lineage, Sufi depth, and legal learning that al-Faqih al-Muqaddam had fixed as the essential character of the Banu Alawi identity. His grave in Zanbal remains among the most visited in the Islamic world, a point of pilgrimage for those who understand that the chains connecting them to the Prophet pass, for hundreds of millions, through this one broken sword.

Biography

Not every great master leaves a library. Some leave a presence — a quality of interior attention so concentrated and so transparently transmitted that those who encountered them were altered in ways that no text could produce. Umar al-Mihdar, born in Tarim around 665 AH / 1267 CE, was this kind of master: a man whose name means "the present one," whose defining spiritual characteristic was an unbroken hudur (presence) with the Divine, and whose legacy consisted less in works written than in a quality of being that was transmitted from heart to heart.

He was a grandson of al-Faqih al-Muqaddam through his father Abd al-Rahman ibn Ali Ba'alawi, born into the generation that was consolidating what the Foremost Jurist had founded. The Ba'alawi community of Tarim in the late thirteenth century was a world of inherited spiritual wealth — families whose grandparents and great-grandparents had studied with the greatest masters of the preceding era, in a valley that had become, against its geographic remoteness, one of the most concentrated centers of Islamic learning and Sufi formation in the world.

Umar al-Mihdar received his formation entirely within Tarim. Unlike many of the great masters of his tradition, he never left Hadhramaut for the journey to Mecca, Medina, or the greater Islamic world. His refusal to travel was itself understood as a spiritual statement: the real journey, in his teaching and in his person, was inward. While others accumulated knowledge by traversing distances, Umar al-Mihdar traveled into the depths of the self and found there what the journeyers had sought across the face of the earth.

His biography in the Ba'alawi sources describes him in terms of his ahwal — his spiritual states — rather than his scholarly accomplishments. He was known for his absorption in dhikr, for a quality of collected attention that could not be disturbed even by the noise and demands of everyday life, and for the effect he had on those who came into his presence: a stillness, a weight of spiritual reality, that settled over students and visitors and reorganized their inner lives without any deliberate effort on his part. The hudur was not a practice he undertook; it was what he was. His epithet al-Mihdar — the Present One — captured this: he was simply and continuously present, in a way that made ordinary consciousness seem like sleepwalking.

He composed poetry — qasidas in the Ba'alawi tradition of devotional verse — in which the language of divine love and prophetic attachment reached expressions of unusual beauty. His poems were recited in Hadhramaut and among Ba'alawi communities for centuries after his death. He died in 738 AH / 1338 CE in Tarim, and his shrine became one of the major pilgrimage destinations of the valley, visited by those who sought to draw on his continuing spiritual presence.

Key Teachings

Umar al-Mihdar taught that the highest station was not knowledge accumulated but presence maintained. The dhikr of the tongue was preparation; the dhikr of the heart was the practice; the dhikr of the spirit was the arrival — a state in which awareness of God was not interrupted even by the normal activities of life. He did not separate the contemplative life from the active: his presence was fully engaged with the community around him, with the teaching of students and the guidance of seekers, but this engagement flowed from and never disturbed the underlying current of hudur that was his constant condition.

He held that the Ba'alawi path was preeminently a path of love — not the romantic love of the poets who used divine love as a metaphor, but a specifically prophetic love: the attachment to the person and reality of the Prophet Muhammad that was, in the Ba'alawi understanding, the fastest and safest path to God. To love the Prophet with increasing intensity and precision was to be drawn toward the prophetic reality, and the prophetic reality was the nearest created thing to God. His poetry was largely an expression of this conviction — verses of longing and devotion addressed to the Prophet, composed in the register of an intimacy that was simultaneously personal and cosmic.

Famous Stories

The Inward Journey: A traveler who had come a great distance to sit with Umar al-Mihdar asked him: "Sayyid, I have crossed mountains and seas to find a master. You are said never to have left this valley. How can one who has not traveled have anything to teach a man of the road?" Umar al-Mihdar looked at him quietly for a long moment. Then he said: "Tell me what you have found in your travels that you did not already carry with you when you left." The traveler was silent. "That is what I have been doing," the Sayyid said, "while staying here." The story circulated in Ba'alawi tradition as an image of the contemplative path that sees the world of exterior journeying and the world of interior exploration as comparable undertakings — except that the interior journey has no fixed destination that can be reached once and left behind.

Major Works

  • Devotional Qasidas: A collection of Arabic poems in the tradition of prophetic love poetry, recited in Ba'alawi communities across Hadhramaut and the diaspora.

Legacy

Umar al-Mihdar occupies a specific structural position in the Ba'alawi chain: he is a key link between the founding generation of al-Faqih al-Muqaddam and the later masters who would systematize the Alawi method into its fully articulated form. The quality he transmitted — hudur, the state of unbroken spiritual presence — became a defining aspiration of the Ba'alawi path, a criterion against which subsequent masters were measured and toward which students oriented their practice. His shrine in Tarim draws visitors to this day, who come to sit in proximity to the place where, according to the tradition, his spiritual presence continues to work on those who come with sincerity. His name, al-Mihdar, remains an epithet of honor across the Ba'alawi world.
